HEAD: STATISTICS SOUTH AFRICA

Statistics South Africa

Stats SA is the central government body in the Republic of SouthAfrica that is authorised in terms of the Statistics Act, 1976 (ActNo. 66 of 1976) to compile and publish national statistics. It is therefore the task of the Stats SA to make statistics available on a wide variety of aspects regarding the South African economy and society to the public sector, organisations in the private sector and the public in general.

The head office of Stats SA is in Pretoria, and there are eleven branch offices that assist with the collection of data and the dissemination of information.

  1. The publishing system

    1. The publications

      There are four main groups of publications: statistical releases, reports, other publications and publications free of charge.

      The alphabetical list of topics is an index of all the subjects covered by the Stats SA statistical publications. The topical list of publications groups together all the available publications concerning a specific topic and indicates whether the information is available in the form of a statistical release or report. The page number refers to the detailed list of publications where more information and a short description of each publication can be obtained.

      In order to facilitate ordering of statistical publications, the series numbers of releases and reports are also listed alphabetically in a comprehensive list according to topic.

    2. How the Stats SA can assist you

      1. Telephonic enquiries

        The Users Enquiries Component of the Stats SA will gladly assist you with any enquiries.
        Enquiries can be made at telephone numbers (012)310-8390/8095/8351 and 8600.

      2. Personal visits

        You are also welcome to call on the Stats SA at rooms 222, 224, 226 in the Steyn's Arcade, 274 Schoeman Street, Pretoria.

      3. Library loans

        The library of Stats SA in Pretoria specialises in statistics as a field of study and carries a wide variety of publications that are also available to the public for reference purposes. Publications may also be obtained by means of the interlibrary loan scheme by calling (012) 310-8018/8490 during office hours. A selection of Stats SA publications are distributed to university libraries, college libraries and other libraries in various countries.

      4. Data bank

        Some of the published data of Stats SA are recorded in a time series data bank. Enquiries can be directed to the Users Enquiries Component.

      5. Unpublished data

        Due to their scope, not all the statistics pertaining to population censuses, vital statistics and subjects such as tourism can be published, but they are available to users.

      6. How to place orders

        • Statistical releases, as well as several other publications, are available free of charge on request. For your convenience an order form is supplied as the last page of this guide.
        • Magnetic tapes containing data, especially regarding the population census and the data bank of the Stats SA, are obtainable from the Stats SA at a minimal cost. Selected data can also be made available on floppies and stiffies if feasible. Please enquire telephonically at the Users Enquiries Component.
        • All other publications have to be ordered directly from the Government Printing Works, Private Bag X85, Pretoria, 0001 or phone (012) 323-9731. The prices (local and overseas) of the publications, together with the title of each publication, appear in this guide. Postage need not be added.
        • Overseas orders:
        • The publications are sent by surface mail unless you indicate that you prefer airmail. If the payment is in foreign currency an additional amount of R7,00 is charged to cover bank costs.
        • Should you request that the publications be sent by airmail, the implied cost must be calculated by the Government Printing Works.

7. Classification

  1. General remarks

    The classification of data is of fundamental importance to the activities of the Stats SA; it forms the basis of every statistical release and report published.

    Orderly classification provides a common framework for the collection, tabulation, presentation and analysis of statistical data in respect of establishments/individuals/ regions, etc. The uniform application of the principles and definitions of the various classification frameworks thus promotes the comparability of statistics available from different sources, as collected and issued by different bodies. All users of statistical data collected by the Stats SA are encouraged to make use of the recommended standardised classifications.

    The Stats SA applies classification, inter alia, in the following spheres of activity:

  2. Industrial classification

    The classification of establishments according to kind of economic activity is done on the basis of international directions and recommendations. These directions and recommendations of the Statistical Office of the United Nations are contained in the International Standard Industrial Classification (ISIC) manual. The directions are suitably adapted by each country according to local requirements. The instructions according to which the Stats SA performs its industrial classification are contained in the Standard Industrial Classification of All Economic Activities (SIC) manual. Classification is done on an establishment (branch) basis and according to the nature of the predominant activity.

    Essential information on the activities of an establishment is gathered by means of a classification questionnaire. The establishment is initially classified on the basis of such information. It may be necessary to revise the initial classification when a completed census questionnaire is obtained from the establishment at a later stage. A Classification Committee of Stats SA officials meets from time to time to revise and extend the instructions as may be necessary.

    Activities are grouped according to major divisions, divisions, major groups and subgroups. The following list, from which the detailed subgroups are excluded, is a summary of the classification relevant in this regard:

    List of major divisions

    1. Agriculture, hunting, forestry and fishing
    2. Mining and quarrying
    3. Manufacturing
    4. Electricity, gas and water supply
    5. Construction
    6. Wholesale and retail trade; repair of motor vehicles, motor cycles and personal and household goods; hotels and restaurants
    7. Transport, storage and communication
    8. Financial intermediation, insurance, real estate and business services
    9. Community, social and personal services
    10. Private households, exterritorial organisations, representatives of foreign governments and other activities not adequately defined.

  3. Classification of areas

    The Stats SA undertakes the maintenance and distribution of an area classification framework. The initial list was compiled in consultation with numerous bodies and is normally revised annually.

    The underlying unit of grouping is a magisterial district. One or more adjoining magisterial districts are combined to form a statistical region. In this regard homogeneity in respect of physical characteristics and economic activities has been exploited as far as is practically possible. Within each magisterial district a subdivision of cities and towns is made, i.e. areas falling under the jurisdiction of a specific local government.

  4. Classification of government transactions

    The transactions of the Government are grouped according to the so-called economic and functional classifications and for that purpose the Manual of Government Finance Statistics of the International Monetary Fund is applied as a manual for all levels of the Government.

    The economic classification differentiates the transactions on the grounds of the influence of the Government on the economy and the rest of the world such as remuneration of employees, interest, subsidies and tax revenue. The functional classification differentiates the transactions of the Government on the basis of the various types of services rendered to the community by the Government, e.g. educational, health and economic services.

    Due to the large number of entries involved, it is not possible to include such a listing.

  5. National accounts classification

    The calculations by the Stats SA are based on the directions and recommendations of the United Nations Statistical Office contained in the latest revised (1993) edition of A System of National Accounts (SNA). The SNA provides a comprehensive and detailed framework for the recording and presentation of transactions relating to production, consumption, capital formation, foreign trade, etc. Furthermore, the SNA provides a detailed framework for systematic and meaningful recording of all flows and stocks of the economic system.

  6. Imports and exports classification

    Imports and exports are classified by the Department of Finance's Office for Customs and Excise on the basis of the Customs Co-operation Council Nomenclature (CCCN) which was extended to the Harmonised System (HS). The Office for Customs and Excise allocates a SIC code based on the industry that manufactures the particular commodity.

  7. Causes of death classification

    With effect from 1947 South Africa adopted the International Classification of Diseases (ICD), as published in the Manual of the International Statistical Classification of Diseases, Injuries and Causes of Death, as a basis for classification of diseases as a cause of death. The ninth revision of this manual for the RSA's own national statistics on mortality is currently in use. The main purpose of the manual is to assist medical practitioners in describing the cause of death as accurately as possible on the death certificate (normally form BI-12). The manual should also prove to be useful to district registrars and medical students. Furthermore, the cause of death, particularly when combined with other demographic characteristics of the deceased, such as age, sex, marital status and occupation, is of great statistical and analytical importance.

    The major categories of the classification are:

    • Infectious and parasitic diseases
    • Neoplasms
    • Endocrine, nutritional and metabolic diseases and immunity disorders
    • Diseases of blood and blood-forming organs
    • Mental disorders
    • Diseases of the nervous system and sense organs
    • Diseases of the circulatory system
    • Diseases of the respiratory system
    • Diseases of the digestive system
    • Diseases of the genito-urinatory system
    • Complications of pregnancy, childbirth and the puerperium
    • Diseases of the skin and subcutaneous tissue
    • Diseases of the musculoskeletal system connective tissue
    • Congenital anomalies
    • Certain conditions originating in the perinatal period
    • Symptoms, signs and ill-defined conditions
    • Accidents, poisoning and violence (external causes)

  8. Classification of occupations

    A standard classification of occupations was compiled by the Stats SA in collaboration with the National Manpower Commission and the HSRC. The purpose of this classification was to set up a framework for the identification of occupations and for the meaningful combination of the same in personnel and statistical systems. This classification is also useful to all compilers and users of information based on occupations in that it will improve the comparability of information from different sources. An important consideration in the preparation of this classification was the retention of the maximum comparability with groupings previously used in the Stats SA population censuses and the former Department of Manpower's manpower surveys. The basis for the classification of occupations is the type of work done, but the educational qualifications required for a specific occupation are also considered in certain cases. The classification is based on The International Standard Classification of Occupations (ISCO) of 1968, according to the following major groups:

    Major groups

    1. Professional, semi-professional and technical occupations
    2. Managerial, executive and administrative occupations
    3. Clerical and sales occupations
    4. Transport, delivery and communications occupations
    5. Service occupations
    6. Farming and related occupations
    7. Artisan, apprentice and related occupations
    8. Mining and quarrying occupations, production foremen and supervisors, operators, production and related occupations
    9. Occupations unspecified and not elsewhere classified
    10. Workers not economically active

  9. Code list of offences

    The Stats SA, the Department of Justice, the South African Police Services and the Military Police compiled a standard code list of offences that is used for the processing of statistics of offences. This code list corresponds with the grouping of legislation and offences under the different main classes in terms of which prosecutions can be done.

    The present classification is based on the following main classes:

    • Government authority and good order
    • Communal life
    • Personal relations
    • Property
    • Economic affairs
    • Social affairs
