CHEROKEE COUNTY SCHOOL SYSTEM'S Y2K PREPAREDNESS

     Section 1.  The Year 2000 Problem:  The Year 2000 Problem, also known as Y2K or the millennium bug, resulted from a method used by computer programmers to save storage space that reflect the year in a program.  Many computers only have fields for the last two digits to represent the year, assuming the first two digits are (19), e.g. this year is "99".  When the century changes to 2000, the same computers will read "00" and assume the year to be 1900.  Further complicating the problem is the fact that the year 2000 is a leap year.  One can imagine the problems this could cause with computerized calculation such as graduation dates or interest calculation.  In addition to desk top computers, this problem could affect networks, software, the computer chips, thermostats, alarm systems, elevators and other devices.  Systems that are not repaired in advance of the new millennium could experience significant problems ranging from total meltdown to minor hiccup to the worst case scenario of appearing to have no problem while actually producing flawed data.

     Section 2.  Y2K Committee:  Individualized Departments within the Cherokee County School System have been working since August of 1997 identifying, updating and testing the System critical computer function as well as corresponding with third party vendors and suppliers in order to minimize the effects of the Y2K problem.  One of Dr. Frank Petruzielo's first actions after taking charge of the Cherokee County School System in February of 1999 was to establish an Ad Hoc Y2K Committee.  This Committee co-chaired by Candler Howell, Assistant Superintendent of Financial Management, and Jackie Hopkins, Assistant Superintendent of Accountability, Technology and Strategic Planning, and staffed by System personnel and consultants has marshalled, analyzed and reviewed the System's substantial prior efforts.  The Committee continues to work pro-actively in order to minimize the effects of the Y2K problem.

     Section 3.  In House Computer Hardware and Software Assessment:  This Committee has conducted a comprehensive system wide inventory and identified date-sensitive critical computer functions within the system, as well as other date system computer functions whose operation has not been deemed to be critical.

     Section 3.1  Critical Functions:  With respect to the aforesaid critical functions, the Committee required the upgrading of all hardware and software to computer components which are represented by their manufacturers to be "Y2K compliant".  Where possible, the Committee required in-house testing of all critical computer functions by "forcing" all mission critical date sensitive programs forward in time to at least March, 2000.  Thus far, certain time sensitive computer functions may not be tested in- house and the system has, therefore, relied upon representation of the system manufacturers and testing data provided.

     Section 3.2  Noncritical Functions:  To a lesser extent, the Committee has required all non-critical date sensitive computer functions to be updated where practicable and cost-effective.  Therefore, minor interruption in non-critical system computer functions could be experienced.  It is anticipated that these interruptions in service will in no way materially impact the System's primary function, the education of the youth of Cherokee County.

     Section 4.  Third Party Vendors/Suppliers:  The Committee has also identified and inventoried all System vendors and has corresponded with each requesting a statement of Y2K compliance.  Of the vendors contacted, the Committee has identified the top ten critical vendors and entered dialogues with each regarding the provision of continued service to the Cherokee County School System and has where required created contingency plans to identify potential interruptions in service prior to the conclusion of Christmas holidays and the students subsequent return on January 3, 2000.

     At present, based upon the ongoing dialogue with System vendors and suppliers, it is anticipated that the Cherokee County School System will not experience any critical interference with third party services which would require the closing of schools.  However, because the compliance of the critical third party providers is totally outside the control of the Committee and the Cherokee County School System, some interruption in school may be possible.  Parents are encouraged to monitor local media for potential adjustments to the school calendar.

     Section 5. Contingency Planning:  In the unlikely event of interruption in service of a critical system provider, the Committee has developed contingency plans, including but not limited to stocking of additional food and water in all school buildings.  All such contingency plans put the safety of the students first and all other concerns second.
