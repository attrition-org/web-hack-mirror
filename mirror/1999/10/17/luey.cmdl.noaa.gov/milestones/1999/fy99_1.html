CMDL FY99 1st Quarter Milestones

Predict and Assess Decadal-to-Centennial Climate Change

 

Q1: Publish results of inverse modeling of global CO2 observations, showing that ecosystems in North America constitute a large net annual sink of atmospheric CO2. In particular, during 1988-1992, the southern half of the North American continent appears to have been absorbing an amount of CO2 comparable to that produced by fossil fuel burning. (CMDL, P. Tans)

Evidence has been accumulating over the last decade that a significant fraction (perhaps about one third) of the CO2 emitted by the burning of fossil fuels is being absorbed by the land masses at temperate latitudes in the northern hemisphere.  This is commonly called the northern hemisphere terrestrial CO2 "sink". A short synopsis, with references, of the various independent lines of evidence pointing in that direction is given by Tans and White (1998).

During 1997 and 1998 a new step was taken in deciphering the global balance sheet of atmospheric CO2.  For the first time the Northern Hemisphere terrestrial sink was specified a bit further: we found that the North American continent played the leading role (Fan et al., 1998).  Since 1995 we have been participating in the Carbon Modeling Consortium, centered at Princeton University, NJ.   The consortium develops models of the global atmospheric and oceanic transport of chemical compounds.  These models are applied to observations of CO2 in the atmosphere and oceans in order to uncover the spatial and temporal patterns of sources and sinks that give rise to the observed concentration patterns.  Because atmospheric transport is generally much faster in the east-west than in the north-south direction the spatial pattern that results from sources and sinks is mostly north-south, and is "smeared out" in the east-west direction.  This implies that it is much harder to uncover east-west source patterns than it is to deduce the sources and sinks as a function of latitude.

In the paper by Fan et al. the annual average CO2 concentrations for the period 1988-1992 are interpreted by two atmospheric models (originally developed at NOAA/GFDL).  The observations are from the first release of the Globalview-CO2 data set maintained at NOAA/CMDL (1996).  Globalview puts together as many well-calibrated data as possible from international collaborating laboratories.  Annual average CO2 sources and sinks are deduced separately for three areas: North America, Northern Eurasia, and the rest of the land area of the world.  Exchange of CO2 with the global oceans is prescribed according to two different scenarios: one based on global observations of the partial pressure of CO2 in surface ocean waters, leading to the transfer of gas across the surface (Takahashi et al., 1997), and one based on a biogeochemical ocean model developed at Princeton.   This leads to four combinations (two models with two prescribed oceanic sink patterns) to interpret the atmospheric data in terms of land sources and sinks. A terrestrial carbon sink of about 1.6 Pg (1.6 billion metric tonnes) of carbon per year in North America stands out in all of these four cases (see Table).

If the large terrestrial CO2 sink in North America is borne out by further measurements, it has tremendous potential implications for carbon management.  In the paper by Fan et al. we mention as possible explanations the ongoing regrowth of previously cut forests along the east coast of the U.S., or the fertilization of the biosphere by higher atmospheric CO2 concentration and/or the wide application of nitrate.  It is also possible that there is a significant buildup of organic matter in agricultural soils resulting from a combination of management practices including fertilizer application.

Q1: Report on findings of the 1997 POLARIS field campaign investigating Arctic ozone losses. (CMDL, J. Elkins and S. Oltmans; AL, D. Fahey; OGP, J. Levy)

The Photochemistry of Ozone Loss in Arctic Region in Summer (POLARIS) airborne campaign was undertaken in 1997 to investigate the seasonal behavior of polar stratospheric ozone (O3) as it changes from very high concentrations in spring down to very low concentrations in autumn (top panel, red line indicates mean O3 column densities for the period of 1978-94). Ozone loss results from catalytic loss cycles involving reactions of total chemically active nitrogen (NOy), odd hydrogen (HOx), and total halogens (Cly, from CFCs, etc. and Bry, e.g. from halons, etc.). The enhanced polar summertime loss is attributed to the increased role of very reactive nitrogen (nitric oxide (NO) + nitrogen dioxide (NO2)) in O3 loss cycles during periods of prolonged solar illumination such as occur at high latitudes during summer. The accuracy with which current photochemical models can describe this large, natural O3 loss serves as an indication of how well the role of increased stratospheric reactive nitrogen from aircraft emissions can be qualified. One of the major goals of POLARIS was to evaluate the level of agreement between model predictions and observations for the 1998 and upcoming 2002 UNEP/WMO Scientific Assessments of Stratospheric Ozone Depletion, an objective of the NOAA Strategic Plan and a major performance measure quantified.

The POLARIS campaign was co-sponsored by NASA’s Office of Mission to Planet Earth and Office of Aeronautics, and NOAA. The mission was based primarily out of Fairbanks, Alaska during three deployment periods (April-May, June-July, and September), with additional flights conducted from Barber’s Point, Hawaii, and Moffett Field, California. Two platforms, including the NASA ER-2 high altitude aircraft (bottom panel) and the Observations of the Middle Stratosphere (OMS) balloon gondola, were employed to operate a total of 24 sampling instruments. A NOAA scientist was the mission scientist. Other NOAA scientists operated three instruments on the NASA ER-2 aircraft (reactive nitrogen, dual-beam UV-absorption O3 photometer, and the Airborne Chromatograph for Atmospheric Trace Species-IV (ACATS-IV) instruments), two instruments on the OMS balloon gondola (Lightweight Airborne Chromatograph Experiment, LACE and water vapor instruments), and one from the ground (ozonesondes). Two theory teams from NOAA also were involved in the mission. The major customers of this research are modelers that examine anthropogenic perturbations to the stratospheric ozone layer and policy makers involved in regulatory decisions involving future aircraft emissions.

Using the POLARIS data set, photochemical models were able to predict the observed NO2 to NO ratio and the observed ratio of very reactive nitrogen to the total chemically active nitrogen in the atmosphere. This agreement indicates that key models accurately represent important aspects of the reactive nitrogen photochemistry in the lower stratosphere. Calculations constrained by in situ measurements of nitrogen, hydrogen, and chlorine species show that very reactive nitrogen dominates the destruction of ozone in the summer polar stratosphere, with significant contributions from the odd hydrogen cycle. This result is significant because an earlier campaign (in 1995-1996) showed that the chemistry for odd hydrogen was the dominant destruction cycle in the midlatitude stratosphere. POLARIS measurements were completed in 1997, a scientific meeting was held in June 1998, a CD-ROM with all of the data from observations and some model results was distributed in July 1998, a special AGU session was held in December 1998, and papers are currently being submitted to peer-reviewed publications. Major ozone depletion was observed in March-April 1997 (top panel, black dots). These events have become more frequent over the past few years. An upcoming campaign, SAGE-3 Ozone Loss Validation Experiment (SOLVE) in 1999-2000, will offer another opportunity to examine polar stratospheric ozone loss but this time during late winter and early spring.
