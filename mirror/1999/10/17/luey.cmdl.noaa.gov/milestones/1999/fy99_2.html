CMDL FY99 2nd Quarter Milestones

Predict and Assess Decadal-to-Centennial Change

Q2: Report an improved budget of atmospheric methane, based on trends, lab studies, and modeling. (CMDL, E. Dlugokencky and P. Tans)

Purpose of the Research

The amount of methane (CH4) in Earth's atmosphere has more than doubled since preindustrial times. This increase is responsible for about 20% of the estimated change in direct radiative forcing due to anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions. In addition, methane reacts with ozone-destroying free chlorine in the stratosphere, converting it to hydrochloric acid which does not react with ozone thus aiding in the rehabilitation of the ozone layer. Methane continues to increase in the 1990s, but the rate of increase has slowed. A better understanding of the global budgets of gases such as methane is needed in order to provide accurate information for policy makers. The analysis was performed to gain a better understanding of how total emissions to the atmosphere have changed in recent decades, and to try to understand the reasons for the decreasing growth rate in atmospheric CH4.

Contribution to the NOAA Strategic Plan (SP) Objective and Performance Measure (PM)

The SP element is to PREDICT AND ASSESS DECADAL TO CENTENNIAL CHANGE, with objectives to CHARACTERIZE THE FORCING AGENTS OF CLIMATE CHANGE and to GUIDE THE REHABILITATION OF THE OZONE LAYER. This effort contributes to these objectives by characterizing the concentrations and growth rates of methane which is both a major greenhouse gas and a controlling factor in ozone depletion. The results of this research will be considered in the next IPCC assessment of the science of global climate change, and the WMO/UNEP assessment of ozone depletion, which are PM’s for these objectives.

Research Efforts

Methane measurements were made from air samples collected as part of a globally distributed network of air sampling sites operated by the CMDL. Measurements from about 60 sampling sites were used to calculate global averages as a function of time. Globally averaged methane data were used in a model (along with annual increases calculated from the same measurements and the methane lifetime) to calculate total annual emissions to the atmosphere for the period 1984 to 1996. The results suggest that if the globally averaged concentration of hydroxyl (OH), which chemically removes methane from the atmosphere, has remained constant (for which there is independent evidence), then total emissions of CH4 to the atmosphere have been nearly constant at -540 Million Metric Tons CH4 per year (red filled circles in bottom panel of the figure). Further, the results show that the decreasing growth rate of methane reflects an approach to steady-state on a time scale comparable to methane's lifetime of about 9 years (dashed curve in top panel). Chemical loss of methane to OH is proportional to the methane concentration and continues to grow as methane increases (cyan triangles in lower panel). At steady-state, emissions would exactly balance losses.

Customers, Dissemination and Use of Results

The measurements are used in chemical and radiative transfer models for studies of anthropogenic climate change, especially for projecting future greenhouse warming based on expected atmospheric concentrations of CH4 and other gases. They are also used in chemical models of stratospheric ozone which predict the recovery of the ozone layer. These model results are used in assessments, for example, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and the WMO/UNEP Scientific Assessments of Stratospheric Ozone. The main result of this work will be useful for policy makers in greenhouse gas emission stabilization considerations and in assessing the effectiveness of the Montreal Protocol regulating substances that deplete the ozone layer.

Significance of the Work

The work attempts to explain the decreasing growth rate of atmospheric methane first reported in Nature (Vol.358, pp. 313-316) by CMDL in 1992. Previously the decreasing growth rate was thought to be due to either decreased CH4 emissions or increased tropospheric OH; in reality, both have remained approximately constant. The result could have policy implications. Extrapolating constant global emissions and OH into the future suggests that globally averaged methane will increase from 1730 ppb in 1996 to a steady-state maximum of about 1800 ppb over the next few decades, with little change in the contribution of methane to the greenhouse effect. This assumes that the original key assumptions were correct, and that neither emissions nor the globally averaged OH concentration change in the future. Such a scenario was not considered in the last (1995) IPCC assessment and would reduce model-estimated future warming. A future constant methane scenario was considered in the 1998 WMO/UNEP Ozone Assessment and indicated that recovery of the ozone layer would be delayed.

Project Status and Outcome

The project was completed and published in Nature, A Continuing Decline in the Growth Rate of the Atmospheric Methane Burden, 4 June 1998, pp. 447-450.

Future Activities

The high-precision CH4 measurements will continue, and they will continue to be analyzed by CMDL scientists and made available to other researchers. Other parameters revealed by the measurements such as spatial gradients, seasonal cycles, and comparisons with other trace gases will be investigated in detail. The most important element in this effort will remain maintenance of the network for the collection of high-quality air samples and the subsequent measurement of the samples for CH4 and other species.

Q2: Obtain ground-based and airborne measurements of the climate-forcing properties of atmospheric aerosols over the Indian Ocean during the international Indian Ocean Experiment (INDOEX). These measurements will help improve the global models used to calculate the climatic effects of aerosols in a region where anthropogenic emissions are predicted to increase substantially in the future. (CMDL, J. Ogren)

Purpose of the Research

The purpose of this effort is to characterize the radiative properties of atmospheric aerosols in a region where anthropogenic emissions are predicted to increase substantially in the future. Prior to INDOEX, little was known about these properties, but exploratory missions had documented the presence of high levels of pollution over the Indian Ocean, far from land. The goal is to improve global models that are used to calculate the climatic effects of aerosols, by providing reliable determinations of the specific aerosol radiative properties that are used in the models. More information on INDOEX is available at http://www-indoex.ucsd.edu.

Contribution to the NOAA Strategic Plan (SP) Objective and Performance Measure (PM)

The SP element is to PREDICT AND ASSESS DECADAL TO CENTENNIAL CHANGE, with the objective to CHARACTERIZE THE FORCING AGENTS OF CLIMATE CHANGE. This effort contributes to the objective by characterizing the radiative properties of a major forcing agent of climate change, namely anthropogenic aerosols. The results of this research will be considered in the next IPCC assessment of the science of global climate change, which is the PM for this objective.

Research Efforts

Duplicate systems for measuring aerosol light scattering and absorption, as well as the dependence of aerosol light scattering on relative humidity, were deployed on the NCAR C-130 research aircraft and at the Kaashidoo Climate Observatory (KCO) in the Maldive Islands for INDOEX (see photos below). The systems were operated for all 18 of the C-130 research flights and for a 7-week intensive study period at KCO from February 8 to March 29, 1999.

The NCAR C-130 research aircraft used during INDOEX.

The Kaashidoo Climate Observatory in the Maldive Islands.

Customers, Dissemination and Use of Results

The primary customers are the participants of INDOEX, in particular the global modeling community, which needs reliable values of aerosol radiative properties in order to calculate the radiative effects of anthropogenic aerosols over the Indian Ocean. The end results of the models are used in assessments such as the IPCC and provide useful information for policy makers regarding climate predictions. Preliminary results were communicated at workshops during the field campaign. Final results will be provided to the INDOEX data base, and will be reported in the refereed literature.

Significance of the Work

These are the first airborne measurements of aerosol light scattering and absorption coefficients over the Indian Ocean. They are also the first measurements of aerosol hygroscopic growth over the Indian Ocean. The measurements are significant because they will allow direct measurements of aerosol radiative properties, rather than educated guesses, to be used in the models that evaluate aerosol radiative forcing of climate.

Project Status and Outcome

The last research flight was on March 25, and the last measurements at KCO were on March 29, 1999. The field data have just been received at CMDL. Preliminary evaluation of the data indicates that the quality and quantity is sufficient to meet the goal of providing modelers with reliable values of aerosol radiative properties over the Indian Ocean.

Future Activities

The next step is to screen the data, apply calibration corrections, and provide a clean, final data set to the INDOEX data center. Our data analysis and publication efforts will focus on (a) summary statistics of aerosol radiative properties in the INDOEX region, and (b) comparison of measured and modeled values of aerosol radiative properties during INDOEX. Because of the unique location of KCO, with very clean conditions part of the year and very polluted conditions during the northeast monsoon, a number of research groups in the U.S. and abroad are interested in continued atmospheric sampling there. We plan to rebuild our sampling system to make it more suitable for extended operation with minimal on-site attention, and to operate it at KCO for a full year if funding can be arranged.
