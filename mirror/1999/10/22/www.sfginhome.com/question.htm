Q: Can a well-drafted Will avoid Probate?

A: No! All Wills must be probated to insure their validity.

Q: How big an estate should I have before I consider a living trust?

A: It is really not a matter of how much you own...Whether you are young or old, married single, have a modest or substantial estate, just about everyone can benefit from a Living Trust...As a matter of fact, most small estates lose a greater percentage than larger estate through the probate process.

Q: If I get a Living Trust, have I finished my estate planning?

A: Not necessarily...Depending on the size of your estate and your situation, you may need additional planning...There are several options for you to consider, and you may discuss these with one of our representatives with the assurance of complete confidentiality.

Q: Does my Living Trust protect my assets from creditors?

A: No...A Living Trust does not shield your assets from creditors.

Q: If my home is in a Living Trust, can I still deduct mortgage interest?

A: The Living Trust has no impact on income taxes...You control your assets and continue to file your income and expenses on your tax return, including mortgage interest.

Q: Will having a Living Trust affect my Social Security benefits?

A: Not at all, because Social Security benefits are considered income, and the Living Trust has no effects on income.

Q: Can I sell my assets once they are in my Living Trust?

A: Yes. You have exactly the same rights as you did prior to the creation of the trust...You can buy, sell, or transfer your assets at any time...You can also borrow against your as before.

Q: Why didn’t my lawyer tell me about a Living Trust?

A: Many attorneys are not aware of the alternatives to probate offered by the Living Trust...Some simply fail to mention this to their clients as it means that the attorney will lose probate fee. Remember that attorneys can receive from 6 to 10 percent of the gross estate for probating the Will.

Q: Do I need to notify my attorney that I replaced my Will?

A: There is no need to notify your attorney...When you execute your trust, you revoke all former Wills.

Q: Can probate be avoided by the use of joint tenancy?

A: Yes and no...Joint tenancy with your spouse can delay probate, but it cannot avoid altogether. Upon the second death, the estate will be probated unless you also have joint tenancy with your heirs...This is risky, however, in that you expose your estate to any 1iabity, lawsuits, or creditors of any of the joint tenants.

Q: Can Congress outlaw the Living Trust?

A: Congress has little chance of ever taking away the Living Trust...The Constitution of United States of America grants certain states' rights...One of these states' rights is the right to create a legal entity such as a corporation or a trust...Congress will probably not be able to invade this area of states' rights.

Q: Can I have a Living Trust if I am unmarried?

A: Of course! If you want to avoid probate, you must have a Living Trust whether you are married or not.

Q: Is the Living Trust valid in all fifty states?

A: Yes, and since the Living Trust is derived from English law, it is recognized in all commonwealth nations and valid in most nations that have have a civil code.

Q: What is an A/B Trust?

A: It is a unique trust between married couples...The trust will divide into two trusts up the first to die. Because of this feature, the estate tax deduction is increased from $650,000 to $1,300,000. It can also preserve half of the assets should there be a catastrophic illness or nursing home care. It is also very useful when there is a second marriage involved and there are two sets of children.

Q: How are new assets handled after my Living Trust has been created?

A: On any new purchase, simply instruct your real estate agent, stockbroker, or ban officer to record title to the asset in the name of your trust.

Q: Do I need to change ownership of my stocks and bonds?

A: Absolutely! All of your assets should be recorded in the name of your trust in order avoid probate. On any new purchase, simply instruct your real estate agent, stockbroker or bank officer to record title to the asset in the name of your trust, or give us a call and will come out and help you!
