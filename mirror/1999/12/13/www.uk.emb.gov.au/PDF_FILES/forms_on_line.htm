Devolution is a major plank of the UK Government’s constitutional agenda, but it is often misunderstood. This paper describes what devolution will mean for Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, and for the United Kingdom as a whole.

Britain's Department of Health and National Health Service (36K)
The National Health Service (NHS) has been in existence for over 50 years. It is administered by a range of health authorities and health boards throughout Britain. The Department of Health, headed by the Secretary of State for Health, Frank Dobson, is responsible for general health matters and the provision of NHS services. The Scottish Office Department of Health, the Welsh Office, and the Department of Health and Social Services in Northern Ireland have similar responsibilities
What the British are good at (issue 16) (57K)
Britains Office of Fair Trading (OFT) is a non-ministerial department of Government, set up in 1973 and headed by a Director-General. It plays a key role in protecting the rights of consumers, and in enforcing Britains competition policy.

Britain's Ministry of Defence and Armed Forces (140K)
The Ministry of Defence (MOD) is not only a Department of State, but also incorporates the highest military headquarters of the Armed Forces. A unified Central Staff is made up of both military officers and civilians. The Secretary of State for Defence is George Robertson.
Ministry of Defence web site
Forestry in Britain (45K)
The Forestry Commission for Great Britain (FC) and the Department of Agriculture for Northern Ireland (DANI) Forest Service are tasked with the sustainable management of Britains existing woods and forests and with a steady expansion of tree cover, to increase the many diverse benefits that forests provide.
Forestry Commission Website
Department of Health and the National Service (37K)
The National Health Service (NHS), created on 5 July 1948, is fifty years old this year. It is administered by a range of health authorities and health boards throughout Britain. The Department of Health, headed by the Secretary of State for Health, Frank Dobson MP, is responsible for general health matters and the provision of NHS services. The Scottish Office Department of Health, The Welsh Office, and the Department of Health and Social Services in Northern Ireland have similar responsibilities.
50 years of the NHS
Department of Health
Department for Culture, Media and Sport (38K)
In July 1997 the former Department for National Heritage was renamed the Department for Culture, Media and Sport (DCMS). It determines Government policy and administers expenditure on museums and galleries, the arts, libraries, sport and recreation, tourism, historic buildings, broadcasting, media, the National Lottery and the Millennium.
DCMS web site
The Welsh Office (33K)
The overall aim of the Welsh Office is fourfold: to shape and implement policies for Wales in order to extend democratic accountability there; to increase economic prosperity for people in all parts of Wales; to improve the quality of life for people throughout Wales; and to enable Wales to realise its potential within the United Kingdom, Europe, and in the wider world.
Welsh Office web site
Public Administration in Scotland (143K)
The Secretary of State for Scotland is responsible to Parliament for the operations of The Scottish Office, of the other Scottish Departments of State and of the Executive Agencies. He is also responsible for Scottish legislation. He must ensure that the United Kingdom Cabinet and other Ministers are fully aware of Scottish needs and circumstances.
Scottish Office web site
Department for International Development - DFID (121K)
When it was set up in May 1997, the DFID was given a wider range of responsibilities than its predecessor, the Overseas Development Administration (ODA). As well as administering the British Governments overseas development programme, the DFID now has a stronger voice than before on trade, debt and environmental issues.
DFID web site
Department of Social Security (DSS) (120K)
Britain has a comprehensive, regulated, State-administered cash benefit social security system which covers the entire population. The current scheme began in 1948. Social security cover is provided through a social insurance (National Insurance) scheme and a range of non-contributory benefits which include extensive 'safety net' provision.
DSS web site
Department of Trade and Industry (DTI)
The DTI aims to help British business compete successfully at home, in Europe and in the wider world.
DTI web site
Promoting Food safety in Britain (120K)
The planned new Food Standards Agency (FSA) will make the safety of food in Britain its top priority. The aims of the Agency, which it is hoped will start work in the second half of 1999, are detailed in a White Paper, 'The Food Standards Agency: A Force for Change', published by the British Government in January 1998.
www.maff.gov.uk
Contemporary Britain (216K)
In the fields of economics, invention, society and culture, contemporary Britain is a dynamic player on a world stage. This special in the 'Spotlight Britain' series highlights several key areas of excellence.
Britain's Department for Education and Employement (286K)
The creation of the Department for Education and Employment (DfEE), in July 1995, brought together the functions of the former Department for Education and the training and labour market functions of the Employment Department Group. This has allowed the integration of policies ranging from pre-school education to the workplace. Separate Departments are responsible for education in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, where there are slight differences in the funding and management of schools and in curricula. This paper details the DfEEs four main objectives and gives some examples of the ways in which they are being achieved.
DfEE web site
