MAP Joins the Fight Against Drug-Resistant TB
    LIMA, Peru - "They were left to die," says one Peruvian health worker of the Morales family. The MAP came with a powerful new combination of drugs, turning what had been a picture of hopeless despair into a family celebration.
    MAP has emerged as a formidable player in the war against a new, rapidly spreading form of tuberculosis (TB) that is resistant to the old "standby" antibiotics. The disease, called MDRTB (multi-drug-resistant tuberculosis), leaves victims gasping for breath with no remedy from conventional treatment.
    There are victims like the Morales family. Living in a shantytown in rural Peru, one of four Morales sisters slowly died after she failed to respond to usually reliable tuberculosis treatment. The disease then struck five other family members.
    "These were young adults," says health worker Jennifer Furin, who has seen the devastation caused by MDRTB in Peru and elsewhere. "But they could no longer work or eat. They were given up for dead."     Death indeed would have won, except for the timely arrival of high potency antibiotics like Ofloxacin supplied by MAP. The treatment program calls for seven such medicines daily for two years. The costs are large normally out of the question for a poor Peruvian family. But the love of Christ expressed through the giving of MAP donors supplied the drugs for the treatment of the Morales family and others in their village.

In Memoriam:
Dr. Gustav Hemwall
1908 - 1998

    Dr. Gustav Hemwall, a foundaing board member of MAP International, died of a stroke in St. Mary's Hospital in Madison, Wisconsin. Dr. Hemwall made more than 25 trips to Honduras, most recently in the spring of 1997 when he was 89 years old.
Ray Knighton, founder of MAP and close friend of the Hemwalls, recalls a quote from the great theologian, William Barklay: "To Jesus, the most important thing in the world was not the correct performance of a ritual, but the spontaneous answer to the cry of human need." "My friend Gus would have said 'Amen' to that,: adds Dr. Knighton.
     Dr. Hemwall is survived by his wife, four daughters, 11 grandchildren, and two great-grandchildren.
