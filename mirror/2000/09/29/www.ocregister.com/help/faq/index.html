FINDING A STORY

How do I find a story?
There are several ways to find stories on our site. The most general way is to look for them by category. For instance, if you're looking for a story about an elected official, check out Politics and Government. Or if it's about a high-school athletic team, check out High School Sports. If it's about a shooting, try Crime and Courts. To see a complete list of our categories, visit our site map.

If you'd like to conduct a more specific search of stories and features currently on the site, visit our search page.

If the story for which you're searching appeared in the paper more than seven days ago, you'll want to search The Orange County Register's Online Archives. There you can find stories dating back to October 1986.

I read a story in the Register recently. Why isn't it online?
Chances are it was a wire story, especially if the topic was focused outside Orange County. Copyright laws prevent ocregister.com from publishing most stories that were not written by a Register reporter. Try searching the Register's Online Archives. If you can't find the story there, it was from a wire service, and we cannot publish it.

How are stories organized?
Stories are organized by traditional newspaper categories such as Sports, Entertainment, Science and Business. However, we also have categories for narrower topics such as education, high-school sports, religion, food, etc. (To see a complete list of our categories, visit our site map.)

If you're looking for stories on schools, try education. Or on the Ducks, check hockey. Don't worry if a story looks like it falls into more than one category. Just try whichever seems logical, and the story will be there. We understand that news doesn't always fit under one tidy topic, and we've designed our site to accommodate that. For instance, a story on the financial impact of a new sports team will be under both business and sports.

I'm looking for stories that ran on a particular date. How can I find them?
If the story ran in the past week, you can browse stories from the last seven days on our site search page. For older stories, you can search the Online Archives of the Register going back to October, 1996.

How far back do your online archives go?
Through October, 1996.

I still can't find the story. How can I get help?
For help in searching the complete archives of The Orange County Register, contact the Register's InfoStore. Their phone number is (714) 565-6077, or e-mail them at infostore@link.freedom.com

Why can't I see the video?

First, you need to downloand and install the free Quicktime Video plugin from Adobe. You can get it here. If you're shaky on how to install plugins, check out our general installation tips for plugins.

Video files tend to be a little larger than the average home user is comfortable with, since large file sizes mean longer download times with a modem. But if the window that pops up contains this icon:

then the video is downloading, and will start playing when it's loaded. An average video will take 1-5 minutes to load over a 28.8 modem.

Why can't I hear the audio?
The audio content on ocregister.com is predominantly RealAudio, which is a widely supported audio format for the Web. However, like most gadgets on the Web, you have to download a plugin for it. You can download the free version (called RealPlayer) from RealNetworks here. (You can also play RealVideo with the same plugin.) If you're not sure how to install a plugin, see the next question, "How do I install a plugin?".

If you have the plugin, and the player window pops up when you click on the audio links, but you can't hear the sound, check your speaker volume. Otherwise, check out the helpful RealNetworks FAQs here.

Why can't I see the 360° virtual views?
First, the most likely reason you're having trouble is that you haven't installed the plugin. You can download it for free here. If you're not sure how to install a plugin, see our FAQ "How do I install a plugin?" Second, make sure that it's not just taking a long time to load. At the bottom of your browser window, if it says something like "Loading 32% of 234K", then everything's fine. Just wait for it to reach 100%, and the image will appear. For more info on this and other Virtual-View related topics, check out the Virtual View FAQ here.

Why can't I see Mike Shelton's animated cartoon?
The animated cartoon requires the Macromedia Director Shockwave plugin for your browser. If you're using Windows 98 or Netscape 4.5, you already have it. Everyone else gets to click here to get the full Shockwave plugin package. This full package will enable you to play Director and Flash Shockwave files, which will come in handy all over the Web. The plugin is 303K, and will take a couple minutes to download. If you're not sure how to install a plugin, see our FAQ "How do I install a plugin?"

If you're using Internet Explorer 3 and you're having problems, click here for support.

What's a browser plugin?
A plugin is a piece of software that, once installed, works behind the scenes with your browser to enable you to enjoy a new (new to you, at least) file format, such as audio or video. Think of it this way: Remember 3-D movies? When you bought your tickets they'd give you a pair of funny little glasses that you'd have to put on in order to see the 3-D. Without those glasses, your brain couldn't process what was going on onscreen. Once you had the glasses, though, kerpow -- everything looked great. A plugin is just like those glasses. Without the right plugin, a browser may not be able to process what it's looking at, which makes your browser sad and unhappy.

The most common plugins are built in to browsers these days. But a few technologies are new enough that the major browser manufacturers (namely Microsoft Explorer and Netscape) haven't had a chance to incorporate them into their product yet. And even if they have, it doesn't do you any good unless you go download the latest version of their browser, which may take a lot more time than you're willing to invest. That's why plugins come in handy. Whenever ocregister.com utilizes a technology that may not be an industry standard yet, there will be a link saying something to the effect of "Hey, click here to get the plugin you need to view this." You click on the link, download and install the plugin (Don't know how? Check out our general installation tips for plugins.), and you'll be able to enjoy the new file format.

 

How do I install a plugin?
As a general rule, installing plugins is a three-step process:

1. Download the plugin file.
Located next to whatever it is your trying to view or hear on ocregister.com, there will be a link to the appropriate downloadable plugin. The link will say something like "Click here to get the plugin!" So click there, download the plugin (be sure to remember where you save the file when you download so it can be located in the next step) and move to step 2.

2. Quit out of your browser and double-click on the file you just downloaded from the plugin site.
Go to the File menu in your browser window and select "Exit" or "Quit.". Find the file you downloaded, and double-click it. This will run a simple installation program. Just follow the instructions you're given during the installation procedure to properly install the plugin.

3. Re-launch your browser.
Wah-lah -- you're all done. Just re-click on whatever it was you were trying to view or hear in the first place.
