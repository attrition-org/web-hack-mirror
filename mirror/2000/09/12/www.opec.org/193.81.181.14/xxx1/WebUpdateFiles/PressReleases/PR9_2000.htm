No. 9/2000              For immediate release

Vienna, Austria        10 September 2000

 

 

 

 

 

Opening Address

to the

111th Meeting of the OPEC Conference

by

 

His Excellency Ali Rodriguez Araque

President of the OPEC Conference

and

Minister of Energy & Mines of Venezuela

 

 

 

 

Excellencies, ladies and gentlemen,

            It is, once again, a great honour for me to welcome you to the 111th Meeting of the OPEC Conference, held at our Secretariat in Vienna.

            Let me begin by extending a warm welcome to our new colleague from Indonesia, HE Dr. Purnomo Yusgiantoro, who recently assumed office as Minister of Energy and Mineral Resources. We look forward to benefitting from HE Dr. Purnomo’s contributions to our deliberations and would ask him to convey to his predecessor, HE Lt.-Gen. Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono, our warmest regards.

            It is also my pleasure to again welcome to the Secretariat Delegations from other important non-OPEC oil producers, namely Mexico, Oman and Russia, and to extend congratulations to HE Alexander Gavrin, Russia’s newly appointed Minister of Energy.

            I am sure that all of you are aware that we are today celebrating one of the most important anniversaries of our Organization. It is exactly 40 years ago — on 10 September 1960 — that OPEC began its first Meeting in Baghdad, Iraq. The principal purpose of that Meeting embodied the very concept of cooperation with which we are so familiar today. At that time, cooperation meant OPEC’s five Founder Members — Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia and Venezuela — creating a united front, in order to defend their legitimate interests in a petroleum industry dominated by the multinational oil companies, more commonly known as “The Seven Sisters”.  That historic Meeting finished on 14 September, with the formal establishment of OPEC.

            As the years passed and its Membership grew, OPEC achieved an astonishing degree of success for a group of developing countries. The Organization provided these countries with the support and the confidence to assert their legitimate rights in the petroleum industry, particularly in terms of the exploitation of their indigenous resources, as well as having an influential say in the pricing of their oil on world markets. OPEC now has 11 Member Countries and is respected, far and wide, as an established part of the international petroleum community. This is, indeed, a grand achievement and one of which we should all be proud.

            At the same time, however, this provides no grounds for complacency. In just over a fortnight’s time, our Heads of State and Government will meet for the Second OPEC Summit in Caracas, Venezuela. The First Summit was held 25 years ago, in Algiers, Algeria. The purpose of the Second Summit is to take a fresh look at OPEC’s aims and objectives, its institutions, its procedures and its achievements, to see whether they match up to the evolving demands and requirements of the 21st century. Where we find shortcomings, we shall seek to rectify them, so that a stronger and more focused Organization emerges for the years ahead. Naturally, we shall spend some time during this Conference reviewing the final preparations for the Summit.

            Our attention at this Conference will also be placed on more immediate matters, particularly oil prices. The erratic price behaviour of the past three years has been the cause of much concern for producers, consumers and the industry. First, there was the crisis of 1998, when prices for OPEC’s spot Reference Basket fell heavily — and even briefly dipped below the psychological $10 per barrel mark — after averaging almost $18 per barrel in the ten-year period immediately before this; that period was marked by a relatively high degree of stability in the oil market. And secondly, after OPEC, with commendable support from four non-OPEC producers, introduced measures which boosted prices and took them to fair and reasonable levels, these prices continued their upward movement, reaching levels which many market analysts believe are not sustainable over a long period. This was in spite of efforts by OPEC and responsible non-OPEC producers to raise output, so as to create prices more acceptable to the market.

            In this brief opening address, time prevents me from discussing the reasons for this year’s high prices. I should, however, like to make the following remarks.

            First, it is misleading to say that prices have trebled over the past 18 months; it is a far more accurate reflection of the situation to say that prices have risen to their present levels from a decade-long average of almost $18 per barrel, as I mentioned above. They were at the oft-quoted low point of around $10 per barrel for only a brief period in December 1998, in exceptional market circumstances. On the other hand, they were almost $25 per barrel in January 1997.

            Secondly, while the prices of most consumer goods have risen significantly over the past decade and a half, those of oil have not kept pace with this trend and, indeed, on some occasions have actually fallen.

            Thirdly, at least for much of this year and in the United States of America in particular, there has been plenty of crude around when prices have risen, and the supply problems have been further downstream and beyond OPEC’s influence.

 

            Fourthly, as we publicly stated at our last meeting, the high levels of taxation on petroleum products in most consuming countries are greatly amplifying the effects of rises in the price of crude, to the detriment of the consumer. OPEC expresses the hope, once again, that the governments of these countries will reduce their high taxes on a barrel of oil — which is much more than producers themselves receive — in the interests of market stability. In addition, speculation in the oil market has become a key factor that has distorted realities and has artificially influenced prices far beyond what the fundamentals indicate.

            And fifthly, OPEC cares very much about sustainable order and stability in the oil market, with fair and reasonable prices for consumers, and is trying very hard to bring this about; but this is not easy in such a complex and speculative environment as the petroleum industry. OPEC is also appreciative of the active support it has received in its recent market-stabilisation activities from some leading non-OPEC producers, notably Mexico, Norway, Oman and Russia.

            Since our Extraordinary Meeting in June, there has been a considerable amount of contact among producers both within OPEC and beyond, as we jointly seek a lasting recipe for market stability. His Excellency President Hugo Chavez of Venezuela, as host of the Summit, has himself visited all the Member Countries at Head of State level, in preparation for that important event.

            We are all agreed on the need for timely and effective action in bringing an end to the instability of the past three years, so that the international oil market can prosper in the early 21st century, to the benefit of producers and consumers alike, as well as the world economy at large.

            I began this address by referring to OPEC’s 40th anniversary. There is also another important event we shall celebrate this year — this is the 35th anniversary of OPEC setting up its Secretariat in the Austrian capital of Vienna. Since 1965, we have enjoyed excellent relations and friendship with the Federal Republic of Austria, the City of Vienna and the citizens of our host country. I should, therefore, like to express the gratitude of OPEC and its 11 Member Countries for everything our hosts have done over the years to enhance this relationship and to make us feel so welcome, and to say: “Long may this prosper in the future!”.

            This evening, the City of Vienna is hosting a grand reception and dinner, at which more than 600 invited guests are expected, to mark OPEC’s 40th anniversary and the Secretariat’s 35th year in Vienna.

            Thank you.
