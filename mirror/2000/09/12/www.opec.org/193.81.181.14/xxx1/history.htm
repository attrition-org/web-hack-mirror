A BRIEF HISTORY OF OPEC

THE ORGANIZATION OF THE PETROLEUM EXPORTING COUNTRIES is a permanent, intergovernmental Organization, created at the Baghdad Conference of September 10-14, 1960, by Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia and Venezuela. The five Founding Members were later joined by eight other Members: Qatar (1961); Indonesia (1962); Socialist Peoples Libyan Arab Jamahiriya (1962); United Arab Emirates (1967); Algeria (1969); Nigeria (1971); Ecuador (1973-1992)* and Gabon (1975-1994)*. OPEC transferred its headquarters from Geneva, Switzerland, to Vienna, Austria, on 1 September 1965, and moved into the present Secretariat building in 1977.

 OPEC's objective is to co-ordinate and unify petroleum policies among Member Countries, in order to secure fair and stable prices for petroleum producers; an efficient, economic and regular supply of petroleum to consuming nations; and a fair return on capital to those investing in the industry.

 The OPEC Conference of Ministers meets in ordinary session twice a year, and is responsible for the formulation of the general policy of the Organization.

The 1990s

At the fifth Meeting of the Ministerial Monitoring Committee, held on 16-17 March 1990, in Vienna, the Ministers reaffirmed their commitment to the November 1989 pricing and production agreement. On 2-3 May 1990, the Sixth Meeting of the Monitoring Committee, convened in Geneva to address the problem of over-supply in the oil market and the consequent deterioration in crude oil prices, unanimously agreed to take immediate collective action to cut back OPEC production by 1.445 mb/d, the reduction to remain valid until the end of July 1990.

 The 87th Meeting of the Conference, held on 26-27 July 1990, in Geneva, after reviewing the oil market situation decided to make the minimum reference price for the OPEC crude basket $21/b, and that the total ceiling for OPEC production for the second half of 1990 should be set at 22.491 mb/d. It was also decided that the Monitoring Committee should henceforth comprise all 13 OPEC Oil Ministers.

 On 8 August 1990, the OPEC Secretariat issued a statement by HE Sadek Boussena, President of the Conference and Minister of Mines & Industry of Algeria, in connection with the developments in the oil market following the outbreak of the Gulf crisis on 2 August. The President said fears expressed concerning possible disruption of oil supply in the short term were the result of psychological and speculative factors rather than the manifestation of real market fundamentals. Nonetheless, OPEC was aware that the developments induced by the events in the Gulf were fraught with uncertainty as regards the equilibrium and stability of the international oil market, as well as the integrity of the Organization. OPEC would accordingly carefully monitor the evolution of the situation and assess its implications for world oil supply, for Member Countries' revenues and for the interests of both producers and consumers, and take whatever action was deemed necessary. HE Boussena stressed, however, that ensuring stability of supply to the market was the shared responsibility of all oil producers, the international oil industry, the consuming countries' governments and international energy agencies.

 On 26-28 August 1990, informal consultations among OPEC Oil Ministers were held in Vienna. These discussions were immediately followed by a Meeting of the Ministerial Monitoring Committee (now comprising all 13 Member Countries) which decided in view of the need to maintain market stability and supplies to consumers, Member Countries would increase production "in accordance with need". The Committee stressed, however, that this arrangement would be purely temporary, applicable only until such time as the Gulf crisis was deemed to be over. Thereafter, the Organization would revert to the terms of the July 1990 Resolution. On 11 December 1990, in Vienna, the Committee held its second meeting and formulated its recommendations to the Conference.

During the 88th Meeting of the Conference, held in Vienna from 12-13 December, 1990, Member Countries reaffirmed their commitment to the terms of the July 1990 Resolution, and agreed to meet in March 1991.

 In regard to a decision taken earlier in the month by the Independent Petroleum Exporting Countries (IPEC) at a meeting in Cancun, Mexico, the Conference agreed to the creation of a technical working committee between the OPEC Secretariat and IPEC to undertake joint studies on issues of common interest, and to exchange information.

 Meeting in Geneva on 11-12 March 1991, the OPEC Ministerial Monitoring Committee decided that in order to achieve OPEC's minimum reference price of $21/b, as stipulated in the July 1990 Resolution, total production should be lowered to 22.3 mb/d for the second quarter of 1991. At the same time, the Ministers called on other oil exporting countries to show a similar sense of responsibility by proportionally reducing their own production levels.

 A meeting of OPEC experts in Vienna on 29-30 April 1991, formulated a recommendation for submission to the 89th Meeting of the Conference, due to be convened in Vienna on 4 June, to the effect that Member Countries should, both individually and as a group, play a more active role in moves aimed at containing environmental damage, in the interest of global economic growth and development.

 In accordance with the directive issued by the 88th Meeting of the Conference, a group of OPEC technical experts met in Vienna on 13-14 May 1991, to discuss the feasibility of a dialogue between oil producing and consuming countries, as a means of promoting market stability. The experts, meeting under the chairmanship of OPEC's new Deputy Secretary General, Dr Ramzi Salman, agreed on the desirability of such a dialogue and formulated a number of recommendations for submission to the 89th Meeting of the Conference.

These recommendations, it was decided, should not at this initial stage, embrace prices and production levels, but confine themselves to an exchange of information and views on the oil/energy policies of both parties, for the purpose of creating a climate of mutual understanding and confidence-building, and b) security of supply and demand under conditions of reasonable stability within a framework of global energy co-operation.

 Meeting in Vienna on 4 June 1991, the 89th Meeting of the OPEC Conference, bearing in mind OPEC's fundamental objective of stabilizing the oil market, as well as its goal of achieving the OPEC basket minimum reference price of $21/b, agreed to retain for the third quarter 1991 the production ceiling of 22.3 million b/d, as agreed upon for the second quarter of the current year. It was further agreed that the Organization's Ministerial Monitoring Committee should meet in September 1991 to determine the appropriate production ceiling for the fourth quarter 1991. It was subsequently announced that this Meeting of the MMC would be held in Geneva on 24 September 1991.

 The Conference welcomed the initiative on the part of Presidents Franois Mitterand of France and Carlos Andres Perez of Venezuela in convening a producer/consumer meeting in Paris on 1-2 July, and expressed the hope that this meeting would foster long-term cooperation and market stability.

 In Geneva, on 24-25 September 1991, the Fourth Meeting of the MMC decided to raise OPEC's production ceiling to 23.65 mb/d for the fourth quarter of 1991, as recommended by the Organization's Marketing Experts. With regard to the environment, the Committee expressed OPEC's solidarity with the developing countries' request for the transfer of environmentally safe and sound technologies to foster efficient economic growth, adequate energy use, as well as to improve their environment and standard of living. The Committee expressed deep concern over the proposed energy tax being considered by the European Community, pointing out that oil was already disproportionately taxed at the consumption end, as a result of which consumers in Europe had been paying for the past three decades, petroleum taxes which were in some cases more than three times the delivered crude oil price. Any additional tax for environmental or other purposes, the Committee declared, would adversely affect free trade among nations, place oil at a comparative disadvantage in relation to all other energy sources and negatively affect the growth of the world economy, especially in the Third World.

 In preparation for the UN Conference on Environment and Development (UNCED), to be held in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, in June 1992, which was expected to adopt a Framework Convention setting out the terms of a global environment strategy, the Organization conducted a Workshop on the Environment for its Member Countries from 14-16 October, at the OPEC Secretariat in Vienna. The event was designed to give Member Countries the opportunity to discuss the environmental measures being contemplated by industrialized countries, and the possible implications of these measures for developing countries in general and OPEC Members and other oil producers in particular. The Workshop participants then made recommendations to their respective Governments on the approach OPEC Countries should adopt at UNCED.

 On 26-27 November 1991, the 90th Meeting of the OPEC Conference held in Vienna, decided to retain the OPEC production level of 23.65 million b/d agreed upon in September 1991. It further reaffirmed OPEC's commitment to the July 1990 Agreement, including the stated objective of $21/b as the OPEC basket minimum reference price.

 The Sixth Meeting of the MMC, held in Geneva from 12-15 February 1992, decided that OPEC's production ceiling should, with immediate effect, not exceed 22.982 mb/d, including Iraq's current production of 505,000 b/d, and be distributed among Member Countries as follows: (in mb/d)

Algeria 0.760, Ecuador 0.273, Gabon 0.273, Indonesia 1.374, Islamic Republic of Iran 3.184, S.P. Libyan A.J. 1.395, Nigeria 1.751, Qatar 0.377, Saudi Arabia 7.887, United Arab Emirates 2.244, Venezuela 2.147, Kuwait 0.812.

The Islamic Republic of Iran expressed reservations to the effect that a ceiling higher than 22.5 mb/d was a danger to the market. Saudi Arabia did not endorse its allocated production level of 7.887 mb/d and maintained its offer to reduce by 500,000 b/d.

 The Committee reiterated its adherence to the essence of the July 1990 Agreement in its entirety and decided to reinstate the control mechanism stipulated therein, whereby the production and supply of oil by Member Countries would be monitored through two sub-committees, as follows:

1) Algeria, Ecuador, Gabon, Socialist Peoples Libyan Arab Jamahiriya, Nigeria and Venezuela (chaired by HE Prof. Jibril Aminu, Minister of Petroleum and Mineral Resources of Nigeria and President of the Conference);

2) Indonesia, the Islamic Republic of Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates (chaired by HE Yousef Omeir Bin Yousef, Minister of Petroleum and Mineral Resources of the UAE and Alternate President of the Conference). The Committee decided to reconvene as soon as Iraq re-entered the market.

 Recognizing that market stability was a joint responsibility of all oil producers, the MMC appealed to individual Independent Petroleum Exporting Countries (IPEC) to participate, with each country reducing its current production level. The Committee felt that the forthcoming IPEC/OPEC Ministerial Meeting on Environment, scheduled to be held in Vienna on 23 April 1992, would provide an opportunity to review the oil market. Thereafter, a Meeting of the Ministerial Monitoring Committee will be convened in Vienna on 24 April 1992.

 An OPEC Seminar on the Environment took place in Vienna from 13-15 April 1992. Some 280 participants heard 13 eminent speakers address such pertinent issues as: preserving the competitiveness of oil in the face of environment regulations, the priorities of developing countries in relation to the environment and development, investment requirements in the oil industry in the light of environmental challenges, and the origin and nature of the alleged scientific consensus on global warming.

 On 23 April 1992, a Ministerial Meeting was held in Vienna between OPEC Member Countries and Independent Petroleum Exporting Countries (IPEC) to discuss the issue of the environment. The Meeting emphasized the need for a general, flexible and comprehensive Framework Convention on Climate Change, and urged that any fiscal or other response measures to the environmental problem should not hinder world economic growth. It also noted the relationship between poverty and environmental degradation in developing countries, and reiterated the need for adequate transfer of financial resources and appropriate technology from industrialized to developing countries.

 The 91st Meeting of the OPEC Conference, held in Vienna from 21-22 May 1992, decided to roll over into the third quarter of this year, the February 1992 Production Agreement of 22.982 mb/d, inclusive of the reservations of both the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia and the Islamic Republic of Iran. The Conference also decided to allow for any additional production from Kuwait; further, that the President of the Conference may call for a Meeting of the Ministerial Monitoring Committee, if warranted by market developments. The Delegation of Iraq rejected the Resolution adopted. The Conference restated its commitment to the essence of the entire provisions of the July 1990 Agreement and decided that Member Countries should continue to promptly report actual production levels to the Secretariat, as agreed.

 The Ninth Meeting of the Ministerial Monitoring Committee, held in Geneva, Switzerland, on 16-17 September 1992, with the attainment of the $21/b minimum reference price in view, decided that OPEC's market share for the fourth quarter of 1992 should be 24.2 mb/d, to allow for any additional production from Kuwait, reiterated OPEC's commitment to the provisions of the July 1990 Agreement, urged Member Countries to continue to report promptly their actual production levels to the Secretariat and appealed to Member Countries not to take any steps that could disrupt the stability of the market.

 During its 92nd Meeting, held in Vienna on 25-27 November 1992, the OPEC Conference regretfully accepted the wish of Ecuador to suspend its Full Membership of the Organization. However, the Conference hoped that Ecuador would be able to overcome the economic constraints facing it and rejoin the Organization in the not-too-distant future. Turning to the international oil market, the Conference unanimously agreed to a production ceiling of 24.582 mb/d for the first quarter of 1993, with the following temporary allocations (in mb/d): Algeria 0.764; Gabon 0.293; Indonesia 1.374; I.R. Iran 3.490; Iraq 0.500; Kuwait 1.500 (this figure represented current production level); S.P. Libyan A.J. 1.409; Nigeria 1.857; Qatar 0.380; Saudi Arabia 8.395; U.A.E. 2.260; and Venezuela 2.360. These allocations received the full support of Member Countries, excluding Iraq, and it was agreed that they should be introduced in December, where possible. Kuwait was permitted any additional volumes as became available during the quarter.

 The Tenth Meeting of the MMC, held in Vienna on 13-16 February 1993, noted a recent fall in oil prices resulting from over-supply and mild weather conditions during the winter, which had led to a relatively low level of stock drawdown. In order to avert any further deterioration in crude oil prices, the Committee decided to reduce OPEC's overall production ceiling to 23.582 mb/d, with effect from 1 March 1993. Member Countries were given the following respective allocations (in mb/d): Algeria 0.732, Gabon 0.281, Indonesia 1.317, IR Iran 3.340, Iraq 0.400, Kuwait 1.600, SP Libyan AJ 1.350, Nigeria 1.780, Qatar 0.364, Saudi Arabia 8.000, United Arab Emirates 2.161 and Venezuela 2.257.

 The Committee also set up a new monitoring system (Ministerial Monitoring Sub Committee) to ensure Member Countries' compliance with the agreed new ceiling. The system would be administered by a triumvirate comprising: HE Abdalla Salem Al-Badri, Secretary of the People's Committee of Energy of the SP Libyan AJ; HE Abdullah bin Hamad Al Attiyah, Minister of Energy and Industry, Qatar; and OPEC Secretary General, Dr Subroto.

 A joint Meeting of OPEC Member Countries and 14 independent oil producers (Angola, Bahrain, Brunei Darussalam, the People's Republic of China, Colombia, Egypt, Kazakhstan, Malaysia, Mexico, Norway, Oman, the Russian Federation, Republic of Yemen and the State of Texas), was held in Muscat, Oman on 13 April 1993.

In a joint press release issued after the Meeting, the delegates expressed their concern that proposed fiscal measures in the shape of a carbon/energy tax on oil, being contemplated by major consuming countries, would complicate investment decisions of oil producing countries in their efforts to expand production capacity to meet expected increases in oil demand. A new wave of oil taxes would have a destabilizing effect on the oil market and the future growth of oil demand and reduce oil producing countries' income and their ability to undertake their own share of the necessary upstream, midstream and downstream investments.

The 93rd Meeting of the OPEC Conference, held in Geneva from 8-10 June, 1993, agreed to roll over the February 1993 Agreement on production allocations and offered Kuwait an additional 10%, or a production level of 1.76 mb/d, an offer which Kuwait did not accept, insisting on a level of 2.16 mb/d. The effect of the June Agreement was that of a simple rollover. The Conference reiterated its commitment to the minimum reference price of $21 per barrel for the OPEC basket of seven crudes, and asked HE Hacene Mefti, Minister of Energy of Algeria, and HE Dr Alirio Parra, Minister of Mines of Venezuela, together with the Secretary General, to prepare the terms of reference for a Strategic Planning Committee.

 The 11th Meeting of the MMC met in Geneva from 25-29 September 1993. It was subsequently decided to convert the Meeting into the 94th (Extraordinary) Meeting of the Conference. Concern was expressed at the continued deterioration of the OPEC basket price, which had fallen below US$ 15.00/b, and the general instability of the oil market. The Conference, therefore, resolved to set the overall production ceiling for the following six months at 24.5 mb/d, to be distributed among Member Countries as follows (in b/d): Algeria 750,000, Gabon 287,000, Indonesia 1,330,000, IR Iran 3,600,000, Iraq 400,000, Kuwait 2,000,000, SP Libyan AJ 1,390,000, Nigeria 1,865,000, Qatar 378,000, Saudi Arabia 8,000,000, UAE 2,161,000 and Venezuela 2,359,000. HE Ali Ahmad Al-Baghli, Minister of Oil of Kuwait, considered that, as a base for any future allocation, Kuwait's allocation is deemed to be not less than 2.16 mb/d. Iraq was not a party to the agreement and did not endorse it.

The 95th Meeting of the OPEC Conference, which was held in Vienna on 23 and 24 November 1993, reiterated its concern about the continued increase in new energy and environment-related taxes imposed by the governments of major consuming countries. The Conference was of the opinion that achieving the price objectives and the stability of the market was an international responsibility.

 The 12th Meeting of the MMC, held in Geneva, on 25 - 26 March 1994, expressed its concern at the continuing pressure on prices and the low level which prevailed since the 95th Meeting of the Conference, as a result of the continuing increase in non-OPEC crude oil production. It decided to maintain the overall production ceiling of 24.52 mb/d for the remainder of 1994. Iraq was not a party to this agreement and did not endorse it.

 Following the departure of Dr Subroto, the 96th Meeting of the OPEC Conference, held in Vienna on 15-16 June 1994, decided that for six months,from 1 July 1994, the President of the Conference, HE Abdalla Salem El Badri, Secretary of the People's Committee of Energy of the Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya and Head of its Delegation, should assume the responsibilities of the Secretary General. The day-to-day work of the Secretary General was to be carried out by Mr Ali A. Fituri, the Governor of the Socialist People's Libyan Arab Jamahiriya, under the supervision of the President. The 96th Meeting also expressed its deep appreciation of the services rendered by Dr Subroto during his tenure as Secretary General for the preceding six years, and it endorsed a proposal to maintain the seat of the Organization's Headquarters in Vienna.

The Ministerial Monitoring Sub Committee was reconstituted under the Chairmanship of HE Dr Erwin Jose Arrieta, Minister of Energy and Mines of Venezuela. Membership comprised HE Dr Abdul Mohsen Medej Al-Medej, Minister of Oil of Kuwait; HE Alhaji Umaru Baba, Minister of State for Petroleum Resources of Nigeria; and the Secretary General. The 97th Meeting of the Conference, held in Bali, Indonesia on 21-22 November 1994, appointed HE Dr Rilwanu Lukman as Secretary General of the Organization for three years from 1 January 1995. The Meeting also paid tribute to HE Abdalla Salem El-Badri, Secretary of the General People's Committee of Energy of the SP Libyan AJ, on the close of his tenure both as President of the Organization, and for having assumed the responsibilities of Secretary General since 1 July 1994, and thanked Mr Ali A. Fituri for his excellent conduct of the day-to-day affairs of the Secretariat during this time.

 In order to further support oil market stability and price recovery, the Conference decided to maintain until the end of 1995 the ceiling of 24.52 mb/d, distributed according to the temporary allocations decided in the 94th (Extraordinary) Meeting of the Conference in September 1993.

In view of the need for timely and accurate production data to be made available to the market, the Conference authorized the Secretariat to release figures communicated directly from Member Countries on their crude oil production and supply.

 The 98th Meeting of the OPEC Conference, held in Vienna,on 19-20 June 1995, discussed the question of OPEC Membership of Gabon, in which context efforts were to continue to retain this Membership.

 In the international oil market, it reaffirmed its position of maintaining and adhering to the agreed level of supply of 24.52 mb/d for the balance of 1995. It elected HE Dr Erwin Jose Arrieta, Minister of Energy and Mines of Venezuela, as its President. HE Ammar Makhloufi, Minister of Industry and Energy of Algeria, was elected Alternate President.

 The Conference decided to reconstitute the Ministerial Monitoring Sub Committee under the Chairmanship of HE Dr Abdul Mohsen Medej Al-Medej, Minister of Oil of Kuwait; with HE Chief Dan Etete, Minister of Petroleum Resources of Nigeria; HE Rakadh Bin Salem Bin Hamed Bin Rakadh, Minister of Public Works and Housing and Acting Minister of Petroleum and Mineral Resources of the United Arab Emirates, and Dr Rilwanu Lukman, Secretary General, as members.

 The 99th Meeting of the Conference, held in Vienna on 21 and 22 November 1995, decided, in the light of the oil market performance during 1995, and the foreseen supply/demand situation as of January 1996, to maintain the ceiling of 24.52 mb/d until the end of June 1996.

 The 100th Meeting of the Conference convened in Vienna, Austria, from 5-7 June 1996. Having reviewed the current market situation, and considering the oil-for-humanitarian aid deal between Iraq and the United Nations, the Conference agreed to set the overall supply ceiling until the end of 1996 at 25.033 mb/d, with the following allocations (except for Iraq), as set forth in Resolution 94.326 of 29 September 1993, to which the Member Countries reiterated their commitment: (in tb/d) -

 Algeria, 750; Indonesia 1,330; IR Iran 3,600; Kuwait 2,000; SP Libyan AJ 1,390; Nigeria 1,865; Qatar 378; Saudi Arabia 8,000; United Arab Emirates 2,161; Venezuela 2,359. The remaining volume of 1.2 mb/d is to be supplied by Iraq.

The Conference decided to reconstitute the Ministerial Monitoring Sub-Committee under the Chairmanship of HE Gholamreza Aghazadeh, Minister of Petroleum of IR Iran, with HE Abdalla Salem El-Badri, Secretary of the General People's Committee of Energy of the SP Libyan AJ, HE Chief Dan Etete, Minister of Petroleum Resources of Nigeria, and HE Dr Rilwanu Lukman, Secretary General, as Members.

The Conference passed Resolutions which were published on 7 July 1996, after ratification by Member Countries. Resolution No 100.350 stated that: "The Conference has taken note of the notice submitted by Gabon on 20 December 1994, and considers Gabon's Membership in the Organization terminated as of 1 January 1995, in accordance with Article 8 of the Statute." 

At the 101st Meeting of the Conference, which was held in Vienna, Austria, from 27-28 November 1996, the Conference, having reviewed the market situation, agreed to roll over until the end of June 1997, the overall supply ceiling of 25.033 mb/d and individual Member Countries' allocations, to which all Member Countries reiterated their commitment. The Conference also expressed its deepest appreciation and gratitude for the services rendered to the Organization by Dr Ramzi Salman, the outgoing Deputy Secretary General, whose term of office ended on 3 March 1997, and thanked him for the active and positive contribution which he had made to its work.

At the 102nd Meeting of the Conference, held in Vienna, Austria, on 25-26 June 1997, the Conference, having reviewed the current market situation, agreed to roll over, until the end of 1997, the overall ceiling of 25.033 mb/d and individual Member Countries' allocations thereunder. Determined to ensure stability in the market, and in the interests of maintaining Member Countries' revenues at acceptable levels, Member Countries strongly reiterated their firm commitment to this arrangement and made an undertaking to restrain output to their agreed level of allocation.

At the 103rd Meeting of the Conference, which was held in Jakarta, Indonesia, from 26 November to 1 December 1997, the Ministers, having reviewed the market situation, agreed to set the OPEC ceiling, for the first half of 1998, at 27.5 mb/d, with effect from 1 January 1998, distributed among Member Countries in accordance with the following allocations (thousand barrels/day): Algeria 908.50; Indonesia 1,456.44; Islamic Republic of Iran 3,942.24; Kuwait 2,190.13; S.P. Libyan A.J. 1,522.14; Nigeria 2,042.30; Qatar 413.94; Saudi Arabia 8,760.53; United Arab Emirates 2,366.44 and Venezuela 2,583.26. The remaining volume of 1,314.08 tb/d is to be supplied by Iraq.

At the 104th (Extraordinary) Meeting of the Conference in Vienna, Austria, on 30 March 1998, the Conference expressed its pleasure in welcoming to the Meeting representatives of non-OPEC Mexico, a presence which was viewed as a sign of the new spirit of co-operation emerging amongst oil producers.

The OPEC Member Countries, having observed with concern the severe weakening of crude oil prices throughout the winter and reflecting their serious desire to stabilize the market in the interests of all oil producers, agreed to voluntarily cut the following amounts from their current production, with effect from 1 April 1998, until the end of the year.

Algeria 50,000 b/d; Indonesia 70,000 b/d; Islamic Republic of Iran 140,000 b/d; Kuwait 125,000 b/d; Socialist Peoples Libyan Arab Jamahiriya 80,000 b/d; Nigeria 125,000 b/d; Qatar 30,000 b/d; Saudi Arabia 300,000 b/d; United Arab Emirates 125,000 b/d; Venezuela 200,000 b/d (total: 1,245 mb/d).

These cuts are based on production as given by selected secondary sources for the month of February 1998, namely: Algeria 868,000 b/d; Indonesia 1,380,000 b/d; IR Iran 3,623,000 b/d; Kuwait 2,205,000 b/d; SP Libyan AJ 1,453,000 b/d; Nigeria 2,258,000 b/d; Qatar 700,000 b/d; Saudi Arabia 8,748,000 b/d; UAE 2,382,000 b/d; Venezuela 3,370,000 b/d.

These figures do not constitute new quotas and the cuts are intended to be only temporary until the end of 1998. In light of its exceptional circumstances, Iraq was not called upon to participate in this agreement.

The Conference recognized and appreciated the production cuts pledged by some non-OPEC oil-producing countries, in particular the Sultanate of Oman, Mexico and others, and agreed to continue consultations with the non-OPEC oil producers so as to establish and maintain stability in the oil market in the future. The Conference also appealed to other non-OPEC oil exporters to support these measures to stabilize the market by moderating their output, in the interests of all concerned.

At the 105th Meeting of the Conference, held in Vienna on 24 June 1998, the Conference expressed its pleasure at the presence of high-level representatives of Mexico, the Russian Federation, and the Sultanate of Oman.

The Conference, having reviewed the current market situation, agreed to make further reductions in the production of Member Countries (excluding Iraq), bringing total reductions to 2.6 mb/d, so that the base (using February 1998 production levels as given by OPEC selected secondary sources), total reductions and Member Countries’ production will be as follows (in barrels/day): Algeria 868,000 (base), 80,000 (reduction), 788,000 (production); Indonesia 1,380,000 (b), 100,000 (r), 1,280,000 (p); IR Iran 3,623,000 (b), 305,000 (r), 3,318,000 (p); Kuwait 2,205,000 (b), 225,000 (r), 1,980,000 (p); SP Libyan AJ 1,453,000 (b), 130,000 (r), 1,323,000 (p); Nigeria 2,258,000 (b), 225,000 (r), 2,033,000 (p); Qatar 700,000 (b), 60,000 (r), 640,000 (p); Saudi Arabia 8,748,000 (b), 725,000 (r), 8,023,000 (p); UAE 2,382,000 (b), 225,000 (r), 2,157,000 (p); Venezuela 3,370,000 (b), 525,000 (r), 2,845,000 (p).

The above agreement is effective from 1st July 1998 and valid for one year. Member Countries emphasized their firm commitment to this agreement.

The Conference expressed its appreciation for the reductions which were pledged by non-OPEC producing countries since March 1998, totalling in excess of 500,000 b/d, including 200,000 b/d from Mexico, 100,000 b/d from the Russian Federation, and 50,000 b/d from Oman, among others. This will bring the total supply withdrawn from the market to more than 3.1 mb/d.

At the 106th Meeting of the Conference, held in Vienna on 25-26 November 1998, the Conference warmly received high-level representatives from Mexico, the Russian Federation, and the Sultanate of Oman, non-OPEC oil-exporting countries whose continued support of measures being taken to support oil prices was welcomed by the Organization.

The Conference decided to realign the scheduling of its bi-annual Ordinary Meetings, which, henceforth, will take place in the months of March and September, rather than June and November. The Conference, having reviewed the market situation, decided that, at its next Ordinary Meeting, it would assess the market situation and take whatever actions or measures that might be deemed appropriate should prices remain at unacceptable levels.
