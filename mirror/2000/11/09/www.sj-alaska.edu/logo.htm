About the Sheldon Jackson logo . . .

The Sheldon Jackson logo is comprised of five, carefully selected components:

Sheldon Jackson the man
    Yeah, your suspicions are true: Sheldon Jackson did not really look like our caricature of him. But the reason we stylized him was because, as you probably know, photographs from his era (late 1800s) always portrayed people unsmiling and austere. And, if you read about Jackson the man, he was energetic and passionate--qualities which don’t emerge from his photograph.
    We’re proud to be named after Sheldon Jackson because:
  • Graduate of Princeton Seminary
  • Became a Presbyterian missionary and oversaw church work in the western US
  • Traveled 30,000 miles/year (in the mid-1800s!), raising thousands of dollars for schools (31) and churches (350!)
  • Collected Native art throughout his travels because, as he wrote a friend, "...in a few years there would be nothing left to show the coming generations of natives how their fathers lived."
  • A close friend of President Benjamin Harrison
  • First General Agent of Education in Alaska
  • Made 33 trips to Siberia, importing nearly 1300 reindeer for Alaskan Eskimos
  • By his death at age 75, had made 26 trips to Alaska
  • Short on height (5'2") but clearly long on energy and faith
The tree  . . .
. . . is a Sitka Spruce. These majestic trees grow to 150 feet and are ideal for aircraft, sailboat masts, and violins because they are the strongest yet lightest of all trees. Howard Hughes’ famous Spruce Goose was built of Sitka Spruce. The young, green spruce tips are very high in vitamin C, so the Russians who inhabited Baranof Island in the early 1800s used them in their beer because it healed scurvy. Sitka Spruce comprise 20% of Southeast Alaska’s Tongass National Forest: the largest forest in North America and the largest remaining temperate rain forest in the world (16.8 million acres!).
The waters . . .
 . . .of Sitka Sound--warmed by the warm Japanese currents of the Pacific Ocean--are some of the richest in the world: home to five species of salmon, halibut, sea lions, otters, herring, killer whales (orcas), and--most abundant of all--humpback whales. SJ’s historic campus sits on the Green overlooking Crescent Bay on the Sound, so we see this proliferation of sea creatures daily, along with the eagles and herons and other bird life which depend also on the sea.

The fish . . .
. . . symbolizes Sheldon Jackson College in two ways:
  1. The one in the graphic is the pink (or humpy) salmon.*  SJ is a leader in aquatic education--for obvious reasons: both fresh water and salt water within five minutes of campus! We have the only production salmon hatchery on a campus in the US, raising four species of salmon: Coho, Chum, Pink, and King. These salmon are spawned in fresh water and released as fry into salt water where they spend two-to-seven years, swimming thousands of miles. As they mature, the salmon return to the exact same hatchery or stream from which they were hatched years before! You can see them leaping for joy as they return home to Sitka Sound each August.
  2. The fish is also the traditional symbol of Christianity.  Sheldon Jackson himself was a Presbyterian missionary and the College is still related to the Presbyterian Church (USA).  Scores of volunteers help the faculty and administration serve students, and most of these come through the Presbyterian Church.  Spiritual -- as well as intellectual and environmental -- growth continues to be encouraged at SJC and is a principle in our Mission.

The mountains . . .
 . . .that protect Sitka appear to jump right out of the ocean. Each mountain on SJ’s home island (Baranof) has its own name and is not part of a specific range. Some of the mountains near Sitka include:
    • Mt. Edgecumbe. Dormant for 10,000 years, this 3200-foot volcano reminds many of Japan’s Mount Fuji. In 1972, a local prankster helicoptered 70 tires to Edgecumbe, deposited them in the volcano’s cone, and set them on fire. When Sitkans arose on April 1, the smoke emerging from Edgecumbe convinced many that the volcano was erupting again. Those who investigated the eruption found "April Fool" stamped in the snow beside the tires!*
    • Verstovia. Probably the toughest trail among the many around Sitka, Verstovia’s elevation is about 3500 feet. It was named by the Russians because a Russian "verst" is 3500 feet.
    • Anahootz and Katlian. Both named for Tlingit leaders, Katlian was a warrior who led his people in their rebellions against the early Russians.

    Seventeen of the US’s 20 highest mountain peaks are in Alaska!
