PUSD Field Science Partnership


A partnership between Page High School (Page, AZ) and
The National Park Service at Glen Canyon National Recreation Area 

 

FIELD SCIENCE PARTNERSHIP CURRICULUM INTRODUCTION

    In May 1996, the Page Unified School District requested funds from the Arizona Community Foundation to begin the development of a partnership with the National Park Service at Glen Canyon National Recreation Area (GCNRA).  At the same time, National Park Service officials submitted funding requests to the National Parks Foundation and the Parks and Classrooms program to provide matching funds for the development of the partnership.  The primary goal of this partnership would be to increase student learning for district students while at the same time enhancing research activities within the Recreation Area.
    The above mentioned entities approved the funding in late 1996 and in February 1997, the Park Service employed an Educational Specialist.  The position description for the Specialist specifically identifies the program goal as a primary function.
   The program is developing into an exciting learning opportunity for students.  Two major research projects are now in place.  Students collect water on a regular rotating time schedule from Lake Powell and are learning how to test that water for various microorganisms.  Students are also monitoring an experimental population of endangered razorback suckers that are being raised in City of Page Golf Course ponds.  Future plans call for a third research project that will involve students in the development of a Park Service policy to manage range uses within the Recreation Area.
    A request for further funding is currently under consideration by the National Park Service.  These funds will be used to expand the availability of program activities to students outside of the immediate area.  Field trips of three to five days are envisioned, with students performing a variety of hands-on learning activities while visiting Lake Powell and the Glen Canyon area.
    This document is an effort to consolidate and identify the current status of the curriculum in use by students in the partnership.  This document will become the model for development of other curriculum topics used in the project.

PRIMARY PARTNERSHIP ACTIVITIES Objectives met as a result of this partnership include:

Employment of a National Park Service Educational Specialist to provide coordination between participating agencies and school district instructors.

Use of professionals from outside the district in both learning and evaluation activities.

Conducting of research in partnership with National Park Service personnel and other agencies involved in natural resource management in the area.

Reporting of data to various audiences including interested scientific community members, funding entities, other students, and the Internet.

    The Field Science Partnership is currently developing two major science projects.  These are the Lake Powell Water Quality Monitoring Program and rearing of an experimental population of endangered razorback suckers.  These projects allow students to work closely with National Park Service personnel and other scientists from the US Fish and Wildlife Service, the Bureau of Land Management, Arizona Fish and Game, Utah Division of Wildlife Resources and the City of Page.  An outline of each of the projects is included below.
 

Lake Powell Water Quality Monitoring Program

    The National Park Service has collected data since the filling of Lake Powell in the 1960’s to determine levels of dangerous bacteria that may be present in the water near heavily used beaches on the lake.  In the past, levels have exceeded safety margins leading to closures of some beaches and canyons to recreation activities.  Student participation in this activity will allow the Park Service to increase the number and frequency of water sampling and allow them to better predict trends that may appear.
 

Razorback Sucker Project

    In 1996, the National Park Service placed an experimental population of an endangered fish, the razorback sucker into two local golf course ponds owned by the City of Page.  The objective is to raise the fish to an appropriate size to decrease the probability of predation from non-natives and release them into the San Juan arm of Lake Powell.  The fish were netted from the ponds and released into the San Juan River in April of 1998.  A new population of fingerlings replaced these in December of 1998 and are currently being monitored.  The students are involved in projects that monitor the water quality, the size and health of the fish.  Students are also involved in mapping projects and in studying the environmental systems that effect the ponds.  Since the water is reclaimed from the city’s water treatment plant, students gain knowledge about water reclamation, Department of Water Quality standards and safety standards not normally found in a high school lab.  The need to determine ideal release locations for future populations will allow student participants to travel extensively within a 150 mile radius of Page, often by boat or even airplane.
