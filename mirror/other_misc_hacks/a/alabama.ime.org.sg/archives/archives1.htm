A New Electronic Packaging Consortium for the New Millennium
Press Release - December 1999

Singapore, December 18, 1999 - Seventeen multi-national corporations (MNC) and promising local enterprises (PLE) companies signed an agreement with Institute of Microelectronics (IME) to form the "Electronic Packaging Research Consortium (EPRC) 2000" to join forces in the new millennium to research and develop competitive enabling technologies for the packaging of next generation integrated circuits (ICs). The consortium shall take effect from 1 Jan 2000 and last for 18 months to complete. The combined efforts are estimated to be S$5 million.

The 17 companies are Advanced Micro Devices, Agilent Technologies, AlliedSignal, Amkor Technology, Delphi Automotive Systems, Hitachi Chemical, Infineon Technologies, Lucent Technologies, MicroCircuit Technology, MicroFab Technology, Motorola, Nitto Denko, Philips, Showa Denko, ST Assembly Test Services, Sumitomo Bakelite and SysPac Technologies. These companies are leaders in their own fields such as semiconductor, advanced materials, equipment and wafer bumping technology. They are expected to contribute significantly to the overall electronic packaging business.

Mr Liew Heng San, Managing Director of the Economic Development Board said: "As we move into the new millenium, enabling technologies for Integrated Circuits packaging will become increasingly important in the next generation semiconductor devices. This collaboration and sharing of experience and capabilities between international and local companies through a consortium like EPRC will enhance Singapore's competitiveness in the electronic packaging industry."

The EPRC program will be targeted at technologies for ICs to operate at speeds of up to 10 GHz, power dissipation of up to 75 W and with up to 1,500 pins. These packages are subsequently assembled onto printed circuit boards (PCB) to form the engines of electronic equipment such as computers and cellular telephones. The ICs are the essential building blocks of the bulk of today’s electronics appliances and equipment. The delicate operation in the manufacturing of an IC determines the size, weight, and speed, and of course, the cost of the end electronic product. The programme shall require innovations in state of the art electrical, thermal and mechanical designs, and very high density circuit substrate and interconnection technologies.

Said Dr Lim Thiam Beng, R&D Manager of the Advanced Packaging and Development Support department in IME, who is responsible for the EPRC, "Together, these 17 companies form a formidable force to collectively address some of the most challenging issues relating to the packaging of next generation ICs. Their willingness to come together signifies their recognition and confidence in a Singaporean research institute to deliver competitive technologies for their businesses. The biggest advantages of the consortium are to leverage on the members’ experience and also to defray the cost of R&D."

ADVANCED MICRO DEVICES (AMD) and IME COLLABORATE ON ADVANCED HIGH FREQUENCY PACKAGE DESIGN

AMD and IME join hands to develop high frequency characterization techniques for AMD’s advanced microprocessor packages. In this project, IME will develop validated simulation models for the current and future requirements of AMD’s multilayer packages.

IME’s integrated package design capabilities have been well received by the industry. These include expertise in the areas of electrical/electro magnetic, thermal, structural and moisture modeling and characterization. The availability of such capabilities enhances the electronic packaging activities in Singapore.

The EPRC is one such "catalyst" in which IME is using its core competencies in this particular area of microelectronics to support the electronic packaging industry to further enhance their capabilities to compete in the world arena. The EPRC is further divided into smaller projects or task teams comprising engineers assigned by the member companies. All the members, including IME shared the responsibilities to realise the goals of the consortium. Such active participation invariably contains elements of training and technology transfer.

Said Dr Bill Chen, Director of IME, "In the short span of 5 years, the EPRC has evolved and advanced from one that addresses generic technological issues into a platform to address the competitive issues for the industry. We are very happy to have earned the respect of the industry. It shows that there is a mutual trust and commitment between IME and its industrial partners. 17 companies is the highest number to join the EPRC so far."

 

FG-LDMOS - a new power device developed by IME

Folded Gate LDMOSFET for Low On-Resistance and High Transconductance

A novel LDMOSFET transistor was proposed and demonstrated experimentally. The silicon substrate surface is trenched by using an extra mask, resulting in a folded gate structure. The channel density is doubled in the experiment. With the FG-LDMOSFET concept, the on-resistance was reduced by 40%, while the transconductance was improved by 80%. The significance of the folded gate concept will be available for CMOS and other MOS-gated devices.

Chartered Semiconductor and IME Collaborate on Deep Submicron Copper and Radio-Frequency Processes
Press Release 12 August 1999

Chartered Semiconductor Manufacturing Ltd (CSM) and Institute of Microelectronics (IME) have entered into a memorandum of understanding for two projects: 1) the manufacture of 0.18um deep-submicron integrated circuits using copper technology and 2) the development of RFICs. The cooperative work supports 0.18um interconnect development work already in progress between Lucent Technologies and Chartered Semiconductor.

Under the first part of the pact, Chartered will transfer a 0.18um copper CMOS logic back-end-of-line process from Lucent’s USA (Orlando, Florida) facility to IME so that the two organizations can reproduce the process and fabricate prototypes. Back-end-of-line processing establishes the way that metal layers -- in this case, copper -- are connected to the transistors that are the heart of an integrated circuit. Working with Chartered engineers, IME will use its fabrication facility to duplicate the steps in connecting the copper and transistor levels of ICs for producing prototypes. Once this is done, the process will be transferred to Chartered’s foundry for volume manufacturing.

The second part of the collaboration calls for IME to develop a back-end-of-line process for integrated circuits for radio-frequency (RF) applications, with support from Chartered. RF ICs allow the realisation of chips for wireless applications such as those for next-generation mobile handsets, wireless electronic toll collection systems, and wireless connection solutions for portable electronic gadgets, to name a few.

In a related project, IME, a national institute for microelectronics R&D, has recently been awarded a total of $120 million through the National Science and Technology Board (NSTB) to undertake a programme in deep sub-micron integrated circuit process technology. The programme consists of copper interconnect, low-k dielectric materials, advanced lithography and in-line metrology for the wafer fabrication industry in Singapore. The National Science and Technology Board (NSTB) has been instrumental in bringing the parties together to undertake such leading edge R&D activities in process technology development.

Earlier this year, Chartered, IME, the Institute of Material Research and Engineering, National University of Singapore, and Nanyang Technological University announced the successful conclusion of a three-year project that had focused on development of 0.25um CMOS processes, which have been high-volume processes at Chartered.

VISIT BY BG (NS) GEORGE YEO,
MINISTER FOR TRADE AND INDUSTRY

IME hosted a visit for BG (NS) George Yeo, Minister for Trade and Industry (MTI) on 6 August 1999. Our Director, Dr Bill Chen presented IME’s competencies in the various areas of microelectronics and shared with him how we have been working with the industry. During the question and answer session, the Minister was interested in some of the challenges facing IME. One of the challenges which was discussed at length is the manpower issue.

The presentation was followed by a lab tour to the VLSI Circuit Design and Test (VLSI) and Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems (MEMS) laboratories. Demonstrations on radio frequency and mixed-signal integrated circuit design projects were incorporated into the tour. For the MEMS area, the Minister was treated to demonstrations consisting of the silicon microphone, accelerometer and bolometer based thermal imager. Other than the thermal imager, the demonstrations comprise of products or projects which IME has successfully delivered to our industrial partners. During the tour, other competencies such as packaging and failure analysis were also presented and related. A tour to the recently completed cleanroom for Deep Submicron Integrated Circuit (DSIC) technology development ended with a sharing of DSIC process technology development programme.

The visit ended with light refreshments, which allowed IME’s management staff and the Minister to exchange further information.

IME GOES Y2K COMPLIANCE

The year 2000 problem (Y2K) or millennium bug is caused by equipment that contains microprocessors that misprocess date calculations. The potential damage of the millennium bug is immense and IME has taken positive steps to ensure that all our equipment and software are shielded from the Y2K related problems. We have completed examining all major equipment and software for Y2K compliance. What remains are the very few items that have yet to receive response from vendors on its Y2K status. IME is on the right path to go Y2K compliance.

 

EDN ASIA INTERVIEWS IME

Innovator translates real-world requirements into mixed-signal devices

Distilling complex design requirements into a mixed-signal ASIC is a demanding job that requires a combination of skills. Thanks to his powerful conceptual and engineering, Dr. Lau Choon-How has compiled an impressive array of innovative designs. The 39-year-old Lau, who has a Ph.D. in VLSI design from the University of Edinburgh, UK, is a technical staff member at Singapore's Institute of Microelectronics (IME).

When presented with a design problem, Lau starts with questions. "The key to engineering is not in knowing the answer, but in asking the relevant questions," he says. The questions help him break down the problem and then create function blocks to solve it. Then he uses his expertise with EDA simulation tools to actually design and verify the function blocks.

For example, when a local company approached the IME with a request for automotive airbag sensors, Lau defined a system and chip architecture for a capacitive based MEMS (microelectromechanical system) accelerometer. This challenging design required precision in both the electrical and mechanical domains. Electrostatic force within the accelerometer threatened to swamp the fragile fA (femto amperes) currents, rendering the sensor useless.

Lau created a model of the accelerometer's electrical and mechanical properties using an electromechanical simulator tool. Then he adjusted the relevant parameters to counter the electrostatic effect.  Lau's early experiences-working on automotive sensors in Europe and customizing compiler tools with Singapore's Silicon Compilation Technology-helped him to develop his skills. In the latter job, his task was to customize silicon compilers to re-target designs for specific process geometries. He succeeded by generating timing models for digital design libraries from foundry specific HSPICE device and process parameters.

After three years of this commercial work, Lau was eager to move into a more research-oriented atmosphere. He joined the fledgling IME in 1992. "We started from ground zero and had to bootstrap ourselves up from that level," he says. Soon Lau and the other staff were developing new methods of integrating CAD tools, modeling, and design techniques to produce mixed-signal devices. 

The designers aimed to build function blocks that could be scaled to suit different geometry requirements. Lau began with an ADC because of its universal use as a front-end signal processing device. He and his team successfully designed a 500-kHz, l0-bit, CMOS, capacitive successive-approximation register (SAR) ADC core in 12 months.

Experience gained in the ADC project boosted Lau's confidence and gave him the fillip to venture into the electromechanical domain of MEMS. An opportunity soon arose: A local company wanted to shrink a discrete design for a stereo audio effects amplifier into a single chip. Intended for PC games applications, the design had to let the joystick control the volume setting. Also, Lau's team faced the formidable task of writing the complex algorithms needed to satisfy the game requirements. After solving all these problems, Lau found that noise from the PC's power supply corrupted the sound quality. He corrected that by incorporating advanced filter circuitry within the chip.

Lau's hard-won skills and his function-block strategy are paying off. For a recent design combining second order sigma-delta DAC and ADC functions, his expertise in mixed-signal simulation and verification allowed his team to complete the design in only nine months. 

 

TECHNOLOGY DIGEST INTERVIEWS IME

- Biting the IME Bait -

In Singapore's bid to become the semiconductor hub of Asia, the Institute of Microelectronics (IME) plays an important role in attracting foreign investors into Singapore. With its research capabilities, IME has been instrumental in advancing microelectronics technology to the next level. By providing such technical and research support, IME aims to attract companies in related industries to invest in Singapore.

IME's quality policy states that IME is dedicated to add value to its partners' business, and to ensure that its research and development processes and procedures are compatible with its manufacturing practices and with the needs of its customers.

The institute was started in 1991 as a department within NUS. Like the Institute of Manufacturing Technology (currently known as Gintic Institute of Manufacturing Technology), the setting up of the institution was recommended by the Economic Development Board (EDB). Currently, the Institute is financed up to 75 per cent by the National Science & Technology Board (NSTB)and 25 per cent by the industry. IME hopes to increase the industry contribution to 40 per cent by year 2001.

Recruiting and retaining good technical staff for research is one challenge IME faces. Dr Tan Khen Sang, Associate Director of Research and Development, explained, "The pool of talent (in Singapore) is limited so we have to reach out to the region.

"Currently, though, IME has about 100 technical staff out of which about 88 percent are local talents. "Many of these people want to go to the US because the R&D culture you get in the US is not found here yet," he continued. 

He himself spent about 14 years in the US working for corporate research and development before joining IME. In the US, he explains, you can find more dedicated engineers. These engineers are happy to spend years in their field as engineers and slowly gain expertise. "In Singapore, engineers want to get promoted quickly to a managerial position. It's the local working culture. No one wants to remain an engineer here."

He sees IME as a place where research culture should be inculcated. One of IME's strategies is to motivate technical staff by allowing them to spend half their time working on core projects. The rest of their time is dedicated to working on customers' projects.

IME's focus is on applied research and developments in microelectronics. To help Singapore achieve world class status in the field of microelectronics, IME has identified key strategic areas focus. These areas are supported by different capabilities within IME coming together to address the needs of its industrial partners.

Some of these capabilities include:

Advanced Services

IME assists industrial partners with technical capabilities which are often not found in this part of the world but which are required for their efficient business operations. The establishment of Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) capability is one which is particularly welcomed by companies working in submicron dimensions which need sophisticated surface analyses. Chip package analysis, transmission electron microscopy and integrated circuit chip prototyping are some of the advanced services that are also available. To date, IME supports a range of advanced services, which enable microelectronics related companies in Singapore to remain competitive.

Sensor Design and Technology

IME has developed a complete set of capabilities in bulk machining of Si substrates, benefiting on-going work in microsensors such as micro-accelerometers and silicon microphones. In addition to the bulk machining, IME is also developing capabilities in surface machining of Si substrates. The integration of these capabilities with that IC design of mixed-signal systems and Si processing of integrated circuits has enabled IME to work on a new area of microsensors known as "smart sensors". With these integrated capabilities, IME and its industrial partners have started work on smart surface micro-machined sensors.

Miniaturisation for Portable Electronics

IME's ability to integrate electronic systems and sub-systems as single chip solutions, particularly with low power requirements, is one of the factors to miniaturisation for portable electronic products. Its capability in RF integrated circuit design further adds to the technologies required for product miniaturisation and portability. On the application side, IME offers digital signal processing (DSP) capabilities, targeting products in multi-media and mobile communication.

R&D Manpower Development

IME supports manpower development through its industrial and student attachment schemes. Industrial attachments are aimed at training existing engineers on relevant microelectronic technologies while student attachments equip final year postgraduate students with capabilities sought after by the industry. IME works closely with the Economic Development Board and the National Science and Technology Board to develop the manpower needed by the industry and that of the national plans for establishment of new microelectronics related industries. Besides attachments, this is also achieved through courses, workshops and seminars on specific microelectronics related areas organised for engineers in the industry.

Deep Submicron Integrated Circuit Technology

With the growing number of companies establishing their state-of-the-art wafer fabrication operations in Singapore, it has become necessary to service the needs of these companies so that they can compete more effectively on the international front. The forecast made by the Economic Development Board to have 25 fabs in Singapore within the next decade further stresses the need to establish support capabilities in these areas. Because of this, IME has been charged with supporting these companies and developing new fabrication technologies that include a 0.25 micron joint development programme, undertaken with Chartered Semiconductor Manufacturing Pte Ltd (CSM), and a longer term programme undertaken concurrently at IME to address issues such as interconnects related to 0.18 micron technologies.

There are also consortium projects. For example the yearly EPRC - Electronic Packaging Research Consortium has identified five projects to work on. The projects for this year are:

a)Wire Bond BGA Packaging
b)Flip-chip BGA Packaging
c)Manufacturing cycle time reduction:Snap cure DA, Non-PMC EMC and Pd Plated leadframe
e)Advanced FEA Modelling for IC packaging Design & Performance Evaluation of High Density Packages & Boards

Members of this consortium include ESEC, Philips, AMD, Plaskon, GUL Technologies, Sumitomo Bakelite, Hitachi Chemical, SGS-Thomson, Hewlett-Packard, Siemens, Johnson Matthey, STATS, Lucent Technologies and Texas Instruments.
