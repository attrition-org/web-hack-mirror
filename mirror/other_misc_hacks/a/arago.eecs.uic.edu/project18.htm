Impedance Based Immunobiosensors
$179,000 - The Whittaker Foundation
Dr. Peter Hesketh (PI); Dr. Jordan Maclay, Dr. William Hanley, Dr. J. Stetter,
Dr. George Wilson, Dr. S. Gendel, Dr. M. DeSilva (Co-PI’s)
Sept 1994 - Aug 1997

The immediate goal of this project is to develop an understanding of the sensing mechanism in a novel ultrathin platinum film conductimetric immunosensor, using the model antigen/antibody pair Staphylococcal Enterotoxin B antibody/Staphylococcal Enterotoxin B. A unique feature of the proposed direct examination is the metal sensing electrode being used. It is an ultrathin platinum film which forms a discontinuous highly porous layer comprised of platinum islands separated by spaces of insulator. The dimensions of the metal islands in the film, as well as the inter-island spaces, are in the same dimensional range as that of antibodies which are immobilized on the surface.
The protein is bound in these spaces so that a mosaic network of metal islands and protein is formed. When a binding event occurs a change in film conductivity is observed which is a direct transduction of the binding processes. The immunosensor comprises an ultrathin layer of platinum on an insulating substrate of silicon dioxide (or silicon nitride) coated on a silicon wafer. An antibody is covalently immobilized on the silicon dioxide surface.
Connections are made to the sensor for conductivity measurements through platinum contact pads spaced 1.3 mm apart. The structure of the ultrathin metal film has been observed by transmission electron microscope(TEM), and atomic force microscope (AFM).
We are investigating the factors that determine sensor performance, including sensitivity, specificity, and stability. We want to develop an understanding of the sensing mechanism in order to design improved sensors. The success of the sensor in responding to antibody-antigen binding depends on two factors: (1) the ultrathin platinum film structure, and (2) the immobilization of the antibody. In order to determine the role of the film morphology, a series of different island spacings and different noble metals on different substrates will be made. The film morphology can be controlled through deposition conditions, in order to tailor make the dimensions of the sensor for a given antibody size (Esfahani et al,92). Our initial work has been with polyclonal anti-staphylococcal enterotoxin B (anti-SEB) and we will continue to use this as a model system. Many techniques have been developed for immobilization of antibodies in liquid phase affinity chromatography. They typically result in approximately 20% of the immobilized antibodies being viable and active. Immobilization schemes that maintain a high degree of antibody activity and optimize the transduction of the binding event will be studied to develop sensors with high sensitivity and stability. To the best of our knowledge our group has reported the first work with an impedance based ultrathin metal film biosensor.
