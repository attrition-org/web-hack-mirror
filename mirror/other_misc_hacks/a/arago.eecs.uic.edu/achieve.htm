Recent Technical Achievements (Since 1992)

Demonstration of a direct immunobiosensor-capable of detecting sub ng/ml concentrations of a bacterial toxin.
Development of a model of crystal plane selectivity in anisotropic etching of silicon-the first model to explain the effects of changing etchant cation.
Development of multilayer microfabricated polymer membranes for biocompatibility-patented by industrial partner and undergoing animal studies.
Design and fabrication of a micromachined accelerometer-believed to be the first displaying an accuracy better than 10% for accelerations below 100g.
Developed methods of modeling two and three dimensional magnetic and electrostatic systems with hysteresis-has since been incorporated in commercial software products.
Delivered prototypes of low power microfabricated carbon monoxide sensors-first microfabricated CO sensor compensated for temperature and humidity variations.
Delivered prototypes of low cost high performance temperature sensor-use in self cleaning ovens.
Delivered a prototype micromachined X-ray focusing element-for use in the Advanced Photon Source at Argonne National Lab.
Fabricated and tested a micromachined SEM electrostatic column-using a commercial thermal field emission tip, and machined deflectors to scan the beam.
Fabricated and tested a field emission tip on a microbridge-out of single crystal silicon tip heated to over 900C.
Fabricated a 60 GHz linear accelerator structure-stacking two waveguide sections.
Demonstrated shadow images in the miniature SEM-with submicron resolution.
Obtained images of TEM grid-with 6um wires
Line scan indicates 0.5um resolution-with a partially micromachined SEM.
Designed and fabricated micromachined silicon substrates-or supporting and locating optical elements in a miniaturized laser system.
Designed and fabricated components of a pin array-for simultaneously dispensing multiple liquid droplets.
Demonstrated communication with unpowered capacitive sensors-using RF telemetry.
Visually servoed motion control with mm/s velocities and .17 µm repeatability-for microassembly applications.
Depth estimates with a repeatability of 8 µm-using an optical microscopes' depth-from-defocus active vision techniques. This technique allows accurate control motion along the optical axis of an optical system during visually servoed microassembly.
