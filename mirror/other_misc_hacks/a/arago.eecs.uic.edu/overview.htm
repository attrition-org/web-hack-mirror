MFC Overview

The MicroFluidics Center at the University of Illinois at Chicago is being established under the NSF Industry/University Cooperative Research Centers model. Microfluidics in the context of the center is defined as the study of miniature devices which can pump, mix, monitor or control small quantities of liquids or utilize liquids to perform work and motion. Integrated circuitry revolutionized the electronics industry. The vision of the Microfluidics Center is to similarly revolutionize the manufacture of products that manipulate or monitor fluids by applying the scaling and integration techniques of IC microfabrication.
To address the barriers to further development of microfluidics technology, a program of activities which addresses both fundamental scientific and application specific issues is proposed. The exploration of application specific issues is a means to transfer microfluidic technology to the center's industrial partners in particular application areas. The center's application specific research is especially relevant to the future development of miniature chemical analysis systems, micro-hydraulic actuators, automotive sensors, precision fluid dispensing systems, and microfluidic component manufacturers.
Many of the technological barriers faced in the microfluidics field are generic to a diverse range of applications. The issues faced include:
» integration of discrete fluidic elements such as pumps, valves, reservoirs, and flow meters into fluidic circuits
» control of surface tension and avoidance of vapor bubbles in the flow
» development of a diversity of micropump and valve designs as well as drive techniques
» a lack of instrumentation for the characterization of fluid flow
» no packaging or miniature interconnects are available on a scale suitable for making connections between miniature fluidic components
» unclogging trapped small particles in channels
» estimating the manufacturing cost of these type of devices
» development and optimization of new material processes and new material combinations integration of sensors into MEMS systems.
With these issues in mind, the proposed technical focus areas for MFC research projects in microfluidics are as follows:

Packaging
* Packaging for Microfluidic Devices
There are currently no convenient methods for interconnecting miniature fluidic devices. Available methods involve coupling small diameter tubes by hand which is labor intensive. We have an approach to connecting these devices using micro flanges and integrated packaging of the electronic and fluidic elements into one structure.
Integration of Microfluidic Devices with Sensors
In order to make microfluidic pumps and other useful devices, various parameters including flow velocity, pressure, and temperature must be measured. Several miniature microsensors are small enough to be integrated into microfluidic circuits, but there is also a need to develop other devices suitable for integration. In particular, flow sensors which can measure flow velocities in the 1 cm/min range need to be developed.
* Thru-wafer Interconnects
Separating the fluidic components in a different part of the circuit from the circuit's electronic components is necessary for a reliable integrated device. By placing the electronics on one side of the wafer and the fluidic circuit on the other, the seal between the fluidic element and the electronic sensor, actuator or drive circuit is less of an issue.
Microfluidic Cooling Devices in Miniature High Power Devices
Circuit components including high density electronic power devices benefit from integrated fluidic cooling structures. The maximum power density achievable has been demonstrated in excess of 1KW/cm2 by Tuckerman et al..

Systems
* Microhydraulic Power Systems
The ability to distribute power from a single pressure generating pump to distributed actuators and the ability to scale the force into a useful range by the area of the actuator piston are the two key advantages of hydraulic actuators. Larger displacements than piezoelectric and electrostatic (comb drive) actuators can be realized. Components for microhydraulic systems include: pumps to impart energy and pressure to the fluid; tubes for delivering the fluid; valves to control the fluid flow; actuators to convert the fluid pressure to mechanical work; sensors to measure pressure, flow rate, actuator displacement, and temperature; and electronics which use the sensor input to control the pumps, valves and actuators.
* Miniature Chemical Analysis Systems
A range of chemical analyses are needed to properly control process flow and to monitor chemical processes. Many of these standard tests can be automated in miniature chemical analyzers. The development of miniature chemical sensors and biosensors has produced electronic devices for a range of analytes from glucose to specific proteins and hormones. By combining the analytic capability of a chemical separation technique with sensors we can further expand the range of analytes and systems that can be analyzed. The world-wide market for miniature diagnostic biosensors and analysis systems is expected to be $700M by the year 2000.

Fluidic Devices
* Magnetohydrodynamic Micropumps and Solder Dispensing with Microfluidic Devices
The dimensions of electronic circuit boards are shrinking as the push for the manufacture of smaller products grows stronger. The increased use of surface mounted components and hybrid designs have made requirements on precision soldering more exacting. Older dispensing technology is reaching a limit of small droplet size. Liquid metal pumping by magneto hydrodynamics is an attractive approach for dispensing small quantities of solder. This technology is also robust because there are no moving parts in the system.
* Design and modelling of microfluidic components: micropumps and microvalves
Several prototype micropumps have been developed by researchers in Europe. However, there has not been a systematic study of alternative drive techniques to examine trade-offs in the mechanical design for each drive system. Modelling of the coupled fluid dynamics-solid mechanics problem requires finite element modelling.
* Fabrication of subtrates and devices for sequencing by hybridization on microchips
Gel array substrates are photopatterned and oligonucelotide samples are loaded using a robotic liquid dispensing system that contains micromachined liquid handling components. Microfluid chambers are being designed and microfabricated to control the flow of small test solutions over the oligonucleotide arrays.

Characterization and Testing
* Optical Monitoring and Diagnostics for Miniature Fluidic Devices
Currently there are no diagnostic instruments for microfluidic devices or systems. The challenge is to use non-invasive techniques to measure flow velocities, pressure, temperature and perhaps chemical concentration gradients. When transparent substrates are used, optical techniques offer the advantage of a great degree of sophistication and functionality.
* Miniature NMR Instruments
NMR is a standard technique for chemical analysis in the laboratory. Utilizing microfabrication techniques, a miniature NMR instrument is proposed which will be a powerful and portable analytical instrument. This technique also is proposed in a simplified form for flow velocity measurements.
