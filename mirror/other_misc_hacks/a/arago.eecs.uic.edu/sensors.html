Sensors

Biosensors and Microsensors

There is a wealth of microsensor technologies that can be bought to bear on the problem of rising health care costs. A multitude of parameters need to be measured to aide in medical diagnosis, including chemical ion concentrations, protein identification and virus detection, as well as pressure, flow and temperature. A pertinent example is the screening of a blood bank for HIV tainted blood. This technology is also very relevant to food safety and pharmaceutical manufacturing. Microsensors also play an increasingly important role in modern industry. Sensors are used to close the feedback loop in many computer controlled systems. In many cases, the sensor is the limiting feature in the system because of the greater progress of computer technology in recent years. Automobiles utilize a constantly increasing number of sensors to allow monitoring and control of combustion, crankshaft location, tire pressure, acceleration, etc. Microwave ovens employ sensors to monitor organic vapors, temperature and humidity. Clearly, advances in sensor technology are of great benefit by adding value to industrial products.

Novel Enzyme Biosensor

One of the novel sensors currently being investigated at the MAL is an ultra-thin metal film biosensor. The sensors are fabricated by evaporation of a thin film of platinum onto an oxidized silicon wafer. The sensor comprises an ultra-thin metal film which allows for the immobilization of the biological material in a thin layer adjacent to the metal islands. The impedance is measured over a frequency range of 100 Hz to 1 MHz. When the enzyme glucose oxidase is coated on the surface the impedance is sensitive to the glucose level in solution. The impedance decreased and the phase increased in response to glucose solutions in the range 5 to 100 mM. The response time of the sensor was less than 15 seconds. This type of measurement has several advantages:

  • the biological film is coated in a thin layer adjacent to the electrode surface resulting in a rapid response time,
  • measurements are made at a potential of just 10 mV,
  • there is no reference electrode, and
  • it is a generic response mechanism that can be used with antibodies, enzymes, and other proteins that undergo binding reactions.

Manufacturing Process Monitoring

The development of efficient and cost- effective manufacturing techniques can be aided by built-in quality controls and a knowledge of the status of the manufacturing systems in real time. Sensors can be a vital link in obtaining the information required. Recent developments in sensor technology have resulted in a variety of microsensors, manufactured using the sophisticated and economical techniques of microfabrication. Microfabricated sensors have electrical outputs that can be interfaced directly or via telemetry with computers and provide information for process development and characterization, for statistical process control, or to monitor every part. At the MAL, we have developed embedded sensors for monitoring the cure of polymeric materials, strains, and delaminations. The choice of the particular sensor system to develop would be determined by industrial need and the compatibility with the manufacturing process.
