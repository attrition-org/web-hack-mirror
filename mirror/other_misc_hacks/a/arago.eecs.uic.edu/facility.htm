Microfabrication Facilities

The Microfabrication Applications Laboratory (MAL) is a research and development laboratory at the University of Illinois at Chicago. It is dedicated to the application of 'chip making' technology to improve manufacturing methods for electromechanical, mechanical, chemical, optical and multi-functional devices which are currently being produced with less efficient techniques. The Laboratory is located on the University of Illinois Chicago campus, just west of Chicago's Loop. The MAL staff consists of seven experienced researchers who are also faculty members in the University's Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, a full time technical liaison, a technical support staff, and associated faculty in other engineering disciplines. The laboratory consists of a three thousand square foot clean room subdivided into five bays and an equal device characterization area. Over two and a half million dollars of equipment is installed in the UIC clean rooms. Approximately one and a half million dollars of that amount has been used to acquire new equipment at the cutting edge of microfabrication technology. Unlike highly automated industrial production equipment, equipment within the Center is flexible and multipurpose in function. Cleanroom equipment has been carefully chosen to provide strong capability within the thrust areas of microsensors, microstructures, micropumps and optoelectronics. The clean room is divided into several bays to provide work space for various functions including metallization, lithography, wet etching and chemical vapor deposition (CVD).
The lithography bay is equipped with a GCA MANN Model 3600 Pattern Generator and GCA MANN Model 3696 Stepper fitted with a 10X reduction lens. The pattern generator and stepper provide in house mask fabrication which facilitates fast turn around when a design iteration is needed. Standard positive and negative photoresist lithography equipment are available in addition to spray development facilities. Two mask aligners are installed, a Kasper aligner and a Karl Suss MJB3 Infrared aligner for 'front to back' alignment of masks to silicon wafers. This special piece of equipment is especially useful when making sensors, membranes, and three dimensional structures.
The metallization capabilities of the laboratory are diverse. There are a total of four systems: a CVC sputtering station, a cryo-pumped S-gun sputtering station, an ion-pumped ultra high vacuum e-beam evaporator, and a diffusion pumped e-beam evaporator. The CVC sputtering system, has two three inch S-guns, two electron guns, two effusion cells, a heated substrate, and a filamentless ion gun. The e-beam evaporator and second sputtering system are used mainly for catalytic metals, an important component of sensor fabrication. The remaining thermal evaporator is used to deposit gold and aluminum contacts.
The oxidation and diffusion area consists of two, three-stack Thermco furnaces. The LPCVD area consists of a new four-stack furnace made by Process Technology configured for silicon nitride, LTO, polysilicon, and metal CVD. An important feature of the LPCVD system is that variable stoichiometry films may be produced for optical waveguides. The plasma enhanced CVD capabilities are provided by an ElectroTech 310 deposition station under computer control, which can provide films of silicon nitride, silicon dioxide, silicon oxynitride, diamond like diamond structure carbon and silicon carbide.
The wet chemical etching facilities are comprehensive, consisting of an assortment of anisotropic, isotropic and electrochemical etching equipment for semiconductors, metals and dielectrics. Plasma etching includes reactive ion etching and sputter etching in an ElectroTech 340 under computer control with six mass flow controllers to provide a versatile array of gas compositions. A host of 'standard' etching recipes utilized regularly by the semiconductor industry and experimental recipes for non-standard materials will be employed.
The inspection and characterization bay contains Olympus optical microscopes, a BioRad FTIR microscope, a Tencor P1 surface profiler, Gaertner ellipsometer, a TopoMetrix Scanning Tunneling and Atomic Force Microscope, and a Metricon Prism coupler for the measurement of film thickness. The Tencor provides state of the art performance in large area surface topography measurements, with nanometer resolution over scans of several centimeters. This device is extremely valuable in characterizing the geometry of micromachined structures to an accuracy of about 100 Angstroms. The Materials Characterization Laboratory, directed by Professor Michael McNallan, includes sample preparation equipment, several optical microscopes, a Siemens D-5000 X-ray diffractometer, and several electron microscopes. Each electron microscopes actually includes two JEOL scanning electron microscopes (one T-200 and one T-300) both equipped with EDS spectroscopy attachments for elemental analysis, one JEOL 100CX scanning and transmission electron microscope, also equipped with an EDS system, and an Electroscan environmental scanning electron microscope which is capable of imaging samples at elevated temperatures and at pressures as high as 10 Torr.
In addition to extensive University and Departmental computing facilities and PCs used to control individual experiments, the MAL also has a dedicated workstation lab with 4 PCs, a Windows NT server, a Unix server, and 8 UNIX workstations (Sun, HP and SGI). These facilities are used for mask design, device design, and finite element modelling.
