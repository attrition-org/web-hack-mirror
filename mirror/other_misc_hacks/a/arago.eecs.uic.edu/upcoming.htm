MEMS RF Seminar

Date : Tuesday, December 21, 1999
Time : 11:00 a.m.
Location : Room 1000 SEO
Title: Micromachined/MEMS microwave structures and active MMICs
Presenter: John Papapolymerou, ECE Professor
Affiliation: University of Arizona, Tucson
Host: Prof. Feinerman

Abstract:

Monolithic Microwave/Millimeter-wave Integrated Circuits (MMICs) have been widely used in communication and radar systems during the last two decades, due to their advantages over conventional waveguide technology. These advantages include small size and weight, low fabrication cost, improved reliability and reproducibility, and broad band operation. As a result of these characteristics, MMICs are very attractive to use in a variety of applications, such as satellite-to-earth communication links, wireless communication networks, system-on-a-chip architectures, anti-collision radars and remote sensing. However, the performance of high-frequency MMICs residing on a single substrate can be limited by factors such as increased dielectric loss, substrate moding, dispersion, and the non-availability of higher-frequency solid-state oscillator sources that are difficult to fabricate. Techniques that address these issues and circumvent the associated problems have been developed over the past 5 years. For passive structures, the implementation of micromachining as one of these techniques will be presented including examples of patch antennas with enhanced performance, high-Q filters, resonators and duplexers as well as packaging. For active structures, W-band GaAs monolithic multipliers and mixers based on Finite Ground Coplanar line technology will be shown. The presentation will also include future trends in the related areas.

About the speaker:

John Papapolymerou received the Diploma in Electrical Engineering from the National Technical University of Athens (N.T.U.A.), Greece, in 1993, and the M.S.E.E. and Ph.D. degrees from the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, in 1994 and 1999, respectively. Currently, he is an Assistant Professor at the Electrical and Computer Engineering Department of the University of Arizona, Tucson. His research interests include micromachined and MEMS based RF/microwave circuits, wireless chip-to-chip interconnects, W-band monolithic transmit/receive modules and RF packaging.He received the Outstanding Graduate Student Instructional Assistant Award in 1997 by the American Society for Engineering Education (ASEE), University of Michigan chapter.
