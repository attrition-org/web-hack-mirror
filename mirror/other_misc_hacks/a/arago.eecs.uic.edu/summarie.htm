Summaries of present technical programs of MFC faculty

Dr. Davorin Babic(EECS)

Physical principles, development and applications of quantum optoelectronic and microelectronic devices based on nanometric silicon. Theoretical and experimental investigation of their optical, electronic and other relevant properties. Radiative and nonradiative recombination channels in silicon quantum dots are being investigated to maximize quantum efficiency and minimize the effects of nonradiative channels. The role of defects and external electric charge on nonlinear optical and electronic properties of nanometric silicon dots is also being elucidated. Advanced fabrication methods for nanoscale optoelectronics and microelectronics of silicon. Intent is to develop efficient and reliable silicon based light modulators, detectors and other optoelectronic devices whose fabrication is compatible with conventional silicon processing technology. These developments could lead to the integration of optoelectronic communication components with micromechanical, sensing, microfluidic and electronic components on the same chip.

Dr. James Boyd(ME)

The formulation and numerical solution of boundary value problems of materials processing, including thermal residual stresses, reaction, diffusion, and moving boundaries due to phase change, evolution of porosity, and crack growth, including the effects of thermal, mechanical, electrical, magnetic, and chemical fields. Design of "smart" materials and MEMS. Smart materials and MEMS sense their environment and respond accordingly by using coupled fields, such as electro-elastic and magneto-elastic materials. The design method consists of solving a micro-scale boundary value problem and then averaging the solution to obtain the overall properties of the "smart" materials and MEMS. Micro-fluidic devices, including miniature pumps, actuators, valves, manifolds, pipes, flow meters, and fluidic logic elements.

Dr. Sabri Cetinkunt(ME)

Improving the accuracy of ultra-precision machine tools using advanced computer control algorithms. Machining of metal mirrors from soft metals for night-vision cameras, optical systems, copy machines, VCR components, as well as ultra-precision machining of artificial hip-joint replacements. Experiments are conducted on a single point diamond turning machine. Development of self-adapting control algorithms based on a class of artificial neural networks called cerebellar model articulation controller (CMAC). The CMAC based controller shows the promise of learning the friction and backlash characteristics in machine tool drive systems.

Dr. Soyoung Cha(ME)

Optical measurements of macro/micro physical properties of fluids, solids, and particles including velocity, temperature, density, species concentration, heat, stress, deformation, shape, size. Application of optical techniques to in-situ detection, product development, and process control. Optical gross-field nonintrusive visualization and nondestructive testing. Holography, interferometry, tomography, light scattering, spectroscopy, and image processing.

Dr. Rhonda Franklin Drayton(EECS)

Application of micromachining techniques to aid in the development novel high frequency circuit/antenna designs used at microwave and millimeter-wave frequencies. The results thus far include the development of monolithic self-packages that offer high performance and miniaturization to planar circuit designs. This high frequency electronic packaging enhances the circuit performance by providing circuit isolation and environmental protection while offering miniaturization and low cost capability. Additionally, high performance antenna have been developed using silicon that show an increase conventional microstrip antenna bandwidth as well as antenna efficiency. Further extensions include the integration of optical circuit components into microwave circuit design in order to achieve high-speed circuit designs that can be used in communications systems. Micromachining, as a tool, can also be used in developing of diagnostic methods to evaluate circuit parameters.

Dr. Alan Feinerman (EECS)

Application of semiconductor processing and fiber optic technology to precision manufacturing. This hybrid technology has given us the capability to generate three dimensional patterns and will create 0.5 cc and larger objects consisting of metals, semiconductors, polymers, and insulators with micron accuracy. The advantages offered are: the miniaturization of existing analytical instruments, the development of new instruments, and improved control of critical process variables (down to a micron scale from a mm scale). Areas of research include miniaturization of low and medium voltage scanning electron microscopes, linear accelerators and undulators, field emission electron sources, and microelectromechanical devices. Miniature analytical instruments can be brought to previously inaccessible samples. These instruments will be easier to use since each critical component, such as charged particle sources, lenses, deflectors, and detectors will be permanently aligned to the optical axis.

Dr. Gary Friedman (EECS)

Computing surface forces in microfabricated devices. Surface forces are dominant in small devices, andhave not been fully characterized for arbitrarily shaped surfaces. Micromechanical actuators based on liquid conductors. One immediate application for such actuators is in micromechanical liquid solder pumps for deposition of solder on printed circuits. Applications of liquid conductor actuators in positioning devices are also under investigation.

Dr. Peter Hesketh (EECS)

Microfabrication techniques for microsensors and microsystems. Bulk silicon micromachining is currently used for the manufacture of pressure sensors and microaccelerometers. It is anticipated that the use of this technology will grow in non-electronic applications, particularly in manufacturing. Systematic characterization of the micromachining process is being undertaken with single crystal silicon spheres for the fabrication of 3-dimensional microstructures. Applications include high aspect ratio structures for x-ray optics, micropumps, microvalves and microfluidic devices.
Current funded projects include investigation of an immunobiosensor for the detection of a bacterial food toxin. This sensor is based on impedance measurements, making it a less expensive method than those currently available. Rapid progress has been made recently in the development of a micropump for small quantities of fluid (nL - L) with applications in microdialysis. This use of fluidic devices has a broad impact in applications ranging from controlled drug infusion, miniature hydraulic components (light weight but powerful) to manipulation of small objects such a biological cells. A study of the effects of microfabrication on the biocompatibility properties of polymer membranes has also been undertaken. Rapid thermal processing of semiconductors is a technique for processing materials with a reduced thermal budget. Current interests include the application to novel sensor materials.
Previous projects undertaken with industry support have included silicon pressure sensors, embedded sensors for monitoring the state of materials during manufacturing processes, glucose sensors and biosensors.

Dr. Ernesto Indacochea (CME)

Phase transformation and structure stability in materials processing and performance. Emphasis on joining, brazing, soldering and coating of metals and ceramics. Material design and development for bonding dissimilar components, ferrous and nonferrous alloys and oxide and non-oxide ceramics. Process optimization, materials degradation and life extension of structural components. Development of alternative electrolytes and current collectors/separator plates in molten carbonate fuel cells.

Dr. Francis Loth (ME)

Simulation of pulsatile flows (biofluids) and measuremetn of fluid dynamic variables. Experimental techniques include 2-D laser Doppler anemometry, Doppler ultrasound, and 2-D particle tracking to determine velocity and wall shear stress. Numerical simulation of 3-D flows using finite element methods. Investigation of the relationship between the fluid dynamic variables and the development of arterial disease.

Dr. Jordan Maclay (EECS)

Microsensors and microsensor systems. Microfabricated sensors can provide valuable information about the state of a system. They are used for environmental, diagnostic and manufacturing process monitoring. Information retrieved from sensors can be used in a closed loop feedback control system. We have developed specialized microsensors for the meaurement of pressure, temperature and the measurement of carbon monoxiede and other hazardous gases. Environmental monitoring and self cleaning ovens are areas that incorporate our sensor applications. In process control, we have developed disposable microsensors that can be embedded within a polymer, plastic, or composite material and can provide information about the curing process. After curing, the sensors can provide information about stresses or temperature within the material. Specifically we have developed sensors for the measurement of dielectric constant, strain, and temperature that can be embedded in a variety of materials. Information provided by these microsensors can be read out with wires or by telemetry.

Dr. Michael McNallan (CME)

High temperature materials chemistry applied to processing and corrosion of advanced materials. Specific programs include: Corrosion of materials in complex industrial environments. Effects of halogen and alkali halide contamination on high temperature corrosion kinetics. Advanced coatings formed by electrochemical anodization or reactions with halogens or halide vapors. Chemical and microstructural effects on tribology of materials in complex environments. Joining of ceramics and advanced materials. Injection refining of liquid metals. Fabrication of metal and ceramic matrix composites. Corrosion of advanced materials, including rare earth containing high strength permanent magnets, intermetallic compounds, and fiber and particulate reinforced ceramic composites based on silicon oxycarbide or matrices formed by directed oxidation.

Dr. David Naylor (EECS)

Microfabrication technology is being applied to the integration of optics with silicon based microelectronics. Major emphases are: the development of building blocks of integrated optics technology such as fiber to waveguide alignment mechanisms, waveguide fabrication methods; and the design and microfabrication of computer generated binary diffraction elements for efficient optical beam manipulation. Methods of assembling miniature optical and fluidic systems are being developed that involve the precise location and bonding of small components. Micromachining is being used as a method of precisely locating optical elements in miniature laser cavities and optic instruments. Miniature optical probes of small fluid channels are being developed using a combination of waveguide and fluid components. Networked instrument interfaces are being developed to allow multi-user remote access to sophisticated measurement systems.

Dr. Brad Nelson(ME)

Intelligent manufacturing systems, automatic assembly, robotics, controls, MEMS, and computer vision. Recent past work has focused on the assimilation of disparate sensing modalities for the control of intelligent systems. This includes an interdisciplinary research effort in visual servoing, force control, and task representations defined in terms of sensor and environmental models. Applications of these interests to various manufacturing domains, including microassembly of hybrid MEMS, high precision machining, and the manipulation of deformable objects are being pursued.

Dr. Ishwar Puri (ME)

Combustion and environmentally friendly manufacturing processes. Characterization of turbulent chemically reacting flows; fire spread; solid and hazardous waste combustion; fluid dynamics of multiphase chemically reacting flows; pollutant formation; characterization of the extinction of laminar and turbulent flames; and gas-phase synthesis of novel ceramic materials. Design, fabrication and characterization of microcombustors for pollutant minimization.
