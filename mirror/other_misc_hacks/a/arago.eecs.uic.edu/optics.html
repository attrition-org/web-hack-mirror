Luminescence in Silicon

We have recently made several significant breakthroughs in the development of all solid state silicon luminescent devices. Several of our earlier attempts at current injection from deposited electrodes resulted in light output that was sporadic, and unreliable. Recent modifications have lead to more consistent results with light output of the same spectral character as the photoluminescence and over much broader areas of the devices. We are presently investigating the effects of a number of different process variations and device structure modifications to optimize the electrically induced luminescence. We have recently succeeded in producing porous layers of sufficiently good optical quality to perform optical waveguide measurements. This has allowed the measurement of the refractive index of the porous layer as a function of polarization direction (parallel or perpendicular to the wafer surface). These layers are now being incorporated into micromachined silicon structures that optimize the coupling of optical fibers to ridge waveguide structures involving the porous material. This will allow the performance of more sophisticated optical measurements such as absorption spectroscopy for light travelling within the porous layer. The effects of optical or electrical excitation will be studied to investigate the possibility of optical gain in the luminescent silicon layer. We have developed a novel low temperature method for depositing indium doped tin oxide (ITO) onto porous silicon. Most deposition methods require heating in the presence of oxygen to produce ITO films of high transparency and low resistivity. This low temperature method produces highly transparent highly conducting contacts with good adhesion without the high temperature annealing steps that are known to destroy the luminescent behavior of porous silicon. The resulting n-type ITO on p-type porous silicon diodes have been probed electrically using current-voltage (I-V) and capacitance-voltage (C- V) measurements as a function of temperature.

Double-V groove ridge waveguides

A novel waveguide geometry has been developed for fabricating optical ridge waveguide structures on semiconductor substrates. Unlike the usual method of depositing a film and patterning it by etching to produce waveguides with transverse optical confinement the DVRW structure is produced by anisotropically etching the substrate to form two closely spaced grooves prior to depositing the waveguiding film. A ridge waveguide is formed in the region between the grooves without the need to directly etch the waveguide material. There are three main advantages of this method. A wide variety of waveguiding materials may be used including those that may be difficult to etch or pattern. The anisotropic etching of the substrate leads to grooves with extremely smooth side walls. The orientation of the planes exposed by anisotropic etching of 100 oriented silicon wafers is such that 90o waveguide bends are easily fabricated.

Polymeric Waveguides

We have developed a method for the batch fabrication of polymeric optical waveguides for use in multichip modules.
