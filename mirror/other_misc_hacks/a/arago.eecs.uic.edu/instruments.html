Analytical Instruments and 3-Dimensional Fabrication

Miniature Scanning Electron Microscope

A new method has been developed for miniaturizing charged particle instruments by precisely stacking silicon chips with pyrex fibers. This technique will significantly reduce the size of a SEM (scanning electron microscope) column and x-ray detector to less than 2cc. These miniature SEMs will have all critical components permanently aligned to the optical axis and the fabrication methods can produce one to several dozen columns. This small size will allow the SEM to be brought to the sample instead of bringing a small piece of the sample to the SEM. A few of the applications being explored are inspecting an orbiting spacecraft's exterior for damage, monitoring thin film deposition and etching, and locating the STM tip on a substrate. Stacking combines this century's two highest precision technologies, fiber optics and semiconductor processing and allows patterning on the submicron scale in the x, y, and z directions. Fiber optic technology can control a fiber's diameter to +0.1%/km and yields 500micron pyrex fibers with submicron accuracy. Semiconductor processing has submicron capability across a 50-300mm wafer as long as the desired vertical features are limited to 500microns. Stacking extends the vertical range of semiconductor processing by a factor of 100. All of the necessary components for a miniature SEM can be fabricated on four silicon chips. The first die will have a micromachined field emission source, the next three die will accelerate, focus, and deflect the electron beam, and the fourth die will also detect x-rays and back scattered electrons. A second method of fabricating instruments by slicing precision structures is being developed. In this method a silicon wafer with a patterned SiO2 layer is bonded to a pyrex wafer and is anisotropically etched. The etching creates v-grooves in silicon islands on the pyrex wafer. A composite glass structure composed of three types of glass (insoluble- metallizable, insoluble-insulating, and soluble) is then bonded into the v- grooves. The glass structure is cut with a dicing saw and the soluble glass removed. Portions of the insoluble glass are then metallized creating the following miniature horizontal electron optic devices: electrostatic quadrupole, travelling wave tube, and a high voltage electron microscope. X-ray optics A dicing saw has been used to cut through 95% of the thickness of a silicon wafer. These sawn wafers can be bent to a sub-meter radius to focus x-rays after the wafers are etched to remove saw damage.
