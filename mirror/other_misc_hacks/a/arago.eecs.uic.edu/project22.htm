Optimization and Reliability in Anodic Bonding
Pending funding from the Microfluidics Center
Dr. Michael McNallan(PI), Dr. James Boyd (Co-PI) and Dr. R. Baurceanu(Co-PI)

Anodic bonds formed between glass surfaces are required for the assembly of microfluidic devices. Anodic bonds are formed when hydrated glasses are brought into close contact at elevated temperature with the passage of electrical current. Anodic bonds between etched glass and oxidized silicon surfaces permit the formation of closed channels from cavities produced by surface etching in photolithography.
The reliability of this bonding technique is low and the effects of material and process parameters on the bond strength are known only on an empirical and anecdotal basis. If microfluidic devices are to be manufactured on a large scale commercial basis, the anodic bonding process must be reliable and predictable. When the mechanism of the process is adequately understood, it can be optimized to design bonding procedures which can meet these criteria.
The objective of this project is to develop a set of design rules for anodic bonding which can be used to predict the effects of glass chemistry, temperature, and applied voltage on the strength and reliability of anodic bonds. Planned Research includes:

» Designing and constructing experimental facilities for anodic bonding
» Establishing appropriate testing procedures for anodic bonds.
» Producing bonds following a three dimensional test matrix of temperature, voltage and time to establish optimum bonding conditions.
» Performing initial bonding with high purity fused silica. Later the effects of additions to the glass of alkali and alkali earth oxides as well as boron oxide will be addressed, leading up to commercial glass compositions. Bonding of dissimilar materials, particularly glass/Si will be investigated. When the bonding behavior has been sufficiently well characterized for bulk materials, the effects of surface pre-treatments on subsequent bonding will be addressed.

 Project Benefits:
Lack of adequate bonding techniques limits the application of microfluidic devices in a number of applications. Anodic bonds can be expected to have superior strength, biocompatibility, and fatigue resistance in comparison to bonds based on organic adhesives or metal solders. Reliable anodic bonding techniques will permit the development of microfluidic devices which operate at higher pressures, with longer lifetimes and in more contamination sensitive environments than can be considered with current technology. Advances in anodic bonding will expand the microfluidic device market.
