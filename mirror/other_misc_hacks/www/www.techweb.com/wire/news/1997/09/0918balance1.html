 Technology News
(09/18/97; 9:30 a.m. EDT)
By Bruce Caldwell, InformationWeek, and Andy Patrizio, TechWire

Don't look now, but fixing the year 2000 problem is only half the job -- maybe less. Testing the fixes could, well, test your patience, resources, and skill.

AT&T, for example, says it estimates that testing its 2000 fixes will eat up as much as 60 percent of the time and money needed for its total compliance efforts. "We'll test in one fashion or another everything we touch, everything we have that will be impacted, from elevators to application code," says George Brucia, the company's vice president for year 2000 efforts.

Although companies started their year 2000-compliance projects at different times, and some testing will continue after 2000, everyone faces the same finish date. Meeting that deadline will require more coordination among business units, business partners, software and hardware vendors, and entire industries than exists or is even contemplated in many quarters.

Replacing older computer systems with year 2000-
compliant systems won't erase the need to test them for business functionality and year 2000 compliance.
The need to test year 2000 projects has been overshadowed by the need to raise corporate consciousness about date-field problems and muster the means to fix them. But testing the fixes will involve testing on a scale never seen before. Until now, most testing done as part of routine maintenance and enhancement touched maybe 20 percent of all code. But year 2000 tests will need to evaluate 80 percent or more.

Worse, there's little help at hand for making testing easier. Tools will reduce the total year 2000 testing effort by just 2 percent, according to a study performed for General Motors. Also, though replacing older computer systems with new ones that are year 2000-compliant will eliminate some code renovation, it won't erase the need to test the new systems for business functionality and year 2000 compliance.

"Very little of the testing effort can be automated," says Michael Yudkin, vice president of corporate systems and architecture at Chase Manhattan Bank, in New York. Most automation is done in the remediation phase, which accounts for just 10 percent of the total effort, he adds.
