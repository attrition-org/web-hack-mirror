Year 2000: Countdown To Federal Meltdown
(09/16/97; 4:00 p.m. EDT)
By David Braun, TechWeb

Like a clock ticking toward the detonation of a time bomb, most of the world's computer systems are counting down the days to Jan. 1, 2000.

It is the date when many of the globe's computers, and even smart devices that contain computer chips, may be thrown into chaos as they fail to recognize the most vital item of information -- the date.

The year 2000 problem stems from the practice of writing software code with two-digit date fields. To save memory, only two digits were used to distinguish one year from the next.

This works well until "99" changes over to "00" on Jan. 1, 2000. Tests throughout the world have found that computers will react unpredictably to "00." Some will read the year as 1900. Others will be confused and shut down. Still others will throw out gibberish.

In the 1960s, people knew this day of reckoning was coming. Back then, though, the year 2000 seemed a long way off. With fewer than 840 days to go and no solution in sight, the year 2000 problem looks increasingly daunting and expensive to fix.

Millions of bank-account holders, for example, could have their balances electronically wiped out. Payrolls could be thrown into chaos, with millions of workers being paid late or not at all.

Consumers might fall into arrears on their mortgages or credit card payments. ATMs may shut down. Government benefits such as social security could be frozen.

A more perilous scenario is that aircraft that rely on computers for navigation might get lost from the radar screen. Elevators that use embedded programmed chips could malfunction.

There is no "silver bullet" to correct the date fields because software is written in different languages and date fields are not standardized. The only reliable way to ensure software is year 2000-compliant is to check each line of code manually, locate the date fields, change them, and test the corrections.

The checking process is labor-intensive and already putting a strain on human and financial resources. The cost of checking code is starting to rocket.

"Time constraints might force agencies to focus on fixing only the highest-priority computer systems and to shift resources from other projects to work on year 2000 efforts."
 -- Richard M. Nunno
Congressional Research Service
Estimates of what it will cost to check and test all the world's software ranges as high as $600 billion. The Gartner Group, an information technology research firm, says it estimates a cost of $30 billion to correct the problem in federal computers.

The White House recently raised its estimate of fixing the year 2000 glitch in federal computers by $1 billion to $3.8 billion. That figure excludes the cost of testing.

"Time constraints might force agencies to focus on fixing only the highest-priority computer systems and to shift resources from other projects to work on year 2000 efforts," says Richard M. Nunno, a science policy researcher of the Congressional Research Service.
