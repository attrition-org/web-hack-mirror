The Perils of Protecting Privacy Online
Diane E. Levine
Planet IT

Privacy has been in the news a great deal lately.

Michigan's attorney general claims DoubleClick violated Michigan's Consumer Protection Act by tracking computer users without their knowledge and consent.

When the world's largest online services provider, AOL, acquired Time Warner, an entertainment provider with its own vast customer database, some news articles questioned whether user/subscriber privacy would be protected.

The New York Times and Xerox both made headlines in late 1999 by firing numerous employees for misuse and abuse of company e-mail. The companies were monitoring the e-mail and reading the content. Did these companies invade the privacy and the legal rights of employees by their actions? No. In both cases the companies had e-mail policies that strictly forbid misusing the e-mail application, so the firings couldn't be contested.

More onerous are the possibilities, feared by privacy advocates, that AOL, DoubleClick, and other organizations will acquire databases of such numbers and content that computer users will have virtually no privacy regarding anything in their lives. When DoubleClick, an Internet advertising company, acquired Abacus Direct, a print advertising company, it caused consternation among some privacy advocates about melding people's online behavior with their real-world identities.

It's still too early to tell what will happen to AOL and Time Warner customers. But DoubleClick, despite protestations, is under investigation by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), the New York attorney general's office, and the Michigan attorney general's office for its database profiling initiative. According to all three agencies, DoubleClick's business practices are being investigated to determine whether consumer privacy violations occurred.
