Developments in Antivirus Software

Network Computing

Shortly before the end of 1999, USA Today reported that 30,000 computer viruses were ready to launch on or around the new year. Although this estimate proved rather inflated, we can count on several new viruses, trojans and other malicious code to usher in the new millennium.

We discussed attacking the virus threat in our last Buyer's Guide to antivirus technology (www.networkcomputing.com/914/914buyers2.html). Since then, virus developers have advanced their infection techniques, and more payloads are leaving system backdoors or stealing data and passwords. Fortunately, today's antivirus features, such as improved heuristic detection, will find new polymorphic strains before they're cataloged. And plug-in technology for your browser or e-mail is improving first-line defenses.

As antivirus programs are expected to do more, they're evolving into an integral part of our network security perimeter. They offer protection from inflows of malicious code and for outflows of sensitive data. Trend Micro's InterScan VirusWall, for instance, lets you tag outgoing e-mail with disclaimer statements and prevent spam mail. Many solutions bundle antivirus products that cover the desktop, servers and Internet gateways. Combating the problem through centralized management and response allows for a better defense with less overhead. Both Symantec Corp.'s Norton Anti-Virus Enterprise Solution 4.0 and Network Associates' Total Virus Defense Suite 2.5 offer administration from a central console. Expect increasing integration as vendors develop product lines that function under a distributed environment with centralized administration. Our updated Buyer's Guide to antivirus software can be found at www.networkcomputing.com/1105/1105buyers.html.
