Scary Propositions For The Internet
Tim Wilson
InternetWeek

Eight years ago, Bill Clinton and Al Gore put a new plank in the presidential campaign platform: technology. Clinton-and particularly Gore, who wrote one of the defining books on the Information Highway-promised to bolster the development of new technology while minimizing government restrictions on it.

How things have changed. The encryption fiasco-in which the executive branch attempted to mandate a trapdoor for law-enforcement agencies while placing severe restrictions on technology export-is more illustrative of the Clinton approach to technology than anything in Gore's book.

Earlier this month, White House officials again flouted the technology industry's wishes by recommending new restrictions on Internet anonymity. In testimony before a Congressional panel, deputy attorney general Eric Holder suggested that ISPs should stop the practice of offering anonymous e-mail accounts.

Holder's comments were offered as part of a Congressional fact-gathering effort designed to address last month's hacker attacks, which brought down several big Web sites. In that context, they are well-intentioned. But they also show a surprisingly knee-jerk willingness to take away the right to anonymity on the Internet.

The Congressional panel also included testimony from Microsoft and Cisco executives, both of whom stood firmly against new laws curbing Internet anonymity. But the views of top-level legislators on Internet regulation continue to be alarmingly narrow.

Wasn't the Clinton-Gore administration supposed to change that?

Ghosts In The Machine. Author Stephen King, whose "Green Mile" series brought back the serial novel, has now moved into cyberspace. Last week, King published his latest work, "Riding the Bullet," as an e-book that will not be available in print.

For $2.50, readers can purchase the book from a variety of online sites and download it in a format readable by Adobe Acrobat. Those who like the book will be able to e-mail a sample to their friends, along with a link that enables them to buy the book for themselves.

As an Internet technology observer, I applaud King's efforts. The unusual distribution approach will open a number of sales avenues for e-commerce sites-and will put new onus on e-businesses to provide fresh, entertaining content on their sites to gain return business.

As an avid reader and King fan, however, I can't help but be disappointed. Reading a good book is one of my favorite ways to get away from the computer. It doesn't seem fair that I will now have to interrupt Chapter 5 to delete another e-mail promising me enormous wealth by starting a business on the Internet. And what will my wife think when I bring my laptop to bed?

Excuse me while I go buy some toner for my laser printer.
