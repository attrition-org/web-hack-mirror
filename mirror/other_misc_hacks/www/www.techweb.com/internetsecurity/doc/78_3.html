More Trends

I've been talking about digital certificates for a few years as well. Originally linked with the public key infrastructure of e-mail applications, these certificates are beginning to see a broader use. As digital certificates gain momentum, we'll find various vendors teaming their security offerings with certificates to provide an additional layer of security. These vendors will include biometrics vendors with different types of biometric authentication, software vendors that use certificates to validate that their applications haven't been tampered with and that the data is secure -- even hardware vendors that want to attest to the fact that their hardware has remained untouched by unauthorized personnel. Linux vendors at Comdex anticipate using digital certificates with their products to guarantee authenticity of users and application content.

Finally, the relaxation of U.S. restrictions on encryption products and their export is creating a rush by vendors to incorporate encryption products in foreign applications and devices, as well as in domestic ones slated for export. Of course, since the retirement of the Data Encryption Standard, a search is still being conducted for the Advanced Encryption Standard. Look for more universality of encryption products as they become commercially adopted and incorporated by vendors globally. But different types of encryption will continue to exist and be marketed. This will be good news for businesses that already have invested large amounts in specific technology, are satisfied, and don't want to switch to another technology.

I think these five security trends will continue to grow in the new year, adjusting and adapting as technology continues to change rapidly. Judging by experience, there is a need for all types of security products, and each probably will coexist, be layered together, and support other types, as needed, to meet the security needs of individual organizations.
