How To Beat Online Fraud
Diane E. Levine
Planet IT

From various news reports:

-More than six million Internet consumers in the United States have been the victims of credit card fraud, representing 7% of online consumers in the United States -- from the National Consumer League.

-Last October, 44 people were arrested and charged with perpetrating securities fraud on the Internet. The Securities and Exchange Commission receives about 300 e-mail complaints a day about fraud, compared to 15 a day three years ago -- from ZDNet.

Here are some tips to help reduce your chances of being scammed when purchasing goods online. - The charge-back rate for Internet merchants (charges reversed to them when credit cards turn out to be fraudulent) is 15%, versus 1% at brick-and-mortar points of sale. Charge-backs grow to 30% when online merchants sell digital products.

An offer came to me via e-mail and immediately seemed too good to be true. A 15-inch monitor, refurbished non-Pentium desktop computer with keyboard, mouse, speakers, and sound card could be mine for $50. Furthermore, the $50 was only for UPS shipping costs. The computer itself wouldn't cost me anything. It was absolutely free! What a deal! And, just before Christmas!

If you believe this offer and are interested in taking advantage of it, I have a bridge in Brooklyn you might like to buy. Since the offer seemed too good to be true, I telephoned a friend who is a U.S. Postal Inspector. Investigating the address where buyers were to send the shipping fee, the Postal Service found a mail drop being used to collect fees without the benefit of actually supplying computers to victims.
