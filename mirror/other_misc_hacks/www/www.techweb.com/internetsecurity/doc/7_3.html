NOT ALL POWER PROTECTION IS CREATED EQUAL (THANKFULLY)

Now that we've stoked your paranoia enough to convince you to surge protect your remote/branch office, you're probably asking, "what kind of unit should I get?" You don't get exactly what your coworkers have over at the main office. Power needs in a small office are extremely different from those in a larger setting. We asked Rich Feldhaus, a product specialist at Tripp Lite, about the potential power problems in a small office environment compared to those in a large office.

"Large offices and corporate work environments tend to be 'wired' for technology, in terms of both network connections and electrical systems," he says. In contrast, many small offices -- especially home offices -- are in buildings that may not be wired with today's technology in mind. "Building circuits may dictate that sensitive computer equipment share power with unusual household items such as air conditioners, kitchen appliances, forced-air fan motors, and other noisy electrical items. Over time, these everyday surges can lead to damage or performance problems for computers and business equipment."

This doesn't mean that you have to sacrifice reliability, though. "Higher-end surge protectors offer similar protection compared with models oriented toward corporate use, with a few exceptions," Feldhaus says. "SOHO-oriented surge products tend to have more outlets to cover the additional phone, fax, and data equipment on a typical small office desktop, as opposed to the centrally located telecom, fax, and networking equipment in a corporate environment." He adds, "Small office suppression products usually require telephone line surge suppression to protect modems, fax machines, and other desktop items with a remote data connection, as opposed to large work environments, where desktop phones and network lines run to each desktop from centralized voice and data hardware.
