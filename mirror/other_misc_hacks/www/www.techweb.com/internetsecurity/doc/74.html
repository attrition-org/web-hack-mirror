Lax Corporate Policies -- Security Shortchanged
Rutrell Yasin
InternetWeek

Many companies are taking a surprisingly laid-back approach to IT security amid the recent escalation in cyber assaults.

A large number of businesses fail to update and consistently review their security policies, which determine the business assets they need to protect as well as the processes and technologies they must implement to properly secure enterprise networks. What's more, many companies don't have an IT security staff and spend relatively few dollars to protect their information assets, according to Network Computing magazine's new survey of 573 IT and security managers.

But it's the lack of an updated, comprehensive security policy that puts companies at risk. Most companies have some type of security practice in place, whether it's assigning user passwords for network access or deploying firewalls to scrutinize the flow of traffic coming in and out of a corporate network via Internet connections. But they often fall short in keeping security policies and practices current with a dynamically changing business and technical scene. This shortfall is especially noteworthy given last year's Computer Security Institute and FBI survey of 521 security professionals: 57 percent reported their Internet connections are a frequent point of attack.

"It's one thing to write a security policy; it's another thing to set in place the schedule to audit against that policy," said Brent Tuttle, director of Internet operations at eCampus.com, an online seller of textbooks.

The survey showed that 73 percent of the companies have a formal policy, but one-third said those policies aren't up-to-date, and one-fourth said they're not effective. About 39 percent said they didn't review policies often or did not have a formal review process.
