A Common Practice Now Under Scrutiny

For those of us concerned about the privacy issue, it should be noted that AOL and DoubleClick aren't the only ones doing data profiling. Everybody who has a PC and/or is using the Internet, is being tagged and profiled. Microsoft software that users readily install on their PCs contains Microsoft's Registration Wizard (RegWiz) that can be used by any Web site to read a user's Hardware ID (HWID) and Microsoft ID (MSID). When a user registers with Microsoft, the RegWiz transmits information on other applications the user has installed on a PC. Additionally, Windows 98 generates a fingerprint known as a Globally Unique Identifier (GUID) that it plants within electronic documents. This GUID helps Microsoft trace authors' identities. The extent of fingerprinting is not known at this time.

Microsoft Internet Explorer 5 and Netscape Navigator also track movements across the Internet. A feature in Microsoft Explorer 5 alerts the Web site when a user bookmarks a page. Although this feature can't be turned off, using a proxy can block it. Proxies block requests for universal software locators (URLs) and delete unauthorized cookies and other identifying header information exchanged between Web servers and browsers. Meanwhile, using Netscape Navigator allows the Smart Browsing/What's Related feature to collect massive amounts of information related to an individual's browsing habits.

Hostile applets can also record URLs as well as information entered on forms, for retrieval by rogue sites at a later time. This information is pretty revealing since it identifies users, user preferences, and user activities on the Web. Marketers regularly sell this information, and it can also be used to generate junk mail solicitations, forge e-mail messages, impersonate users on the Web, and illegally use credit card numbers.

We've seen, through the recent Microsoft antitrust trial, that e-mail is not gone once deleted. When you delete a file it actually remains on the computer until you write or copy something onto that exact space. Special software can recover these ghosts, and businesses have the legal right to use this software to recover and read messages on any employee's machine. Also, we are guilty of giving away information we may not want marketers and others to know. Who hasn't seen a contest with a great prize that you can enter by filling out a form with personal information? Some of these contests offer great prizes, but in order to enter you must exchange information on your location, gender, preference, income, family, and so on. This is also true when you subscribe to some of the free Webzines. Of course, you can eliminate the problem from this source by not subscribing, but many of us find valuable information in technical, travel, financial, and lifestyle newsletters.

Where do we go from here? The Internet has grown so quickly legislation hasn't kept pace. The rights of computer users are still pretty much in a “gray area.” It's interesting to note the U.S. Constitution does not guarantee a right to privacy, and while the Fourth Amendment protects U.S. citizens against unreasonable search and seizure by the government, that's the extent of coverage and protection given to an average PC user.

The issue of privacy won't go away. We can't continually complain about it without taking action. As the Internet grows, so does the user population. It's up to us to make decisions and laws to protect the population from being preyed upon by unscrupulous individuals and companies that surreptitiously collect or sell information about us without our knowledge or consent.
