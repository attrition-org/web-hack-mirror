Standards Issues, High Cost Plague Acceptance

Vendors realize the enormous commercial potential and the need to move along quickly in PKI product development. Exasperated by the slow pace of the Internet Engineering Task Force, vendors recently formed the PKI Forum. The group's founding members (Baltimore Technologies, Entrust Technologies, IBM, Microsoft, RSA Security, VeriSign and others) intend to focus on commercial business issues. Some of these issues, such as certificate revocation, can wind up being a real problem for interoperability of products. Despite the hype, publicity and continuing product development, I find PKI an expensive security solution. It's also frequently cumbersome because of the lack of interoperability and the amount of administration and coordination required.

There's a real emphasis among PKI Forum members to make their products compatible and interoperable. That has come about primarily because even with the great number of PKI vendors, there are problems making their products work together.

For instance, Shym Technology has a large number of users of its PKEnable tool kit. This product is popular despite the existence of larger companies, such as Entrust Technologies, that also offer tool kits. Entrust even offers its tool kit for free. So why the success of a small firm like Shym? The answer is the flexibility of its tool kit. Shym's tool kit works well with a number of certificate authorities, while the Entrust tool kit basically works with the Entrust Certificate Authority alone. That's exactly why companies like Entrust Technologies became part of the PKI Forum. They hope to increase the speed with which solutions for coexistence and interoperability are found and introduced into the business world.

Part of the renewed cooperation among vendors is being spurred by increased user demand. Many projects that have been in development for several years are now about to go "live." The largest project I know of is the Healthcare Internet Interoperability Security Pilot. Supporting more than 500,000 health-care users at hospitals, government agencies and insurance companies, the PKI project runs a variety of public key methods. In what is a ground-breaking move, all the certificate authorities that are involved in the project are sharing policies. Still lacking a root authority, the health-care PKI isn't ready to go live just yet.

I applaud the efforts of vendors to develop interoperability mechanisms and techniques, but I will have to see and test it to believe it. I expect 2000 to be an important year for the introduction of revolutionary technology that will simplify the administration of PKI and permit products from different vendors to work seamlessly together.
