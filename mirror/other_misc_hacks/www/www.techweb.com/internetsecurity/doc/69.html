Vendors Lock Up Smaller Businesses
Aisha M. Williams with Bob Wallace and George V. Hulme
InformationWeek

While network security is a pressing issue for all companies, small and midsize businesses can be especially vulnerable to hacker attacks and other security breaches. Vendors are beginning to recognize the sales opportunities for security products to these businesses and are offering technologies and services using pricing structures designed to appeal to them.

Security investments usually aren't among the top budget items of small and midsize businesses. "Part of the blame for that can be laid on the vendors," says Jason Wright, an analyst at Frost & Sullivan, who released a research report on that issue last week. "Vendors need to explain to the small-business owner why security is important."

By month's end, BellSouth Corp. plans to make available a range of network security offerings to small and midsize businesses for a monthly subscription fee. The regional telecommunication company's services will include intrusion detection and response, anti-virus filtering, and Web-site blocking. Prices will range from $400 to $3,000 per month for a 50-user network.

In April, NetGuard USA Inc. will begin shipping its GuardianPro eNT firewall system, priced starting at $3,000 for a 25-user license. Late last year, 3Com Corp. introduced the SonicWall firewall for small companies; prices start at $700.

Smaller companies typically use always-on Internet connections, such as digital subscriber line and cable-modem lines, that are affordable but more susceptible to illegal entry. "You are connected to the Internet 24 hours a day. But if you're not implementing a security policy, it's akin to leaving your front door unlocked," says Andrew Moloney, director of entry-level systems in 3Com's network systems business unit. What's more, smaller companies often don't have a large and experienced IT staff that can assess risks and implement security measures.

"There's a whole class of small and midsize companies that haven't accepted responsibility for protecting their networks," says Steve Hunt, a senior analyst at Giga Group. Christopher Keller, co-founder of fledgling E-commerce portal SalesGate.com, learned the hard way that close scrutiny and management of networks is essential: Hackers broke into the company's site last month. Keller blamed the security hole on miscommunication among business managers, systems integrators, and systems administrators, and what he claims was a flaw in Microsoft's E-commerce Web server software.

Keller says the experience has prompted SalesGate.com to beef up security in a hurry. "If you have never gotten a speeding ticket," says Keller, "you're not going to buy a radar detector."

security tips for small businesses

-Encourage employees to choose uncommon passwords that have at least eight characters and can't be found in a dictionary; require new passwords every 90 days

-Stay current on patches and updates for virus protection, Web-server, and security software

-Educate employees about the dangers of opening E-mail attachments from unknown sources; these attachments may contain corruptive software code

-Install a complete security package that includes firewalls, intrusion detection, and policy management

-Provide telecommuters with a secure, centrally managed server for remote traffic
