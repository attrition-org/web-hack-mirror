The Social Studies Educator

Geography Sources

National Geographic Society
http://www.nationalgeographic.com/main.html
This is the Web site of the National Geographic Society, and as you would expect, it is full of great features and wonderful photographs. One feature of special interest is "Glad You Asked," where you can have your geography questions answered by experts. Buy here you'll also find special tie-ins to the Society's magazine articles, explorations, games, activities, a great interactive atlas, and much more.

 

The View From Space: Microsoft Terra Server
http://terraserver.microsoft.com/
The new Microsoft Terra Server contains over a terabyte of satellite and aerial image data depicting our planet, some at a resolution of one meter per pixel. At this time only a small fraction of the globe is represented in the database so you won't necessarily be able to zoom in on your grandpa's farm in south Alabama and count the chickens. The site also allows you to buy images, either as downloadable files or hardcopy photo prints.

 

VolcanoWorld
http://volcano.und.nodak.edu/
This collection of great resources includes: "ask a volcanologist," data on and satellite photos of recent eruptions, images of volcanoes, and lesson plan ideas. The site is supported by NASA's Public Use of Earth and Space Science Data Over The Internet Program and hosted at the University of North Dakota.

 

Ocean Planet: An Online Exhibition from the Electronic Smithsonian
http://seawifs.gsfc.nasa.gov/ocean_planet_preview.html
This compelling resource, designed to "promote celebration, understanding and conservation of the world's oceans," features a variety of excellent multimedia tours (you can even custom-design your own!) as well as complementary educational materials. Ocean Planet is an online companion to the new traveling exhibit of the same name created by the Smithsonian Institution's National Museum of Natural History.

 

The Weather Channel HomePage
http://www.weather.com/twc/homepage.twc
Visit The Weather Channel's website and get your weather-related questions answered by a Weather Channel meteorologist. Every day, a featured meteorologist chooses the most interesting questions submitted and posts them with the detailed answers.

 

The Weather Unit
http://faldo.atmos.uiuc.edu/WEATHER/weather.html
The Weather Unit is an excellent collection of weather-related lessons for every subject area as well as ideas for class field trips and classroom props. The Weather Unit, designed for grades 2-4, is the first component of a planned archive of K-12 thematic units submitted to the public domain. The archive is administered by Dee A. Chapman at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, where the site is hosted.

 

The Daily Planet
http://www.atmos.uiuc.edu
The Daily Planet contains an electronic textbook of multimedia instructional modules designed around different topics in meteorology, such as pressure, wind, and clouds. It also features pointers to up-to-date climate data, satellite images, surface maps, and more. This useful resource is provided by the Department of Atmospheric Sciences at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign.

 

WeatherNet and the Weather Underground
http://cirrus.sprl.umich.edu/wxnet/
WeatherNet, created by Michael MacDonald, provides a superb collection of links to weather data, graphics, movies, shareware, live camera views, and more! Be sure to check out the link to the Weather Underground page for information about its collection of K-12 classroom activities and projects designed to support earth sciences education. Both of these fantastic services are hosted by the University of Michigan Department of Atmospheric, Oceanic, and Space Sciences.

 

INTELLiCast Weather Service
http://www.intellicast.com/
INTELLiCast provides one of the most colorful and user-friendly sources of current weather information, images, and forecasts on the World Wide Web. (It's a lot like a TV weather report.) The service, created by WSI Corp, covers national and international weather as well as the weather in several major U.S. cities. Much of the information is updated daily.

 

Mapmaker, Mapmaker, Make Me a Map
http://loki.ur.utk.edu/ut2kids/maps/map.html
Mapmaker, Mapmaker, Make Me a Map is an engaging unit on map reading with an introduction to key terms and concepts in geography. It is designed for Middle School or High School age students. This lesson appears in Science Bytes, an online publication of the University of Tennessee.

 

The Galaxy Page
http://www.seds.org/other.html
The Galaxy Page, maintained by the University of Arizona chapter of SEDS (Students for the Exploration and Development of Space), hosts a rich, well-organized collection of links to space-related sites. Resources include virtual tours (we recommend The Nine Planets tour of the solar system), graphics, and information categorized under The Solar System, Astronomy, Aerospace, and Visions of the Future. Whether you're looking for information about past comets or plans for future starships, this is the place to start.

 

Passport to Knowledge Projects
http://quest.arc.nasa.gov/interactive.html
NASA's K-12 Internet Initiative has developed a series of online projects to connect students with working professionals to share in authentic scientific and engineering pursuits such as explorations of the Antarctic or outer space. The Passport to Knowledge Project site contains archives of past projects, including field journals, pictures, classroom activities, bibliographies, related links and much more, as well as information on upcoming projects.

 

City.Net
http://www.city.net/
City.Net is a comprehensive collection of links to information on cities, regions, and countries around the globe. It includes many links to maps, language resources, elementary and secondary school listings, community networks, entries from the CIA World Factbook, travel information, and other community resources created by thousands of enthusiasts, small businesses, and governments. The entries are uneven in quality -- some contain great guides, interesting photos, and virtual tours, while other are more bare bones. Nevertheless, the site provides a way to "visit" communities around the world.

 

The Electronic Volcano
http://www.dartmouth.edu/pages/rox/volcanoes/elecvolc.html
The Electronic Volcano is "a window into the world of information on active volcanoes." It ties together a wide variety of volcano-related Internet resources, including maps of active volcanoes, photo and radar images of volcanoes and eruptions, articles on active volcanoes, and several bibliographies of journal articles and abstracts of interest only to the serious volcanologist. The introductory material for this site can be read in English, Spanish, German, Italian, French, and Russian.

 

Maps
http://acorn.educ.nottingham.ac.uk/ShellCent/maps/
Maps is a useful resource on map design and map reading created by the UK Social Science Research Council. This site hosts several excellent graphics that would be great for any class on map reading. Note: The site uses high school level language.

 

Worcester's Weather Web
http://nis.accel.worc.k12.ma.us/WWW/Projects/WeatherWeb/weather.htm
Worcester's Weather Web is a classroom project created by middle school students at the Accelerated Learning Laboratory, a magnet school in Worcester, Massachusetts. The Weather Web contains information and photos the students have collected on a variety of different weather phenomena - from acid rain to typhoons - as well as a collection of weather-related links and more. An interesting example of how students are using the WWW in project-based learning.

 

Earthquake Information
http://www.civeng.carleton.ca/cgi-bin/quakes
This amazing near-real-time earthquake bulletin shows the precise time, location, and magnitude of the most recent twenty earthquakes in the world. A clickable map allows users see location of an earthquake's epicenter displayed on a world map at varying resolutions. An excellent resource for current information. Earthquake Information is provided by the National Earthquake Information Service (NEIS) of the U. S. Geological Survey as part of a cooperative project of the Council of the National Seismic System. The site is made available on the WWW by Neal M. Holtz and the Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering at Carleton University, Ottawa.

 

How Far Is It?
http://www.indo.com/distance/
This service, provided by Bali Online, uses the University of Michigan Geographic Name Server to find the latitude and longitude of two locations, and then calculates the straight-line distance between them and their geographic coordinates. A map showing both locations, using the Xerox PARC Map Server, is also provided. For locations within the United States, elevations are also included.

 

What Do Maps Show?
http://info.er.usgs.gov/education/teacher/what-do-maps-show/index.htm
The U.S. Geological Survey has provided this online teaching package (appropriate for upper elementary and junior high school classes) on the understanding and use of maps. The site contains four 30-minute lesson plans, including maps and reproducible activity sheets. This site also contains links to other USGS materials suitable for elementary school.

 

The Xerox PARC Map Viewer
http://pubweb.parc.xerox.com/map
The Xerox PARC Map Viewer creates custom maps of the U.S. and the world based on user-supplied variables. Each map image is created on demand from a geographic database and the user can "zoom in" to view specific map regions with great precision. This resource is maintained by the Xerox Palo Alto Research Center.

 

The U.S. Geological Survey Home Page
http://info.er.usgs.gov/
The U.S. Geological Survey Home Page hosts a large number of educational resources for teachers and students interested in cartography, geography, and related subjects, as well as a variety of fact sheets, links to related educational sites, and more. You can also order USGS materials from this site. It's well worth a look.

 

ICES Disaster Resources for Educators
http://www.ag.uiuc.edu/~disaster/resource.html
The ICES Disaster Resources Web Site provides several educational packages on disaster preparedness and recovery, such as Children, Stress, and Natural Disasters: A Resource Guide for Teachers. This site also contains links to information from state and federal agencies, educational institutions, relief organizations, and information networks.

 

Bad Meteorology Page
http://www.ems.psu.edu/~fraser/BadMeteorology.html
Did you know that raindrops aren't really shaped like teardrops? (They actually look more like hamburger buns!) Meteorology professor Alistair Fraser exposes this and other popular meterological fallacies on this highly informative and readable site. This site also has links to other Internet resources that attempt to sensitize teachers and students to examples of the science myths.

 

Project GeoSim
http://geosim.cs.vt.edu/
Project GeoSim is a joint research project of the Departments of Computer Science and Geography at Virginia Tech to create educational modules for introductory geography courses. Each module includes a tutorial and simulation software to carry out lab exercises. Module topics include a geography quiz, population growth and migration, characteristics of U.S. counties and states, and more. GeoSim modules are free at this site and run on several different computer operating systems.

 

Color Landform Atlas of the United States
http://fermi.jhuapl.edu/states/states.html
The Color Landform Atlas of the United States contains topographical and political maps to the county level for each of the fifty states. Each state link brings up a menu of links to maps and other online information about the state.

 

Perry-Castaneda Library Map Collection
http://www.lib.utexas.edu/Libs/PCL/Map_collection/Map_collection.html
The Perry-Castaneda Library Map Collection at the University of Texas at Austin holds more than 230,000 maps, many of which have been scanned and made available at this site. This extensive online collection includes hundreds of high quality, copyright-free maps of nations and cities, as well as many historical maps. A special "Electronic Maps of Current Interest" section highlights maps of places in the news. This site also hosts a variety of links to other map-related sites.

 

UT-LANIC
http://lanic.utexas.edu:80/
University of Texas -- Latin American Network Information Center , UT-LANIC, available in both English and Spanish, provides country and subject directories, links to online libraries, searchable indices, and many other resources about the region of Latin America. Information provided for each country includes: economic development, media, online services, general statistics, and tourism, among many others.

 

CoVis Geosciences
http://typhoon.covis.nwu.edu:80/Geosciences/index.html
The CoVis (Collaborative Visualization) Project at Northwestern University involves students in inquiry-based work groups that use state-of-the-art visualization techniques to study atmospheric and environmental phenomena. The CoVis Web site hosts a generous array of online lessons, data visualization tools, explanations of geoscience phenomena, and other curriculum materials. Teachers will especially appreciate the CoVis Teachers Lounge which provides a newsletter, academic calendar, and other useful resources for teachers interested in joining the CoVis community.

 

The Chesapeake Bay Program Homepage
http://www.epa.gov/r3chespk/
This site highlights the activities of the Chesapeake Bay Program, a federal and tri-state effort geared toward restoring the Bay's aquatic life and wildlife. The site contains environmental and scientific data on the Bay and its rivers, information on the efforts taken to reduce pollution, and tips on how citizens can assist in the clean-up efforts.

 

Nature Photographs
http://www.fys.uio.no/~trondre/bilder/bilde-ikoner/scenery
Compiled by Trond Reitan, a doctoral candidate in theoretical physics at the University of Oslo, this is a nice collection of nature photographs, including the Grand Canyon, Mount Everest, the Alps, castles, Monument Valley, and many more of the world's beautiful places.

 

Flags of All Countries
http://www.wave.net/upg/immigration/flags.html
Flags of All Countries is well-organized, up-to-date collection of flags of the world, from Afghanistan to Zimbabwe. The flags are presented in an alphabetized list for easy searching.
