November 3, 1998
Information Processing -
A Keyword in the Framework of Countering Terrorism

Eric Herren,
ICT consultant

Preface
In the world of cyberspace, information has become one of our most vital interests. Information is available in all possible varieties of quantity and quality. We are living in the “age of information.”

In general we can say that information has something to do with sending and receiving signals. This article will focus on the process of receiving signals in connection with the “time” factor—in other words the need to process information quickly and react appropriately.

If time is not something that one has to worry about, incoming signals can be easily identified, selected and forwarded to further processing. Without the pressure of time the focus on information can be wide open, and information that cannot be processed immediately will be collected and analyzed according to ones capacity.

In terms of classical military and political intelligence, the interrelationship between time and information processing is well known and is put into practice in every possible phase.

In connection with counter terrorism, time becomes one of the primary issues. The lives of people depend on the real-time processing and the use to which one puts the available information on all levels of operation. Decisions are made and actions are initiated only after the successful processing of information—from the analyst in the office to the assault team on the front line.

During a counter-terrorist operation, time management becomes more and more important the closer the assault force comes to the terrorist. The conception of the next step becomes identical with the next step itself. The “time” factor is almost zero.

Thinking: arranging actions!
“Thinking proceeds from doing. Thinking begins
when the foundation for doing is put at risk or has to be corrected!”

A human being is constantly using his or her resources to unconsciously attain a goal. The mechanisms of behavior are often totally involuntary. In our daily life we can make the distinction between what we are doing and what we are thinking. We travel to work without needing to give any thought to how to climb the stairs; we just do it, perhaps thinking all the while about the upcoming meeting.

Thinking starts when the stairs are covered with obstacles; it starts with attentiveness, the consciousness of acting. Now suddenly new information can influence our behavior.

Training of Counter-Terrorist Units
Professional training covers the range of technical and tactical aspects of a situation. Many different techniques are employed in training the physical component of the soldier. Most of the “combat actions” become automatic movements. Such “automatization” only makes sense where it can be integrated into a higher task in the context of an active process.

Purposeful behavior means to splitting up a complex action into different fractional actions. Many of these fractional actions are so called automatisms.

Automatisms are situated somewhere between intentional actions and simple reflexes. The consciousness controls the automatisms “remotely” and inserts them if necessary into more complicated interrelations.

In the course of regular training, fighters simulate all kinds of situations and scenarios. A lot of training is done in a “closed circle.” The locations are well known and one approaches the facility with the consciousness of being in a “training area.” Every scenario creates a new pattern of inter-linked fractional actions.

Reality
Usually the “real incident” includes the element of surprise—something new and unforeseen. The fighter is forced to curtail existing automatic actions and react according to the “new” information. He must create a new pattern of acting, depending on his skill and other abilities.

The outcome of the new situation is dependent mainly on the correct assessment of the latest information. A positive assessment will lead to a positive psychological reaction. Doubts or negative assessments will create fear and lead to a negative psychological reaction. Simulations demonstrate that in the stress-fear relation the important factor is not so much the degree of stress, but the reactions to it.

One of the most important capabilities with regard to a positive assessment is the ability to process information. One has to be able to detect the new information as such, as soon as possible. Attentiveness even during an extended period of time is part of this skill. Perception abilities ease the process. The incoming information has to be selected according to specific criteria. The new information can lead either to analytic or reactive behavior.

The significance of the reactive behavior is the focus. Thinking and doing become one. In extreme situations one cannot allow ones concentration to drift away to “what will happen?” If the time factor is close to zero, every additional thought is an obstacle to the best possible reaction. For a good reaction it is important to focus on the moment. Most panic reactions take place when one looses the moment and attempts to force an outcome. If one can stay “within the moment” there is usually sufficient room for a proper reaction.

The nervous system is not capable of continuous processing of incoming sensory information; the vast majority will simply be filtered out! The role of nervous system is to identify which incoming signals require processing. The capacity for concentration is the leading factor in receiving information. Once our saturation point is reached with regard to concentration, nothing more will be received.

The role of the brain is the identification of the incoming stimulation and the actual decision making process—a process that depends on the fighter’s abilities—which will lead to a particular physical action.

For successful information processing, every forwarded stimulus needs a referent. The input has to be connected to stored datum that supports the decision and finally results in a particular action.

Combined Training
Without going too deeply into psychological and physiological details, we can highlight the following needs with regard to new forms of training for highly professional units.

  • Knowledge (taught)

    • In order to accommodate forwarded information all types of referents must be created. These referents take the form of knowledge. All possible information related to the task at hand must be available in order to support the decision making process. To create this kind of knowledge we should use the interdisciplinary method—thus gaining the advantages all possible aspects of a given situation.
     
  • Ability (trained)

    • The need of different abilities is not limited only on physical or technical skills. If we focus on Information processing we need as well abilities in:
    • concentration
    • perception
    • attentiveness
    • selective reception-capability
    • and many more
To initiate a quick and appropriate reaction to incoming information we need, most of all, the mental capability to impart the whole process—from the formation of the information, through its detection, up until the initiation of the response. One must be familiar with all the techniques used in mental training to create likely scenarios. The strong visualization of a scenario creates a situation for the brain as if it already happened. Positive patterns of action can be programmed into the central nervous system. Thus, familiar “real” input takes place in the brain the same way. The mental “idea” of solving the problem serves as a guideline for the actual action.

The use of automatic movements proceeds from the directions given by the stored “footprints” of the mental training. From this point of view even the best fighting skills will not help if they are not activated at the right moment of the confrontation.

Summary
In order for the soldier to cope with all kinds of scenarios, and react with a positive assessment of the situation, new training methods must include mental and intellectual abilities and skills.

Modern technology helps us to detect and “receive” more and more information. To filter this information in light of the aims of the operation—needs of the moment—and to further process it in order to arrive at the correct decision in the fastest possible way; this is still up to the human being.

Our task is to create adequate mental “footprints” to keep the behavior of the soldier aligned on the target while still as flexible as possible. If reality is identical with the unfolding of the action in training, one doesn’t have to think.

Thinking starts when the patterns of acting need to be corrected according to incoming signals that are different than in training conditions.

Information processing on all levels in countering Terrorism is one of the keywords. Information warfare too, has become reality on the front line of counter-terrorist operations.

To simply react to someone with a gun does not necessarily mean to engage the terrorist!
