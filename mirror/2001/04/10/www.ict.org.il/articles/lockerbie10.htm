December 21, 1998
The Lessons of Lockerbie

Yoram Schweitzer,
ICT Research Fellow
 
December 21, 1998 marks the tenth anniversary of the explosion of a Pan-American aircraft in the skies over Lockerbie in Scotland that killed 270 people. 

Today, ten years after the event, the Lockerbie Affair still refuses to pass from the public agenda. More than merely a human tragedy, the incident represents a milestone in the fight against international terrorism. 

The verdict of the Anglo-American investigative commission that Libyan intelligence agents were behind the attack placed Libyan leader Muamar Gadhafi’s regime in the center of a storm of international activity aimed at punishing Libya for its responsibility for the disaster.

Up until then—despite the fact that Libya’s involvement in international terrorism since the 1970’s was blatant and well known—no effective punitive action had been taken by the countries on the receiving end. Limited military operations conducted by the Americans against Libya did not deter her from terrorist activity. Quite the contrary; these operations increased Libyan activity and support for international terrorism. Thus, the sinking of two Libyan vessels in Sirte Bay by American planes (March 1986) lead Gadhafi to order the revenge attack at the “La Belle” Discotheque in Berlin. This attack was the pretext for American punitive air strikes against Tripoli. Gadhafi, for his part, retaliated by executing a murderous campaign of terrorism over the next few years, the culmination of which was the downing of Pan Am flight 103.

The disclosure of Libya’s responsibility for the Lockerbie attack—in addition to an attack on a French UTA airliner over Najar, in September 1989, which cost the lives of 170 passengers—provided the U.S., France and England with the proverbial “smoking gun.” It enabled the US and Britain to invoke the authority of the UN, leading to the imposition of comprehensive international sanctions against Libya

It should be noted that contrary to all expectations, it was the economic and political sanctions imposed on Libya, and not the military actions, that proved to be most effective. Since the imposition of the sanctions in April 1992 Libya has ceased direct involvement in international terrorism and has worked to suppress the activities of the terrorist organizations operating under its auspices, such as Abu Nidal, the Japanese Red Army and others.

In the framework of his struggle against the Security Counsel sanctions, Gadhafi has conducted a cunning campaign for the lifting of the political and economic embargo on Libya. He also attempted to end the affair in such a way as to save face and reduce the threat to his rule from within, while at the same time disclosing as little as possible the involvement of his regime in international terrorism.

This campaign earned Gadhafi merely marginal gains, such as the demonstration of his “endurance” in the face of the American and British demand that the two suspects be handed over to their own juridical powers. The two countries agreed to the trial of the suspects before an international court at the Hague. Gadhafi occasionally managed to flout the embargo on air travel to and from Libya; the flight of Libyan pilgrims to Mecca out of Tripoli in 94, for example, and the demonstrative landing of the African Union delegation in Libya.

However one fact remains: the international coalition that coalesced in the aftermath of the Lockerbie Affair succeeded in completely neutralizing Libyan terrorist activity—activity that had been until then a byword for the phenomena state-sponsored terrorism.

We should remember that the fight against terrorism in general, and against state-sponsored terrorism in particular, is complicated by the individual, sometimes contradictory, interests of the countries involved. This fight does not admit of “knock-out” victories, but only of victory by “points,” the final score to be determined by the test of time.

The relegation of Libya from her status of “pivotal player” to the sidelines marks an unequivocal triumph for the Western alliance against international terrorism. This achievement succeeded in reducing the scope of terrorist activity in the nineties.

The primary lesson to be learned from the outcome of this tragic affair is that the only effective strategy against international terrorism—especially state-sponsored terrorism—is by means of an international coalition working in close cooperation. Only such a coalition is capable of imposing the extensive economic and political punitive measures effective in deterring the supporters and perpetrators of international terrorism. This lesson is applicable too, to the present efforts against Iran, Sudan, and Afghanistan.
