The Consequences of the American Strikes

Boaz Ganor, ICT Director

One of the questions arising from the American strikes is, what will be the consequences?  Will they achieve their goal of preventing further terrorist attacks against American interests?  Or will the present attacks result in a "boomerang effect" -- an escalation involving more and more deadly terrorist attacks against the United States?  Every nation facing terrorism knows that it must act offensively to prevent terrorists from perpetrating attacks freely whenever and wherever they choose.  However the nation that decides to attack terrorists must take into consideration the possibility that such action may increase the terrorists' motivation to take revenge for the damage to their capabilities and to their "image."

A terrorist attack is a function of two factors -- the motivation of the terrorists to carry out attacks, and their capability to do so.  Offensive action against terrorist organizations, by its very nature, increases their motivation to execute revenge attacks.  However, at the worst such strikes won't increase their ability to carry out more attacks; and at best, will greatly damage their capabilities to do so.

The question now is, have the American strikes done enough damage to the infrastructure and operational capacities of bin Ladin's network, as to preclude, at least for the short term, the possibility of revenge?  Or will bin Ladin be able to continue operating as before?  Looked at this way, it would seem that bin Ladin and the organizations allied with him, not to mention his many followers around the world, still have the ability to execute terrorist attacks.  But now their motivation to carry out such attacks will be greater than ever, as they will feel they must avenge themselves and restore their image.

The U.S. can attempt to forestall this escalation of terrorism by conveying the message clearly that it has both the capability and the operational intelligence to strike a far heavier blow against its attackers, and that the present offensive in Afghanistan and Sudan is merely the "tip of the iceberg" of American capabilities.  However, this is not enough.  The terrorist organizations involved here do not operate in a vacuum.  They enjoy the support and refuge of nations.  The United States must also make it clear to these nations that if they support and nurture terrorist groups such as bin Ladin and his followers, they will be held responsible for the actions of those terrorists if they don't prevent further attacks and disarm the perpetrators.

The American forcefulness in combating terrorism cannot stand alone. It is incumbent upon other enlightened nations to stand by the United States, and to declare to terrorist organizations and their adherents that they can expect neither support nor sympathy from the rest of the world.
