February 8, 2001

The Advent of Ariel Sharon:
A matter of war & peace

Boaz Ganor
ICT Executive Director

The recent victory of Ariel Sharon in the recent Israeli election, with the support of an overwhelming majority of the Israeli public is intimately linked to the peace process, terrorism and the Palestinian return to the path of violence after the failure of the Camp-David summit in October 2000.

Since the signing of the Oslo accords in Washington in 1993, political violence has been used as a bargaining chip by the Palestinians time after time, in order to achieve additional political concessions from the Israeli government. At first, during the term of the late Prime Minister Rabin, from 1993 to 1996, the violence usually took the form of Hamas terrorist activity. The motto of Israeli counter-terrorism strategy at the time was “we shall pursue peace as if there were no terrorism.” Not only did terrorism not threaten the fulfillment of the Palestinian national interests, but some Israelis ministers even openly declared at the time that the terrorist attacks in the Gaza Strip showed that Israel had no choice but to withdraw unilaterally. The message was clear: terrorism and political activity work together to bring about Israeli political concessions. This was one of the reasons that Arafat did not use his military capabilities since the establishment of the Palestinian Authority to crush the military wing and infrastructure of Hamas. He wanted to preserve his capability to renew violence against Israel.

The two main concessions that former Prime Minister Netanyahu made during the peace process—the withdrawal from Hebron, and the signing of the Wye agreements—occurred only after the outbreak of two well-orchestrated “popular” uprisings. The first of these was in September 1996, after Israel has opened to tourism the Western Wall tunnels in Jerusalem. The second was in 1997, after the Israeli government decided to establish a Jewish neighborhood in Har Homa in Jerusalem. By making concession after—and only after—Palestinian riots, Netanyahu sent a clear message to the Palestinians: Israeli concessions can only be won through political violence.

Prime Minister Barak went to Camp David with all the intention of signing a final peace treaty with the Palestinians. He was ready to make political concessions to the Palestinians that were unconceivable in the eyes of the average Israeli citizen. Not only was he ready to establish a Palestinian state on more than 93 percent of the West Bank and Gaza strip, he was ready even to divide the Israeli capital, Jerusalem, into two capitals—one Israeli and the other Palestinian. What’s more, Barak agreed to withdrawal from the Jordan Valley, a step which all previous Israeli governments—both Likud and Labor—had long regarded as endangering the very existence of Israel. This is because, without the buffer zone of the Jordan Valley, the most densely populated part of Israel is a strip only 14 miles wide. Had Arafat agreed to Barak’s proposals, this narrow strip would have been squeezed between the newborn Palestinian State and the Mediterranean Sea, and in case of a war could easily be sealed off to split Israel in two. The only way to prevent the invasion of the Arab armies into the Palestinian State in case of war would be for Israel to retain military control of the Jordan Valley, an area with very few Palestinian inhabitants.

But this was not the most impossible of Barak’s proposed concessions. He also offered to grant 100,000 Palestinians “the right of return.” These refugees would live in Israel, rather than in the new Palestinian State, and would be given Israeli citizenship. What this means is that, in accordance with the natural birthrate in the Palestinian family, in another generation these newcomers would number approximately 500,000 people, and, together with the 17 percent of Palestinians already living in Israel, would endanger the State’s Jewish majority.

All of this was not only a real threat to the existence of Israel from the Israeli point of view, but also could be seen as contrary to the foundation of the Oslo accords, which clearly demand concessions from the both sides equally. From statements made by several Palestinians leaders, Arafat included, it was clear from the beginning that in return for the creation of a Palestinian State the Palestinians would not demand the return of refugees to Israel, but would instead agree that they become citizens of the new Palestinian state. Thus, according to the original terms of the Oslo Accord, the Israeli-Palestinian conflict would officially end with existence of two independent states in the Holy-Land—one Palestinian, and the other Jewish.

But, as the late Israeli foreign minister Abba-Eban used to say: “The Palestinians have never missed an opportunity to miss an opportunity.” Arafat refused the Israeli concessions at Camp David, remembering perhaps that Palestinian violence always gave him more concessions from Israel. Therefore he chose to initiate the so-called “Intifada El-Aqsa” armed uprising, a step planned long before Camp-David. Arafat was right again. Barak was not only prepared to give him more concessions after the Intifada broke out, but he was also ready to continue negotiating with Arafat while violence and terrorism were being carried out against Israelis on a daily basis. And this time it was not Hamas violence doing the Palestinian Authority’s dirty work. Rather, it was violence initiated and controlled by Arafat himself through his own Fateh organization.

However, Arafat made one mistake. He apparently did not anticipate that the Intifada would lead to the fall of Barak’s government. When it happened, it was clear to him that he had pulled the string too far; he cannot hope to obtain from Sharon the concessions offered by Barak. Thus, the past few weeks have witnessed a flurry of activity, as Arafat enthusiastically seeks to close a deal with Israel and sign the final agreement. But by Israeli law, an interim government cannot sign an agreement of such importance to the future of the State. So once again, Arafat has missed the train.

In the recent election, the Israeli people granted Ariel Sharon an overwhelming majority, not only to protest the concessions that Barak was ready to give the Palestinians, but also as a declaration of their disappointment with the Palestinians and with Arafat. The common saying in Israel, heard everywhere these days is, “we have no partner for peace.”

Although the newly elected Prime Minister is considered one of the most dogmatic of Israel’s leaders, he is also one of the most pragmatic. He will probably do his utmost to achieve peace with Syria and the Palestinians. His attempts will be determined by the character of the government that he will be able to put together—whether it be a unity government with the Labor party, which will give him room to make concessions to the Palestinians, or alternately, whether it will be a right-wing government, which would tie his hands where the peace process is concerned. But Sharon’s success as a peace-maker will also depend on the willingness of Arafat to be pragmatic as well, and upon his readiness to make concessions from his side.

The problem is that even if Sharon does all he can to reach a settlement—agreeing to the creation of a Palestinian state, the withdrawal of Israeli troop from most of West Bank and Gaza Strip, and perhaps even the evacuation of some of the Israeli settlements in there areas—he will not be able to make the kind of concessions that Barak was prepared to give, and which the Palestinians refused. In particular, Sharon will not be able agree to a withdrawal from the Jordan Valley, the return of 100,000 refugees, nor the division of Israel’s capital. It was on just these issues that Sharon was elected; in agreeing to give what Barak was willing to give, he would be betraying the will of the electorate.

However, even though the Palestinians rejected Barak’s offer, the very fact of its being made raised the expectations of the Palestinians. In the absence of such concessions, the possibility of violent escalation real. The Palestinians may well attempt to use violence in order to “break” the Israeli government, by striking at the Israeli public morale, and this may take the form of severe terrorist attacks in Israeli cities. In the case of a wave of terror attacks perpetrated by the Palestinians or by Hizballah, the resultant Israeli retaliation could set the stage for a regional war. In such circumstances, peace in the Middle East in the near future may well be Mission Impossible.
