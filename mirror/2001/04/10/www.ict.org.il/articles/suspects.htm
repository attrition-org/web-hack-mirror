Three Organizations Claim Responsibility - Real or Spurious?

Reuven Paz - ICT Academic Director

Three Islamic Extremist organizations have so far taken responsibility for the two bombings on Friday August 7th, 1998, in Nairobi and Dar al-Salaam. All three are previously unknown names in the Islamic extremist arena. These names may well be cover names whose purpose is to mislead investigators. Alternately, they may be aliases used as a consequence of rivalry between various groups. The three names are “Al-Aqsa Group” (Jama`at al-Aqsa), The Meccan Group” (Jama`at Makkah), and “The liberation Army of the Islamic sanctuaries” (Jaysh Tahrir al-Muqaddasat al-Islamiyyah).

All these claims of responsibility were made via anonymous phone calls to Arab journalists, characterized by scant detail, so that it is difficult to identify the exact nature of the organizations or of the callers, beside the Islamic militant nature.

The phenomenon of taking responsibility under unknown and newly invented names is a common one. This tactic has been used by various Islamic militant organizations for some years. It is usually done in order to hide the identity of the organization responsible for the attack, or due to rivalries between various groups. Some groups take advantage of the initial confusion right after an attack in order to provide themselves with publicity or to try to augment the confusion.

At present, the most probable group or groups to have carried out the two bombings would seem to be a collaboration of Afghan activists and the Egyptian Islamic Jihad. These groups have recently united under an umbrella organization called “The Islamic World Front for the struggle against the Jews and the Crusaders” (Al-Jabhah al-Islamiyyah al-`Alamiyyah li-Qital al-Yahud wal-Salibiyyin). The Front was declared in an announcement on February 1998 at a press conference in Pakistan. The announcement was made jointly by Saudi exiled millionaire Osama bin Ladin, Dr. Ayman Zawahiri, one of the leaders of the Egyptian Jihad in exile, Rifa`I Ahmad Taha, the leader of the Egyptian “Al-Gama`ah al-Islamiyyah”, Munir Hamzah the secretary of the Association of Pakistani Clerics, Fadl al-Rahman Khalil the leader of “Al-Ansar Movement” in Pakistan, and Sheikh Abd al-Salam Muhammad Khan the leader of the Jihad Movement of Bangladesh.

The declaration of the establishment of the Islamic Front was accompanied by two Islamic Decrees (Fatwah) by Bin Ladin and “The Association of Islamic Clerics in Afghanistan” (Ittihad al-Ulama’ fi Afghanistan), declaring a religious war against the USA. The war was proclaimed a personal religious duty upon every Muslim “to kill Americans and their allies, civilians as well as army men, and steal their money wherever they are.”

The connection between the Liberation Army and the Islamic Front was also mentioned by Egyptian militant Islamic activists in exile in London, in an interview with the London-based Saudi newspaper “Al-Hayat”, in August 9th, 1998.

The possible Egyptian connection to the two bombings may be seen from another bit of information from “Al-Hayat”. On Wednesday August 5th, 1998, the Cairo-based reporter Muhammad Salah published the content of a pamphlet sent to him a day earlier by the Egyptian Jihad, in which the group threatened to hit American targets. The attacks were meant to avenge the extradition of four Egyptian Islamists arrested in Albania. The men were arrested on an American tip, and turned over to the Egyptian authorities, under American pressure.

It should be noted that Iranian officials have condemned the two bombings, as has the spokesman of the Egyptian Muslim Brotherhood. Other important Islamist groups like Hamas or Hizballah have so far not reacted to the bombing.
