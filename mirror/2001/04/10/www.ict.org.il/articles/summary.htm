Summary of the Latest Developments in the U.S. Embassy Bombings

Israel denies report on Kenya attack warning

Reuters reported that the Israeli government denied a previous report in the Israeli daily Ha'aretz that claimed that Israel intelligence told the United States to disregard a warning about a possible attack on the American embassy in Nairobi.

Ha'aretz daily cited an Israeli security official as saying that Washington had sought Israel's opinion on the reliability of the source of the reported warning, to which Israel had replied that, based on its past experiences with the man, the reliabtility of the source was questionable.
 
David Bar-Illan, a spokesman for Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said no basis for the report could be found. "We've checked back six months and there is absolutely no truth or basis to the story," he said.

Source: Reuters, August 12, 1998

Were the embassy bombings revenge for CIA involvement in extradicting terrorists from Albania?

The Washigton Post reported that American officials are investigating the likelihood that the attacks could have been acts of revenge.  In June of this year a raid was carried out by security personnel from the United States and Albania in Tirana against an alleged cell of an Islamic terrorist organization.  The raid evidently "went off like clockwork."

The Albanians arrested two suspected employees of Saudi millionaire Osama Bin Laden, and the Americans took custody of a van load of documents and computer gear.

However, the Albanians leaked a sketchy account of the raid, "including an accurate statement about the CIA's prominent role in its planning" to Albania's largest paper. According to the Washington Post, "By the time a second raid was conducted two weeks later -- in which two more suspects were arrested -- any hope of keeping Washington's fingerprints away from the operation had died."

A possible connection between this sequence of events and last Friday's bombings of the U.S. embassies in Kenya and Tanzania has provoked American officials to begin investigating whether the attacks might have been revenge by Bin Laden's organization for the CIA's role in the arrest of four of its alleged members in Albania.

Source: Washington Post, August 12, 1998

CIA Blocked Two Attacks Last Year

CIA operatives foiled two attacks on U.S. embassies and disrupted three other incipient plots last year.  The planned attacks were discovered after the CIA  infiltrated terrorist cells, and succeeded in monitoring and intercepting electronic communications, according to administration and congressional sources cited by the Washington Post.

The sources suggested that intelligence successes, both in preventing embassy attacks and aiding in the arrest of more than 40 suspected terrorists since 1993, may have contributed to the decision by whoever was behind Friday's bombings to pick the embassies in Nairobi and Dar es Salaam, both of which were deemed low security risks by the State Department.

The two more advanced plots, sources said, did not involve embassies in Africa.

Source: Washington Post August 11, 1998

U.S. Closes Threatened Embassies

The United States has temporarily suspended operations at about half a dozen embassies after the State Department received information on threats to U.S. interests. According to the Associated Press, the State department named U.S. interests in Egypt, Malaysia and Yemen as threatened targets. In Malaysia most services were suspended, and Americans were cautioned to be on the alert. The warning was also in effect in Egypt and Yemen. A senior U.S. official said the warnings were based on "credible information," but declined to provide any details.

The U.S. Embassy in Kampala, Uganda, remained closed for a second day Tuesday, and Ugandan and U.S. security personnel were on high alert five days after terrorists bombed American diplomatic missions in neighboring Kenya and Tanzania.

Tanzania's ambassador, paying a condolence call at the State Department, said some of the 14 foreigners detained in the bombing of the U.S. Embassy in Dar es Salaam may have had a role in the attack. All 14 had been turned over to the FBI for interrogation. They include six Iraqis, six Sudanese, one Turk and one Somali, he said.

Sources: Associated Press, August 11, 1998
