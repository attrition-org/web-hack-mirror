Lesson Overview


Part A: The Great Cause of Union

Some of Abraham Lincoln’s contemporaries called him nothing more than a "country lawyer," "a huckster in politics," "half-horse, half-alligator," and homespun and uncouth. Other critics were less kind, labeling him an "Illinois beast."1 Despite such epithets, Abraham Lincoln became the sixteenth president of the United States on March 4, 1861. All agreed he took office under some very unusual circumstances. In ten southern states his name had not even been on the ballot, so he got not one popular vote from those citizens. Moreover, only a few days after the popular election, in protest of Lincoln’s victory, South Carolina called a special convention to consider secession. On December 20, 1860, the state left the Union, and by the time Lincoln was inaugurated, six other states had "followed South Carolina over the precipice."2 They called themselves the Confederate States of America. President Lincoln did not recognize the independence or legitimacy of the CSA, considering the states to be in rebellion. When in April 1861 he tried to "provision" Fort Sumter, in the Charleston, South Carolina harbor, U.S. troops were fired upon. The Civil War had begun. Yet since the United States government did not recognize the CSA, which therefore did not have the status of "foreign nation," no declaration of war was made. The president called the situation a "rebellion," a "domestic insurrection." The southern states were "sister states" that must be restored through firm leadership to their proper relationship with the Union. President Lincoln took bold steps toward this restoration. He called up a 75,000-man militia to serve for three months, and proclaimed a blockade of Confederate ports. At the end of April, the president took the unusual step of suspending the privilege of habeas corpus along the route between Philadelphia and Washington where pro-southern sympathizers had been harassing troops coming to defend the capital city. Furthermore, in May 1861, Lincoln ordered the paying out of $2 million from the federal treasury, earmarked for other purposes, without specific permission of Congress.

Part B: "Thenceforward, and Forever Free"

Finally, in his strongest application of "restoring order," Lincoln used his powers as commander in chief to free the slaves in states still in rebellion against the Union. Though the president believed that slavery was protected by the Constitution, he opposed this "peculiar institution" as degrading and immoral. He came to believe that "military necessity" gave him ample power to free certain slaves in the southern states during what he called a "domestic insurrection." Through this limited, war-defined proclamation, he took an important first step toward ending slavery in America. However, this, and other actions of Lincoln during the civil war, raised questions about the extent of the president’s power as commander in chief.

Specifically the lesson addresses these issues:

Were Lincoln’s actions those of a president determined to "faithfully execute" the law in a time of crisis, or did he usurp powers given by the Constitution to Congress alone?

How does the president legitimately expand his powers in areas where the Constitution is silent, especially in a time of domestic emergency?

What happens if the president attempts to extend his power beyond constitutional perimeters in a time of great national crisis? Who has the power to stop him?

How vigilant is the citizenry in protecting its civil liberties against undue expansion of power in a time of crisis?
