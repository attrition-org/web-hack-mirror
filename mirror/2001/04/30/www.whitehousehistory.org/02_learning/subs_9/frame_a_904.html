Lesson Overview

Using the tools of his office, a president holds enormous power to bring changes, both subtle and dramatic, in the nation he governs. Certain leaders of the civil rights movement understood that by using organized mass pressure they could tap this source of executive power to accomplish goals.

Part A: President Franklin D. Roosevelt, A. Philip Randolph, and the Desegregation of the Defense Industries.

Among civil rights leaders, perhaps no one recognized this potent presidential power more than Asa Philip Randolph. In 1940, this dignified, tall, fifty-one-year-old man was at the pinnacle of his career as a national labor organizer. As the president of the all-black Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, he had a complaint with the federal government. War was raging in Europe, and it seemed inevitable that the United States would be drawn into it. To provide the resources needed by the beleaguered British and to prepare the United States for possible involvement in the war, the nation had begun to rearm. After years of depression, employment figures were up, as huge defense contracts created new jobs. But not for black Americans, for the defense industry was thoroughly segregated. So were the armed services. When a meeting with President Franklin D. Roosevelt to address these problems yielded no results, Randolph threatened to bring 100,000 black workers to the capital for a "March on Washington" as a means of calling attention to these injustices. Randolph, who had scheduled the march for July 1, 1941, refused to back down without substantial gains, and Roosevelt was forced to act. Using his power to issue executive orders, the president desegregated the defense industry and the civilian federal government by a single stroke of a pen. Furthermore, Executive Order 8802 created the Fair Employment Practices Committee to investigate reported cases of discrimination. Though Randolph called off the march, the organizational structure for a planned March on Washington was not disbanded, just put on hold for another time.

Part B: President Harry S. Truman, A. Philip Randolph, and the Desegregation of the Armed Services

Despite the fact that blacks were hired in defense industries during World War II, the armed services were not desegregated. The legal separation of blacks through so-called Jim Crow laws affected not only civilians but servicemen as well. Blacks continued to be taken into the service under a limited quota system, and although they sometimes trained with white troops, they rode Jim Crow trains and lived in segregated barracks.1 Since the end of World War II, A. Philip Randolph had been seeking ways to pressure the federal government to end discrimination in the armed forces. A study released in 1946 revealed that little was being done to change the segregated status of blacks in the military. In late 1947, Truman had requested passage of a program called Universal Military Training, a preparedness approach that required every young man between the ages of eighteen and twenty to be trained for one year as a reserve force. These units could be called to active duty in the event that the cold war turned "hot." Though it was unlikely that Congress would enact this program, it seemed certain that some kind of draft legislation would be forthcoming. Truman had given a strong civil rights message to Congress in February 1948 but black leaders saw little evidence that he was backing his words with action. When the president made a strong appeal in early March for Universal Military Training and a Selective Service Act (or draft), he made no mention of changing segregated conditions in the military. Though Truman had said he wanted to rid the armed services of discrimination, a strong desegregation clause had been deleted from the Selective Service Act at the request of the army. In a White House meeting with Truman shortly thereafter, Randolph let it be known that the passage of a "Jim Crow" draft would cause an uproar among blacks. "Negroes are in no mood to shoulder a gun for democracy abroad so long as they are denied democracy here at home," he told the president.2 Truman was outraged, calling Randolph’s statement a threat and "treason." Randolph continued his pressure. Testifying before the Senate Armed Services Committee, he stated, "I personally will advise Negroes to refuse to fight as slaves for a democracy they cannot possess and . . . enjoy."3 The response, even from liberal senators, was disheartening. Randolph came to believe that only Truman could set the "moral pace" and end segregation. Continued application of pressure had its effects. It would not look good for a nation that was supposed to be the "bulwark of democracy" against communist tyranny to have a million black Americans refuse military service. On July 26, 1948, President Truman signed Executive Order 9981 desegregating the military. Still believing the order to be ambiguous, Randolph insisted on meetings to clarify its language and gain a strong commitment for follow-through. On August 18, 1948, A. Philip Randolph called off the campaign of civil disobedience.

Part C: President John F. Kennedy, A. Philip Randolph, and the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom

By 1963, a surging civil rights movement was gaining great momentum. Spurred by the Supreme Court ruling of Brown v. Board of Education, dramatized through the new medium of television and led by the charismatic Dr. Martin Luther King, blacks were beginning to apply massive pressure to agencies of the government, demanding change. John F. Kennedy was president and appalled by violence against civil rights marchers in the South; he had begun to label civil rights a "moral cause." In June 1963, Kennedy, though still concerned about alienating southern voters, had sent a civil rights bill to Congress. Black leaders knew the struggle for that bill’s passage would be fierce. To encourage a positive vote on the bill, dramatize the need for change, and impress Kennedy with the magnitude of the movement, a peaceful march for "jobs and freedom" was planned. A. Philip Randolph, who had conceived of this idea so long ago, gave enormous energy to the March on Washington of August 28, 1963. A man of great integrity, he was able to coalesce both the militant, moderate, and conservative elements of the civil rights movement to plan and execute this great event. The march, 250,000 strong, provided dramatic evidence not only to the president, but also to Congress, that blacks would continue to fight for racial justice. Understanding the power of this "massive political force" to press for change, Kennedy used the White House as a setting for a meeting with Randolph, Martin Luther King Jr., and other black leaders both before and after the march. In those Oval Office meetings, two great forces of democratic power came together: the constitutional authority of the president of the United States, and the leadership of a popular mass movement. The successful and peaceful March on Washington was "a high point for those who believed that the grievances of the blacks could be redressed by working within the system."5 Low points were ahead for the civil rights movement, both in terms of its splintering leadership and its ability to effect solutions for deep social and economic problems. Yet for A. Philip Randolph, the March on Washington was the fulfillment of a lifetime of work and validated something he had always believed: power flows from the people.
