Soreness and stiffness in exercised muscles and joints are often experienced by both professional athletes and "WEEKEND WARRIORS." From a scientific perspective, any one of at least four factors may be a causative agent:

  • Minute tears or ruptures of individual muscle tissue increase the likelihood of structural changes in muscle fibers with a resulting enzyme efflux that causes delayed soreness.
  • An accumulation of metabolites in the muscle resulting from prolonged exercise after a latent period. The edema caused by increased osmotic pressure excites sensory nerve endings, thereby causing pain.
  • Overstretching and possible tearing of the muscle's connective tissue harness. A significant increase in hydroxproline levels hours after exercise suggests that connective tissue damage in and around the muscle or an imbalance of collagen metabolism (a degradation process) is somehow involved in exercise-induced muscle soreness.
  • A diminished muscular oxygen supply due to inadequate blood flow in the active muscles can leave the undernourished fibers vulnerable to injury. This ischemia can produce pain and initiate a reflex contraction in the muscle. This situation in turn prolongs the ischemia and sets up repeated cycles of pain, ischemia and spasm.
