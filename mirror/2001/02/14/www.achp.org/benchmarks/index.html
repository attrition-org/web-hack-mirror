Overview

The Benchmarks in Quality & Safety (BIQS) initiative advances a population-based framework for health plans to facilitate the work of provider partners to improve the health of individuals and communities. The program sets an expectation that health plans will pursue broad improvement goals in specific areas responsible for poor health in a significant segment of the community (e.g., heart disease or diabetes). The selected goals must require comprehensive improvement of health services and interventions as well as attack underlying reasons for poor health in the community at large. It also advances a recognition program of benchmark performance based on this framework through establishing criteria related to structural components in place and process and outcome measures to improve the health of a targeted population.

The Alliance will provide a framework for high performing health plans, share information on best practices, and guide plans in need of assistance to the best available sources of quality improvement technology. Alliance health plans will document and freely share the identified best practices in the key health area of accountability. The framework is patient-focused and deals with methods health plans use to add value. BIQS offers answers to these questions:

  • How does the health plan participate in improving the health of the population covered and served and the health of the community? In doing so, how does this targeted improvement of health go beyond physicians to reach other key stakeholders like hospitals and community providers?
  • How does the plan facilitate the work of providers and the processes of care while enhancing quality and reducing waste in the system?
  • How does the plan support enrolled members in managing their own health and in making informed decisions about their health care? How does the plan provide information to members, prospective members and the community on the quality of care and service?
  • How does the health plan make itself accountable to its communities and purchasers in achieving its long and short- term health goals and process goals?
  • How does the plan ensure patient safety?

Advancing a new paradigm beyond process improvement, this framework has two unique features. The first is its focus on the achievement of health outcome goals. An example is the reduction of heart events -- deaths, admissions and procedures among the enrolled population with coronary heart disease (CHD). To achieve such a goal might require improvements in multiple areas such as secondary prevention of CHD, smoking interventions and enhanced management of congestive heart failure. The other unique framework feature is the expectation of community involvement to address behavioral norms or systematic health care issues in the community. The measurement system and designation are being advanced as a model and test run for a new method of accountability that would be further advanced by independent accreditation agencies.

 

BIQS: Key Points

  • BIQS could dramatically improve clinical outcomes. We hope to introduce this methodology broadly to each of the communities served by ACHP member plans and then nationally.
  • ACHP health plans have advanced this methodology and feel confident of its benefit to patients and consumers. We have developed methodologies to identify patients at risk, to design optimal care in partnership with physicians, and to provide physicians with feedback to assure adherence to best practices.
  • This comprehensive framework includes community-wide interventions, assuring patient safety and having the consumer play a central role in his or her health.
  • Health plans that work cooperatively with groups of doctors are in the best position to achieve these dramatic results.
  • This BIQS framework uses performance measurement to mark the progress of improved health for people with heart disease.
  • As leading edge health plans, these companies will share their experience with the rest of the industry and will communicate the importance of measurement and actions to improve quality and outcomes with the public.

BENCHMARKS IN QUALITY AND SAFETY: RAISING PERFORMANCE STANDARDS IN HEALTH CARE

(Facilitating measurable improvement in the health of the members and the communities we serve through meaningful improvements in the delivery of health care services.)

Introduction

Alliance of Community Health Plans (ACHP), the foremost national association of not-for-profit and provider based plans, is working to improve America's health care. In the belief that the need to improve health care quality has become urgent, ACHP (formerly The HMO Group) has embarked on a new and strategic campaign to define and distinguish the characteristics of the trustworthy health plans that make up our membership. We are determined to heighten the awareness of legislators, regulators, the media and other opinion leaders about the values and the value added programs that our health plans and others can and do bring to the evolution of the new health care paradigm. The strong public policy voice that the industry seeks can be attained and sustained only through genuine accomplishments in pursuit of a mission all members share. To this end we will work together to identify performance expectations and areas of accountability which will become the clearly recognized "signature" of health plan membership in ACHP.

This paper provides the overall objectives of the Benchmarks in Quality and Safety program and describes the framework for the performance expectations and standards of membership in the Alliance. Alliance of Community Health Plans' Board of Directors, (through its Medical Directors and its Quality Committee assisted by interested participating organizations), will define the distinguishing characteristics and the criteria which will be used to evaluate member plans and applicants for membership. We suggest seven areas for initial attention by the new Quality Committee and a phased approach to application of a set of standards for accountability in each clinical or health area. This will be the major focus of attention for the Alliance into year 2000 and beyond.

The Benchmarks in Quality and Safety Program: Overall Objectives

The Benchmarks in Quality and Safety initiative advances a population based framework for health plans to facilitate the work of provider partners to improve the health of individuals and communities. The program sets an expectation that health plans will pursue broad improvement goals in specific areas responsible for poor health in a significant segment of the community (e.g., heart disease or diabetes). The goals chosen must require comprehensive improvement of health services and interventions as well as attack underlying reasons for poor health in the community at large. It also advances a recognition program of benchmark performance based on this framework through establishing criteria related to structural components in place and process and outcome measures to improve the health of a targeted population.

The Alliance will provide a framework for high performing health plans, share information on best practices, and guide plans in need of assistance to the best available sources of quality improvement technology. The performance expectations will focus our efforts on exchanging information among member plans. Alliance health plans will document and freely share the identified best practices in the key health areas of accountability.

The framework is patient-focused and deals with methods health plans have in adding value in the processes of delivering and financing health care. The results we will demand of ourselves can only be achieved in well organized and adequately integrated systems in which high quality providers are identified, supported by the plans in their work and with rewards directed toward improved health outcomes. The model we offer answers these questions:

  • How does the health plan participate in improving the health of the population covered and served and the health of the community? In doing so, how does this targeted improvement of health go beyond physicians to reach other key stakeholders like hospitals and community providers?
  • How does the plan facilitate the work of providers and the processes of care while enhancing quality and reducing waste in the system?
  • How does the plan support enrolled members in managing their own health and in making informed decisions about their health care? How does the plan provide information to members, prospective members and the community on the quality of care and service?
  • How does the health plan make itself accountable to its communities and purchasers in achieving its long and short- term health goals and process goals?

These questions are seen in a context of specified health and health care improvement goals in areas such as heart disease, behavioral health, diabetes, women's health, child development, cancer and injuries. The defined populations affected may be high utilizers of medical care while other initiatives will address larger population segments at high risk for illness or injury.

Advancing a new paradigm beyond process improvement, unique features of this framework include its focus on the achievement of health outcome goals. An example might be the reduction of heart events -- deaths, admissions and procedures among the enrolled population with coronary heart disease (CHD). To achieve such a goal might require improvements in multiple areas such as secondary prevention of CHD, smoking interventions and enhanced management of congestive heart failure. The other unique feature of the framework is the expectation of community involvement to address behavioral norms or systematic health care issues in the community. Health plans would be expected to make a contribution in improving the health of the community, which would have indirect benefits to the health of its enrolled population. Lastly, the measurement system and designation are being advanced as a model and test run for a new method of accountability that would be further advanced by independent accreditation agencies.

Whether a health plan obtains benchmark performance in a particular health area will be determined by establishing the presence of structural components and process and outcome measures. Standardized measures from HEDIS will be used for comparative purposes and other health plan generated measures will be examined to note the level of measurement underway at a health plan and the improvement of processes and outcomes over time. As more outcome measures become standardized through HEDIS, more emphasis will be placed on outcome measures and more comparative analysis can be done, assuming there are also risk adjusters available. Initially there will be more reliance on identification of structural components than might be desired. Ultimately, this benchmark performance should be primarily driven by outcome measures. It is envisioned that health plans that meet the criteria would receive some sort of designation like a "merit badge" in a health area.

The first step in this process will be to establish standards and then develop criteria regarding what it means to be in compliance with a particular standard. It is hoped that this Alliance will work with an organization like NCQA in institutionalizing these merit badges. To enhance the credibility of this designation, NCQA and ACHP would work with interested organizations, depending on the topic, such as American College of Physicians, American Heart Association, American College of Cardiology, American Diabetes Association, Washington Business Group on Health and the Employer's Managed Health Care Association, in establishing standards and criteria. These organizations would be joint sponsors of the merit designation. By becoming involved early in this pilot process, both the Alliance and its member plans will be recognized as pioneers of this designation and retain the designation as a signature of the Alliance.

What follows is a general description of each component of the framework as it might relate to a specific health care area. Criteria for the benchmark high performers are a part of the Surveyor Guidelines, which provide further detail of the expectations for this designation.

a. Quality Infrastructure Prerequisites

Health plans that meet ACHP's BIQS Eligibility Criterion, "Quality Infrastructure Prerequisites", may apply for BIQS Merit Designation. The Eligibility Criterion is described in the Surveyor Guidelines. While the BIQS Eligibility Criterion encompass areas addressed by NCQA standards (quality management and improvement and preventive health services in particular), the actual BIQS Criteria go beyond NCQA standards in the area being examined. Receipt of a BIQS Merit Designation indicates that a health plan has met the Quality Infrastructure Prerequisites and then gone beyond these standards in the subject area. Differences between BIQS and NCQA accreditation of a health plan (which also requires efforts and examines specific areas of health improvement) include broad population-based goal setting requiring comprehensive interventions, community partnerships and participation in efforts to improve the health of the community.

Plans that meet the quality infrastructure prerequisites:

  • Provide sufficient evidence in resource commitment, structural design and process capabilities to demonstrate competence in quality improvement.
  • Provide members with adequate assistance, including pertinent information on qualifications, to facilitate informed choices about physicians.
  • Provide mechanisms for resolution of disputes about health plans' coverage decisions.
  • Measure access to primary care physicians and non-primary care consultants.
  • Share measures with health professionals and employ strategies to improve access as appropriate.

b. Engagement in Population-based Health Improvement and Health Outcomes

This model of population health improvement is largely based on the work of HealthPartners and presented in an article by Nico Pronk and Patrick O'Connor, MD, "Systems Approach to Population Health Improvement" (J Ambulatory Care Management, 1997). The goal is to improve the health of a targeted population. Elements of this model include: 1) to set specific, appropriate and measurable population health improvement goals; 2) to assess the health risks and health status of the identified population (this directs the program planning and establishes a baseline and aids in the identification of members who would most benefit from specific interventions); 3) to assess the willingness of the members to participate and eliminate barriers; and 4) to have each individual assess his/her readiness to change behavior. Based on goals, willingness of members to participate, the health status of the population, and readiness to change, appropriate programs can be developed, implemented and then evaluated. Pronk and O'Connor go on to discuss what is required to sustain this effort, which includes the need for substantial resources, measuring process and outcomes measures and developing long-term relationships with stakeholders.

This area can be expanded to include a wide continuum of care and focus on improving outcomes of care. The Foundation for Accountability (FACCT) has developed a similar framework for outcome measurement. It includes finding measures in areas referred to as: Staying Healthy (preventive and wellness services), Living with Illness (functional status, disease management) and Changing Needs (long-term care). The active participation of a delivery system in measuring outcomes along this spectrum for a particular health area is the sign of a comprehensive and effective system of care. Measures to track include: clinical process measures, patient satisfaction and in the long-run, functional status such as disability days.

This area of standard rating would hold plans accountable for:

  • Taking a comprehensive approach to providing population based health services with the aim of improving health status. A critical component of this approach is selecting aggressive goals for health improvement in areas selected by the health plan.
  • Identifying patients at high risk of illness or injury.
  • Developing stratified interventions for patients with varying degrees of risk factors to improve and enhance the health of targeted populations. The health plan measures the processes and where feasible, the outcomes of care impacted by the interventions.

c. Systematic Effort to Improve the Process of Care and Service

Within each selected area of concern, organized systems of care must be held accountable for efforts to improve the processes of medical diagnosis and treatment in acute, chronic and end of life phases of care. This is done to improve the satisfaction of the patient with the experience, improve the medical outcome and reduce waste in the system. The more lean the production, the more standardized the process and the more systems-thinking involved in planning the process, the better the expected outcome. This systems thinking involves an examination of the work process, identification of the root causes of a problem and a redesign of the process which might involve information system solutions (i.e., reengineering). To meet aggressive health goals in specified areas requires making changes in multiple places along the continuum of care (preventive, acute and chronic care) and determining high leverage points to maximize a system's return on investment. In addition to improving existing processes of care, the system looks to develop entirely new systems of care and intervention to achieve its goals. Scientific evidence from the literature and collecting best practices experiences are used in designing new approaches to health care.

The essential skills and proper orientation to manage care on a population basis go beyond interventions targeted to change behavior in specific clinical areas. Improved performance of physicians requires the collaborative development of specific care management skills in such areas as case management, case reviews, clinical guidelines, data review and quality management. Physician education is a key component in achieving change on the front lines of the delivery system; the trustworthy health plan facilitates these developments.

This area would assess how medical care systems, with the assistance of the health plans would:

  • Develop a comprehensive improvement strategy linked to the achievement of overall health improvement and evidence that the process improvements were measured and linked to significant improvement in outcomes of care. There is evidence of strong leadership commitment to this endeavor.
  • Develop a significant concentration on improvement in each selected health area sustained over a period of time. The areas are chosen along the broad continuum of care and service.
  • Develop and collect reliable and valid process measures related to the overall outcome goal that demonstrate year-to-year improvement in the system.
  • Routinely collect and share information with its providers about patients' satisfaction.

d. Consumer/Patient Empowerment

Increasingly, individuals are learning to seek physicians and other providers who match preferences and perceived needs. Such patient preferences for physicians may be based on age, gender, medical history, previous experience with physicians or many other factors. We recognize the special dynamics of choosing a physician within an HMO system and the additional decision support appropriate to the system's unique features. Information can allay anxiety. With this in mind, information about physicians' education, graduate training, areas of expertise, accessibility and availability and meaningful quality measures should be provided. This should be a basic feature of any trustworthy and accountable health plan.

Patients should be supported and encouraged to seek health care information and have active roles in the development of personal care plans. There is an important role for accountable health plans in the facilitation of shared decision making. The mass of information and information technology available is nearly overwhelming to individual providers anxious to support their patients in increasing their autonomy. Plans should be expected to support providers in this role. Of equal or greater importance is the opportunity to develop a new pathway for intervention strategies targeted to involve patients in patient-initiated behavioral change.

This area would assess the part health plans play in providing:

  • Information to members on physicians and venues to encourage informed choices. In each health area how are the higher-risk patients guided and assisted?
  • Information to members about general health issues and specific conditions and treatments.
  • Mechanisms to involve individual patients in treatment choices.
  • Information to members on the ethical content of plan policy and benefit decisions.

e. Access to Services

Access is defined here as care that is appropriate and timely and provided by the right caregiver to the satisfaction of the informed patient. Patients with appropriate care plans and proper access are more likely to utilize resources at the appropriate rate and level. In specific areas, such as heart disease, how does the system facilitate the use of the appropriate physician access during non-symptomatic, acute and chronic phases of care? How does the system monitor satisfaction of the provider (as well as patient) on the key issues of access and what facilitative strategies and remedies are in place for shortcomings?

This area is included in the prerequisites for BIQS and examines the role the health plan plays in:

  • Measurement of direct access (e.g., waiting times) and corrective improvement strategies.
  • Measurement of utilization factors relating to access standards (e.g., ER visit and admission rates for chronic conditions).
  • Measurement of satisfaction with access and response strategies.
  • Accessing information innovations (e.g., E-mail).

f. Community Action and Quality Information

Member plans of the Alliance of Community Health Plans have been consistent leaders in providing increasingly meaningful and accurate information to purchasers and consumers. The objective has been the encouragement of value-conscious purchasing and informed decision making in health plan and physician selection. Making provider-specific information more readily available through collaborative partnerships with physicians, defines the direction of accountable health plan efforts. The means and format chosen by the health plans to make such information accessible and understandable is also critical.

Plans provide community benefits and services that have measurable impact on the community as well as the membership directly served. Such services now include subsidization of premiums and the operation of Medicaid and Medicare programs. Community benefit also includes support of biomedical and health services research and services directed to the entire community (e.g., immunization and nutrition awareness programs). We regard the regular reporting and willingness to be held accountable to our communities in these areas as an obligation of the trustworthy and accountable health plan.

This area will assess how the health plan:

  • Demonstrates awareness of and concerns itself with community issues in the selection, project design and execution of interventions.
  • Establishes relationships with providers in its communities and has engaged them as partners in advancing towards the health plan's specified goal(s).
  • Makes quality data and information available to the general public as well as the membership about reaching health outcome goals established for the health plan.
  • Provides and presents detailed and accurate information to the public on measurable community benefit programs and activities.

g. Assuring Patient Safety

Patient safety is currently a serious concern in health systems. The Institute of Medicine's (IOM) recent report To Err is Human: Building a Safer Health System raised awareness and prioritized patient safety as an area for health system improvement. According to the report, "more people die in a given year as a result of medical errors than from motor vehicle accidents, breast cancer, or AIDS".

Patient safety in BIQS is defined as ways that health plans address prevention of avoidable patient injuries in health care, either medication errors, surgical errors or negligence. The health plans that receive a BIQS designation have made patient safety a priority corporate objective. Health plans that assure patient safety review morbidity and mortality statistics for procedures (i.e., cardiovascular) and have policies and procedures to guide elective treatment to hospitals and clinical teams that:

  • Demonstrate superior measured outcomes.
  • Meet evidence-based volume criteria.
