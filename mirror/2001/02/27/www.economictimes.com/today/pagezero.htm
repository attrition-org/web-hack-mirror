Monday 01 January 2001
Wrap-up | Finance | Industry | Economy | Corporate | Infotech | The Markets | Commodities | Politics | Comment | Profiles


Wrap-up
Curry in a hurry minus bandwidth
HAPPY New Year. India’s entry into the third millennium hasn’t happened with balletic precision. We lurch into 2001 with the chaotic hop-step-and-bhangra-wiggle of an Indipop song and dance routine.

Finance
Bank on more consolidation
IF the banking industry seemed to sleep through most of the first half of the year, the latter half saw a flurry of activity spurred by policy changes and their implications for future bottomlines.
M&As in India Inc
THE M&A engine in India is humming with activity, and its getting pretty loud too. The last calendar year saw a spate of big ticket deals, but the action in 2000 was far heavier.
A bitter sweet story
COMPARED to the rapid fire financial sector reforms that sweeped the country in the post-crisis years of the early nineties, the changes in the last years of the millennium may appear glacial.
Major milestones
THE STAGE was set for a lower interest rate regime right at the start of 2000. On January 14 the governement cuts interest rate on PPF and postal savings by one percentage point and this was followed by a cut in general provident fund rate by 100 basis points in the Budget to 11 per cent.
Together, we move ahead
The pace of consolidation in the banking sector is picking up and is expected to get even more rapid this year. If not through deregulation, then definitely through play of market forces.
Small and mighty
One private bank — HDFC Bank — is valued more than all listed nationalised banks put together.
This way or that
IMD has helped stabilise the markets for now. But a lot will depend on how global oil prices move.
Reality check
Most institutions had announced several plans for new initiatives during the year. We take a look at the status of these initiatives
Ringside view
Seshadri Sen,
Insurance is Ricky Martin
COME December and it is time for the ubiquitous customary flashbacks resplendent with memories, both sweet and sour, both hits and misses. A lot many things happened this year in the financial markets, but let us put it to a tune.

Industry
Rollicking times ahead for tipplers
THE REMOVAL of quantitative restrictions on the import of spirits is bound to bring cheer to spirited tipplers in the new year. With the April 1 deadline not far, connoisseurs of ambrosia don't have much longer to wait for the doors to open.
Mixed signals slow broadcasting boom
THE COUNTRY saw broadcasting thriving with the turn of the century. More than two dozen satellite channels were added over the last year, with another 20-30 in the pipeline.
Passenger cars hit speed-breaker
DIPPING growth, a plateauing market, policy pussyfooting and a tax trapeze -- that just about sums up Y2K for the passenger car industry.
Mobile industry rings in a happy new year
THE MOBILE beep is expected to get louder in '01. By the time we bid adieu to this year ('01) the country could have added another 2-3 million cellular subscribers (the total subscriber base is close to 3 million now). If there is greater clarity on regulatory issues and foreign funds flow in, the number could be even 5 million, feel leading industry players.
Cementing of alliances may slow down
AFTER a long time, the cement sector is breathing easy again. Following the recent price hikes -- by Rs 20 to 55 per bag -- effected across major markets, cement companies are in no mood to let go this opportunity even if it threatens to put brakes on the pace of consolidation in the sector. Cement prices in Mumbai, the largest market in India, are currently ruling at Rs 180-182 per bag compared to Rs 125 in October 2000.
Garments industry picks up the threads
THE REVELRY started in October. And, the New Year will definitely be chock-a-block with action. Taking a cue from the thriving garment industry in Bangladesh, the government finally liberated the garment sector in India from the shackles of licensing and small scale reservations when it announced the new textile policy in October 2000.
Healthcare set to thrive
THE NASCENT corporate healthcare sector in India which has been going through birth pangs for sometime now is set to come into its own in 2001. The year is likely to see the arrival of new entrants in the Rs 92,000 crore industry. At least two new players Max India and Fortis, are set to open their facilities in the first half of the new year.
Breathe deeply and stick out your tongue
ITS THE bugbear of the Indian drug industry. Key legislation that has the potential to alter its fortune is taking its own time coming. As for Y2K, the Vajpayee government did not take a single decision on the contentious issues of patents and pricing, leaving the industry on tenterhooks. So will this New Year be any different?
Consumer was king in 2000
THE FIRST year of the new millennium - if we sidestep chronological controversies here, as did all the revellers last year - has just passed into history.
Commercial vehicle industry in reverse gear
THE COMMERCIAL vehicle industry is virtually living on hope. As of today, the industry seems to be struggling to keep its head above the water if recent growth figures are any indication.
Consolidation takes a breather in healthcare industry
IT'S BEEN a bit of an anti-climax. The wave of consolidation threatening to sweep over the Indian pharmaceutical industry appears to have lost momentum mid-way.
Aviation alive with sounds of change
PRIVATISATION was the buzzword in the Indian aviation sector during the year 2000. The landmark decision to hand over management of Air-India and Indian Airlines to private parties, the steps taken to lease out four international airports and the bid to establish an economic regulatory authority for the civil aviation sector have made it a watershed year.
Power utilities suffer Rs 25,000 cr a year as T&D losses
GUESS how much the country has been losing each year on power transmission losses. A mind-boggling Rs 20,000-25,000 crore. Technically described as transmission and distribution losses, this is an euphemism for large-scale theft in India. Which actually brings out the extent of criminality in the transmission racket.
TV firms set to take a hit
THIS is the question whose answer could fetch you many crores. What do you do to pump up colour TV sales? Companies that make those boxes face year-end sales of about 5 million, a negligible 4 per cent growth over last year’s 4.8 million number. Is the Indian market, where cable TV reaches nearly 70 million homes, saturated with soaps and KBC?
Fun set to become serious business
THE FAMILY entertainment and amusement parks business is expecting to grow a scorching threefold in the next three years. The industry has attracted an investment of Rs 1,000 crore so far but is gearing up for incremental growth.

Economy
The Chinese are coming!
THE GUESSING game goes on. Months have passed since Chinamania afflicted India Inc but a clear picture on the exact impact of imports from China is yet to emerge. Still, the Chinese `invasion' of the Indian marketplace is perhaps the most debated issue in domestic business circles since the liberalisation unleashed in the early 1990s.
Govt spares consumers global oil shock
CONSPIRACY theorists rejoice. There's a group of people from 11 nations - the oil ministers of Opec - who can plausibly take some of the blame for most of the heartbreaks in the year.
Another year of living dangerously
IN A few days from now finance minister Yashwant Sinha will begin work on his fourth budget, assisted by a team headed by the fourth finance secretary to work under him. This will set some sort of record for administrative churn within North Block, which houses the ministry of finance.
First wave of reforms rolls on
THE PACE of reforms in 2000 has hardly been blistering. If anything, blustering was more the ministerial norm. Yet, even if the second wave of reforms has been conspicuous by its absence, the first wave continued to roll on in some sectors.
Company-centric regulations on the anvil
IF THE year gone by is any indication of things to come, corporate India could well see a more 'corporate-centric' securities regulator in the coming year.
CPI-AL show prices falling for rural consumers
IF YOU follow the wholesale price index, these are inflationary days for the Indian economy with inflation rates crossing 7 per cent. But not so for rural India.
World trade growth peaked in 2000
2000 represented a peak for global trade flows. The volume of world trade growth surged by 10 per cent in 2000 -- the highest growth in the decade.

Corporate
Companies get a little of the human touch
WELCOME to a kinder, gentler workplace. After one year of frenetic employee turnover, soaring corporate salaries and developed nations relaxing quotas for overseas workers, Indian employers have seen the light and are preparing to treat employees better than before.
Corporate churning picks up pace
IN his new avatar as cricket commentator, Australia's ex-cricket captain Bill Lawry would have taken one look at the churning in corporate India and drawled, "It's all happening here."

Infotech
Will I&B tune in to tomorrow's bandwidth?
THERE was a lot of talk, a lot of activity and there was some movement as well. But by the end of 2000 the information & broadcasting ministry's story still has a lot of incomplete chapters.
Gateways to fuel ISP expansion into VSNL turf
THAT interminably long wait before your instant messenger flashes on screen may soon be a bad memory left behind in 2000. Consumers frustrated with internet connections that time out, drop or are excruciatingly slow, can hope to chase away their virtual blues as ISPs invest millions of dollars to set up international private gateways.
When bricks met clicks
NEW years seem to have a way of putting question marks on the fate of the New Economy. Last year, it was the post-Y2K life of the software industry. This time there are troubling messages about its bottomlines and growth rates.
Is IT a white elephant without power, broadband?
LET us face it. The ministry of information technology was set up at a time when the hype around the IT sector was at its peak...maybe even as a result of all the hype.
Global trends to affect Indian software services
THE SOFTWARE services sector in the coming year in India is going to be shaped by global and regional trends.
US dotcoms come full circle, over 175 go bust
THE PREVAILING feeling in the US about dotcoms has come full circle. A year ago, start-ups were reinventing the economy, wooing investors and attracting piles of money.
Road to faster Net: Pvt cos take lead
IF you are already exasperated about the number of times the road in front of your house has been dug up, things are about to get a lot worse.

The Markets
Brandex fared the best in 2000
THE STOCKMARKETS will probably call 2000 the year of 'back to the basics'. A near-total u-turn characterises the occurrences during the year. Nothing illustrates this better then ET's three proprietary indices: Mindex, Lifex and Brandex.
The markets: waiting to exhale
THE YEAR 2000 began with a big bang. On the first trading session, all the software stocks hit the upward circuit filters. Even the Sensex started its day with a gap of more than 200 points. The reason for this extremely bullish behaviour was euphoria because there was no major Y2K problem which could have given Indian software companies a run for their money.
Upside visible in Old Economy scrips
INDIAN markets are cautiously optimistic about the coming year. After a sharp retreat from an all-time high of 6,150 in early 2000 to around the 4000 level by the end of the year, the stockmarkets are eagerly awaiting a trigger for decisive trends in forthcoming months.
Bye-bye tech, hello biotech
TECHNO is passe. Trance is in. And what a fall it's been ever since. After reaching an orgiastic high at the infotech counter, Dalal Street's busy ruminating on the Y2K scare. What the Bug failed to do to the hardware industry a year ago, the bears have done to software scrips aplenty. Call it the bug-bear of the new millennium or whatever you will, the New Economy lies in tatters. So what if the brick-n-mortar stock lay crumbling as well?
Broadbase your bets for mutual benefits
AFTER being accustomed to high double digit and even triple digit returns from heavily concentrated tech sector equity mutual funds, 2000 has turned out to be a disappointment for mutual fund investors.
Debt scores over equity again
DEBT has once again emerged as a preferred option over equity for mobilising capital in 2000. Around 65 per cent of the total funds raised between January 1 to November 30 this year were through debt issues, with the balance 35 per cent by way of equity.

Commodities
Surge in energy costs guides metal dynamics
IN THE non-ferrous sector, aluminium has been one commodity that has seen a lot of movement in recent times. Production cutbacks by global majors from June 2000 saw a drop in stock levels and a firm-up in its prices.
In full bloom
THE YEAR in question started off with a bang for the Indian floriculture industry. Millennium celebrations and the millennium’s first Valentines Day saw exports of Indian flowers move up.
They still hurt you, anyway
Coffee prices are steadily declining in spite of a bumper crop and affecting growers’ health. Meanwhile, all over the world cigarette manufactures are being sued for the effects on consumers’ health
Mixed fortunes for steel sector
WHILE it has been a year of mixed fortunes for the steel industry, the sector has witnessed a sharp dip in prices towards the end of the current calendar year.
Spice sector feels the heat
THE SPICE sector has entered the new millennium with nearly 30 per cent fall in exports during April to November of this fiscal. The fall in the export of pepper was the main reason for the decline in total spice exports. According to the spokesmen of the spice sector, the industry has to look for value-added products to come out of the declining trend in exports.
Rubber loses elasticity
The removal of quantitative restrictions in several commodities by April 2001 would affect the rubber sector badly as there will be a flood of imports. Meanwhile, pepper exports have fallen by 30 per cent this year
Tea gets cold
PRICES of almost all varieties of tea were hit. A closer look at costs and productivity is essential.
Diamonds: sky is the limit
INDIAN exports are expected to scale a new high of $9bn during 2001. Uncertainty about adequate supply of roughs from De Beers is forcing exporters to look for supplies elsewhere to meet commitments overseas.

Politics
Vajpayee's unfinished tasks
PRIME Minister A B Vajpayee may have been voted man of the year on a website; he may have succeeded in scoring points over an inept Opposition in Parliament; his allies may still be willing to peddle Vajpayeeism. But, there's a big but.

Comment
The year of Kashmir...
BY and large, 2000 was an uneventful year, with the economy and politics chugging along as usual. Yet it could go down in history as the turning point in the Kashmir dispute. So many Kashmir initiatives in the past have petered out that we should not bet too much on the new one.
...mixed signals...
IT’S easy to get despondent about the performance of the economy during the year gone by. On almost every front, it’s been way below both expectations and what is needed to power India into prosperity in the twenty first century.
...and aberrations
IN RETROSPECT, a term used this summer by an official of the Nepal Tourism Board could define the year 2000. He had called the December 1999 hijacking of IC-814, an aberration.
