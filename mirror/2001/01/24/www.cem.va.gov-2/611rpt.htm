This report has been prepared to meet the congressional reporting requirements of Section 611 of the Veterans Millennium Health and Benefits Act, Public Law 106-117.  This is the first of a series of reports the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) is required to submit.

 

    

Legislative Requirements

 

Public Law 106-117, Section 611, directs the Secretary of Veterans’ Affairs to establish a national cemetery in each of the six areas in the United States that the Secretary determines to be most in need of such a cemetery to serve the needs of veterans and their families.  This legislation also directs the Secretary to submit to Congress a report on the establishment of these six national cemeteries, which shall include the following:

 

(A)   The six areas of the United States determined by the Secretary to be most in need of the establishment of a new national cemetery.

(B)   A schedule for such establishment.

(C)   An estimate of the costs associated with such establishment.

(D)   The amount obligated from the Advance Planning Fund in the Construction, Major Projects appropriation to initiate planning and site identification activities for such establishment.

 

Section 611 also requires the Secretary to submit an annual report on the establishment of these six national cemeteries that includes an update of the information requirements listed above.  Annual reports are required until the establishment of the additional national cemeteries is completed.  The Conference Report accompanying the Veterans Millennium Health and Benefits Act states that Congress expects the locations of the six new national cemeteries to be established will be based on the findings contained in the 1987 and 1994 Reports to Congress on the National Cemetery System.

 

 

Background

 

Veteran deaths are increasing each year as World War II and Korean War-era veterans continue to advance in age.  Based on the 1990 census, annual veteran deaths are expected to peak at 620,000 in the year 2008 before beginning a gradual, slow decline. Over the next decade, annual veteran deaths are expected to increase 10.5 percent from a total of 561,000 in 1999 to 620,000 in 2008.  This progressive increase in veteran deaths  results in a corresponding increase in the demand of burial services in national cemeteries.

One of the strategic goals of NCA is to assure that the burial needs of veterans are met. Experience and recent historical data show that over 80 percent of persons interred in national cemeteries resided within 75 miles of the cemetery at time of death. Based upon this experience, NCA considers the burial needs of veterans to be met for those veterans that have reasonable access to a burial option.  Reasonable access to a burial option means that a first interment option (whether for casketed remains or cremated remains, either in-ground or in columbaria) in a national or state veterans cemetery is available within 75 miles of the veteran’s place of residence.

 

 

Previous Reports on the National Cemetery System

 

Public Law 99-576, the Veterans’ Benefits Improvement and Health-Care Authorization Act of 1986, required VA to identify the geographic areas in the United States in which the need for burial space for veterans was greatest.  An initial Report was submitted to Congress in 1987 prior to the 1990 census.  A second Report was submitted in 1994 using new demographic data and a revised methodology. 

 

The 1987 Report identified ten areas of the country most in need of a new national cemetery based on concentrations of the veteran population.  These areas included:

 

·        Albany, New York

·        Chicago, Illinois

·        Cleveland, Ohio

·        Dallas/Fort Worth, Texas

·        Detroit, Michigan

·        Miami, Florida

·        Northern California (San Francisco area)

·        Oklahoma City, Oklahoma

·        Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania

·        Seattle, Washington

 

The 1994 Report stated, “Oklahoma City and Pittsburgh were identified in the 1987 Report to Congress.  Based on current demographic data, these areas are not on the list of 10 most in need.  However, in the time since the release of the 1987 report, Congress has earmarked funding for advance planning and completion of EISs at these sites.  VA has proceeded with EIS contracts and plans to include these areas in the NCS planning process for future consideration.”   The 1994 Report listed the following areas:

 

·        Atlanta, Georgia

·        Albany, New York

·        Chicago, Illinois

·        Cleveland, Ohio

·        Dallas/Fort Worth, Texas

·        Detroit, Michigan

·        Miami, Florida

·        Sacramento, California

·        St. Louis, Missouri

·        Seattle, Washington

 

The rankings in the Reports were not priority listings, but lists depicting veteran population.  Subsequently, four new national cemeteries have been opened in areas identified in these reports:

 

·        San Joaquin Valley National Cemetery  (California – 1992)

·        Tahoma National Cemetery  (Washington – 1997)

·        Saratoga National Cemetery  (New York – 1999)

·        Abraham Lincoln National Cemetery  (Illinois – 1999)

 

Additionally, two other national cemeteries from the Reports are presently under construction near Dallas, Texas and Cleveland, Ohio and will open during the year 2000.

The remaining geographic areas identified in the two Reports are, in alphabetical order:  Atlanta, Georgia; Detroit, Michigan; Miami, Florida; Oklahoma City, Oklahoma; Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania; Sacramento, California; and St. Louis, Missouri.

 

 

Identification of Areas for Additional National Cemeteries

 

The remaining five geographic areas identified in the 1994 Report to Congress represent those areas of the country most in need of a national cemetery. The VA will use the findings in the 1987 and 1994 Reports to Congress, and the Millennium Act Conference Report, to determine the six locations where new national cemeteries will be established.  The demographic information on the veterans population contained in these 1994 rReport remains valid.  The VA will proceed with the development of new national cemeteries in four of these the following locations:  Atlanta, Georgia; Detroit, Michigan; Miami, FloridaOklahoma City, Oklahoma; Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania; and  Sacramento, California.

 

The St. Louis metropolitan area is the fifth area remaining from the 1994 Report to Congress.  There are seven areas of the country remaining from the 1987 and 1994 Reports to Congress most in need of a national cemetery.  However, since thisese Reports was were issued, additional land has been acquired from the adjacent VA medical center to extend to the year 2010 the service life of Jefferson Barracks National Cemetery, which currently serves the greater St. Louis area.  In addition, NCA is continuing to work with the VA medical center to identify more land that could be made available for cemetery use.  The burial needs of St. Louis area veterans are being met.

 

Additional areas most in need of a national cemetery were not listed in the 1994 Report.  Consequently, until a new study is completed, as required by Section 613 of the Veterans Millennium Health and Benefits Act, VA is unable to determine what other areas are “most in need”.  However, VA is currently developing plans for two new national cemeteries to serve veterans residing in the Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania and Oklahoma City, Oklahoma areas.  The 1994 Report recommended that VA continue to plan to provide service for these areas.  These areas were listed in the 1987 Report and Congress has earmarked planning funds for new cemeteries in these locations.The establishment of Combined, these six new national cemeteries in these six areas will allow NCA to make additional progress in meeting its strategic goal to assure that the burial needs of veterans are met.  The strategic impact of these cemeteries can be measured by the change in the percentage of veterans served by a burial option in a national or state veterans cemetery within a reasonable distance (75 miles) of their residence.  In the FY 2001 President’s budget, NCA estimated that 82 percent of the veteran population would be served with reasonable access to a burial option in FY 2005.  With the establishment of six new national cemeteries in the locations identified above, the percentage of veterans served would increase to 88 percent.

 

 

Status of  New Cemetery Development

 

The development of new national cemeteries generally takes 5 to 7 years.  A schedule for establishment includes the following major processes:

 

·        Site Selection  (12 months)

·        National Environmental Policy Act Compliance  (12 months)

·        Land Acquisition  (6 months) 

·        Master Planning and Design Development  (12 months)

·        Construction Documents Preparation  (9 months)

·        Construction Award and Completion  (18 to 24 months)

 

To provide service to veterans as quickly as possible, NCA is committed to reducing the average length of time associated with each of the steps in the development process identified above.

 

An estimated cost for the establishment of a the six new national cemeteryies identified in this report is $20 to $24 million. for each cemetery.  This estimate is based on recent NCA experience in establishing new national cemeteries.  Funding estimates for the specific phases of the development process include:

 

·        Site selection and Environmental Assessment  ($125,000)

·        Master Planning and Design Development  ($1 million)

·        Land Acquisition  (from no cost to $4 million)

·        Construction Document Preparation ($1,700,000)

·        Construction Contract  ($17,000,000)

 

Costs will vary from location to location due to a number of different factors, including land acquisition costs, cemetery size and projected workload, environmental factors, and topography.  Progress in the establishment of these cemeteries and site specific funding needs will be assessed annually in conjunction with the budget formulation process.

 

 In FY 2000, Advance Planning Funds in the Construction, Major Projects appropriation will be used to fund the first phase of development of 5 new national cemeteries.  A total of $500,000 was provided in the FY 2000 appropriations bill to begin the site selection and environmental assessment process for the establishment of new national cemeteries in the areas of Atlanta, Georgia; Miami, Florida; Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania; and Sacramento, California.  VA has allocated $125,000 for each of these four areas.  In addition to these four areas, VA has included $125,000 in the FY 2000 budget operating plan to begin the site selection and environmental assessment process for a new national cemetery in the Detroit, Michigan area.  NCA officials are seeking possible cemetery locations in these five areas and are working with veterans affairs officials from each State to facilitate the site selection process.  Funding of $2 million is included in the FY 2001 President’s budget for Master Planning and Design Development activities for these sites. 

 

The FY 2000 budget also included funds for concluding the planning process in the Oklahoma City area.  A site for the sixth new national cemetery at Fort Sill, Oklahoma has been identified and acquired to serve the Oklahoma City area.  On March 6, 2000, a contract of $568,000 was awarded to the architectural and engineering firm of Poe & Associates of Oklahoma City to complete the Master Plan and Phase I Development design work.  The contract awarded to Poe & Associates also includes an option for the preparation of construction documents.  This work should be completed in the fourth quarter of FY 2001., after which time a construction contract could be awarded.     

 

Funding of $2 million is included in the FY 2001 President’s budget for the second phase of development for four sites:  Atlanta, Georgia; Detroit, Michigan; Miami, Florida; and Sacramento, California.  Specifically, this funding will be used for Master Planning and Design Development activities.
