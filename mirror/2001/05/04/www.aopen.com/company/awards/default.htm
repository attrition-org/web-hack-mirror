Awards & Reviews
[AX37 Plus]

  • AOpen AX37 Plus won the Top Grade Hardware Award from the  Active Hardware
    The AOpen AX37 Plus is the highest performing board based on the VIA Pro266 chipset that we've yet tested. It is very well conceived, and carried itself well throughout our analysis.
    By including a number of interesting features - such as the ability to adjust the FSB in 1MHz steps, to adjust the Vcore, a RAID controller, and DDR-SDRAM support - the AX37 Plus constitutes a true performance champ, and an obvious choice for Overclockers - especially given how well it responded to tweaking. So, its with great honor that we award the AX37 Plus with our "Top Grade Hardware Award".

    Reviewed by Active Hardware, 2001/4/9
[AX37 Plus]

  • AOpen's AX37Plus Highly Recommended by VIAHardware.com
    I am quite an impressed by the quality AOpen has imbued into their AX37 Plus. Overall, this is one of the most stable boards I have ever worked with, and it shows great thought went into the design and manufacturer of the board. Also, it shows the maturity of the Apollo Pro266 on launch, we could only hope for such a trouble-free chipset when Pro133/133A was launched.

    Reviewed by VIAHardware.com, 2001/3/14
[AK73 Pro]

  • Trumph again!! AOpen AK73Pro won again the Editor's TOP CHOICE Award from the techweb, Maximum3D.
    Throughout testing, the AK73 was rock solid, even during our 24-hour burn-in testing featuring Prime95....Overclocking was a success, with our trusty Duron making the 900 mark for the first time in its life, something that had been troublesome on some of our other machines

     Reviewed by Maximum3D, 2001/2/13 

[AK73-1394]

  • AOpen AK73 1394 Won High Grade Appraisal from Anandtech.
    It quite clear that the AK73-1394 is a very well rounded motherboard. The design is no different from the AK73 Pro, but this time AOpen has included a single-chip Firewire solution. Performance-wise it is almost the same as the AK73 Pro, while proving to be even more stable, allowing it to compete head to head with ASUS and ABIT KT133 solutions

    Reviewed by AnandTech, 2001/2/2
[AK73 Pro]

  • With the introduction of the AK73 motherboard, AOpen has officially opened itself to the newest generation of Socket A logic-boards. Unlike the AK72, which was a Slot A board that implemented the VIA KX133 chipset, the AK73 uses the Socket A CPU interface coupled with the VIA KT133 chips. As a result, the AK73 supports both ATA100 drives, and PC133 memory, and also happens to sport an integrated AC"97 audio-card along with a number of other interesting features.

    Reviewed by Tom's Hardware Guide, 2001/1/19
[DX2G Plus]
  • As longtime readers of 2CPU.com will know, I have a penchant for Xeons and Xeon motherboards.  You've seen my article HERE about Xeons, and read my review of the Tyan Thunder-X dual Xeon board HERE.  Since I first got into the world of Xeons, a lot has changed.  Intel has gone back to a socketed chip format instead of Slot 1, or the Xeon's enormous Slot-2 format.  The Pentium 4 has also been introduced, and it seems that Xeons are on their way out.   Let us take a moment and remember an awesome processor.  A processor with up to 2MB of full speed L2 cache.  A processor that needs heatsinks that weigh almost a pound.  A processor that packs 8-way SMP power. 

    Reviewed by 2CPU.com, 2001/1/19
[AK73 Pro]
  • I would strongly recommend the AOpen AK 73 PRO to anyone wanting to get the most out of their motherboard and Socket A CPU. For this reason we are pleased to give the AOpen AK 73 PRO our Life Sentence award for Power Users,Gamers and Value Users.

    Reviewed by Tweakers Asylum, 2001/1/19
[AX34 Pro II]

  • Overall the AX34 Pro II is definitely a motherboard which grew in stature as testing was carried out. It had a tough act to follow in the form of the AX3S Pro which in many ways set the standards by which we started to judge the AX34 Pro II.

    Reviewed by Processor Emporium UK, 2001/1/9
[AK73 Pro]

  • This board is simply stunning. AOpen boards always have very good compatibility and we experienced no AGP or DIMM problems at all here, even when the board was fully populated with 3 128 megabyte DIMMs. If only we could say that about other KT133 boards...
