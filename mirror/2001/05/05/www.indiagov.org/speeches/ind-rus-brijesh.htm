37th Munich Conference on Security Policy
[February 2-4, 2001]

Statement by Mr Brajesh Mishra, National Security Advisor & Principal Secretary to the Prime Minister of India on 
India-Russia Relations
India-Russia relations have several remarkable characteristics. They have demonstrated a robust strength, withstanding major structural changes in both the countries. Indo-Soviet relations moved almost seamlessly into Indo-Russian relations, in spite of the post Cold War political and military realignments and the upheavals caused by the breakdown of our economic arrangements with the Soviet Union. We retained our mutuality of interests and shared perceptions even in these totally changed circumstances. There was, and remains, a national consensus in both countries on the fundamentals of our bilateral relations. As President Putin said in India in October last year, the two countries have never clashed even once nor has there ever been any serious dispute or contradiction between us.

A number of objective factors have shaped the congruence of outlook and shared concerns which have determined the course of India-Russia relations: 

  • History evolves -continuously and politics is ever volatile, but geography remains constant. There is a compelling geostrategic logic for close Indo-Russian relations, which has been clearly grasped by every leadership of both our countries, regardless of its political or economic ideology.
  • The two countries have a common interest in the development of a multipolar world based on a cooperative security order. In fact, even in the bipolar construct of the Cold War, the development of India's relations with the Soviet Union was perhaps one of the first expressions of multipolarity. 
  • We do not share a border, but we do share neighbours and a number of security threats from across our borders. As President Putin pointed out in his address to the Indian Parliament in October last year, the same individuals, the same terrorist and extremist organizations are involved in terrorist acts from the Philippines to Kosovo, including in Jammu & Kashmir, Afghanistan and Chechnya.. Sensitivity to each other's security concerns has been an integral part of our relations. We understand Russia's real security concerns in Europe, Central Asia and the Caucasus and about the destabilizing effects of undermining existing arms control and arms limitation treaties. We receive the same understanding from Russia on our concerns. 
  • Russia's "National Security Concept" of January 2000 and its "Foreign Policy Concept" of June 2000 enunciate a worldview similar to that of India. We share disquiet at insidious attempts to undermine sovereignty and to justify intervention in the name of human rights, to encourage protectionism in the guise of labour and environmental standards, and to impose alien sociocultural conditions in the name of globalization.
  • Neither country perceives a threat from the strength of the other. In fact, each has a stake in the increased political and economic power of the other. Uniquely, the undercurrent of competition or suspicion, often evident in relations between two equal partners in such close geographical proximity, is totally absent in the Indo-Russian equation. Russia's public and unequivocal support for India's permanent membership of the UN Security Council is only one illustration of this.
  • The increasing political and economic importance of Asia has created a new security matrix in which both India and Russia have crucial and complementary interests. Asia does not have an institutional security framework like that in Europe. Any Asian security framework can be sustained only with strong inputs from India and Russia. The importance of Indo-Russian relations for a stable security order in Asia is enhanced by the fact that both countries have separately been steadily strengthening their bilateral relations with other major powers - USA, EU, China, Japan, as well as Iran and Central Asian, ASEAN, and West Asian countries. This web of bilateral relations helps us to promote a cooperative security order rooted in pluralism.
  • Over 500 years ago, a Russian traveller, Afanasy Nikitin, became the first recorded European to set foot on Indian soil, over 25 years before Vasco da Gama. He travelled along the land and sea route, which we today call the North-South transport corridor. In September last year, India and Russia signed an agreement on developing this corridor to promote bilateral trade and other exchanges by reducing transport and transit costs. A cursory glance at a Eurasian map would show that this corridor has more than an economic significance. 
  • As two large and modernizing economies, we have similar perspectives on the pace and direction of the globalization process. We have the complementarities to exploit the opportunities created by economic liberalization and technology diffusion to our mutual advantage.
Our close bilateral relations are serviced by intensive and sustained dialogue at all levels. In implementation of the decision on annual summit meetings, our Prime Minister will be going to Moscow later this year. Regular exchanges are maintained between Foreign Ministries, Defence Ministries and our National Security Councils.

It is no secret that the two countries have had substantial military-technical cooperation over many decades. A significant proportion of the military equipment of our three armed services is of Russian (or Soviet) manufacture. In recent years, our defence relations have moved from a mere buyer-seller nexus towards joint research, development and manufacture. 

We place special emphasis on cooperation in the energy sector. This is a crucial element in our goal of energy security. Indian companies hope to enter the Russian oil industry through investments in Sakhalin. It is the same objective that drives our cooperation in nuclear power: Russia is collaborating in the construction of a 2 x 1000 MW nuclear power plant in Kudankulam in Southern India. 

We acknowledge the underachievement in our bilateral trade, which has an annual turnover of only about US $1.6 billion. Much of our trade is through the rupee repayment track by which India repays the rouble debts of the Soviet Union days. [Incidentally, among the countries with debts to the former Soviet Union, India is the only one to have reached agreement with Russia, fully addressing the debts]. The financial and banking crisis in Russia in 1998, and the slowdown in our economy due to the Asian crisis had some impact on our trade, but we have put them behind us. We are shifting gradually from our traditional areas of economic interaction like power, steel, coal and pharmaceuticals to areas like space, information technology, robotics and oil exploration.

Of course, I am not saying that we do not have our differences. But we recognise and manage them with mutual understanding and without rancour. We have our separate views on nuclear proliferation. During his visit to India, President Putin expressed the hope that India would continue to move toward a non-proliferation regime, taking into account its national interests. Unlike other nuclear powers, Russia did not impose economic sanctions or participate in indiscriminate technology denial regimes after India's nuclear tests. This would have simply had the effect of throwing out the baby of our flourishing civilian economic and technological cooperation along with the bath water of proliferation concerns, which were, in any event, unfounded. Russia also refrained from lecturing, hectoring, posturing or offering gratuitous advice on the validity of our security concerns and on how we should address them. I think this demonstrated the maturity and depth in our relationship. 

It is this mature understanding of the mutuality of interests that inspired our Declaration on Strategic Partnership, signed during President Putin's recent visit to India. Strategic partnership is not a military alliance, nor is it directed against any third country or group of countries. It is a long-term perspective on bilateral relations, based both on geostrategic realities and economic opportunities. It is a declaration of our solidarity in the emerging political, economic and security scenario of the new millennium.
