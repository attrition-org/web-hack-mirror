Speech by Minister of State for External Affairs U.V. Krishnam Raju 
at
inaugural of the seminar 
on
'State Sovereignty in the 21" Century: Concept, Relevance and Limits'
(Indian Council of World Affairs, Sapru House, March 29, 2001; 1030 hours)  

I am happy to welcome all of you to this Seminar on the subject of 'State Sovereignty in the 21st Century: Concept, Relevance and Limits'.

The subject of the Seminar is indeed topical.

The increasing pace of globalisation, modern means of communication, the breath taking reach and possibilities of information technology, the power of the media and public opinion have all challenged the traditional notions of state sovereignty and the hitherto unchallenged primacy for national governments in dealing with internal affairs.

The end of the Cold War, following the dissolution of the former Soviet Union, has given rise to a new debate on unipolarity versus multipolarity. The tendency to impose solutions unilaterally has come under criticism. At another level, the process of creation of supranational institutions in Europe also has implications for the notion of nation states and sovereignty.

The events in some parts of Africa, and in southeast Europe generated concerns related to genocide, crimes against humanity, egregious violations of human rights. Suggestions have been made that the international community, however defined, had the right or the duty to intervene in such situations.

I invite you to examine whether this is an easy principle to apply in every situation, whether general norms for action in such situations can be developed, or whether each situation would have to be inevitably assessed and responded to in view of its own specific and peculiar context.

At the same time, most countries, especially those who have attained independence in the not too distant past, cherish the principle of state sovereignty. Narrow and self interests of the proponents of such intervention can also never be wholly dissociated from any larger humanitarian projections. However, does sovereignty also need to be balanced by the need to ensure accepted principles of governance.

It is also worth recalling that the UN Charter empowers the Security Council to act for the maintenance of international peace and security, and, interpret for itself what may constitute a threat or breach of such peace and security. Can a call for humanitarian intervention, bypassing the Council, be justified on account of the concerns cited, or would it become a recipe for international political disorder.

Instead of calls for intervention bypassing the Council, as has also been suggested by several analysts, should the focus be more on the quality of decisions taken by the Council, its process of decision making, and its unrepresentative character.

Furthermore, in nascent nation states in the developing world, should the attention be more on strengthening pluralism and nation building, rather than external intervention. Would external intervention help the long term process or would it tend to put stress on the political and social fabric, and ironically instigate situations which were sought to be cured. 

What could, in this context, be the relevance of a positive agenda, through a global compact to lift developing societies from the devastation of underdevelopment, and for which national and global responses could be agreed upon. Would this be better and more effective than a negative agenda based on intervention and presumption of failure.

With these initial thoughts, I leave you to your well informed and in-depth deliberations on the subject. All of you have distinguished records of achievement in your separate fields of diplomacy, academics and journalism. The subject will benefit from your varied perspectives and in-depth perceptions. 

We hope to follow up an this Seminar by an event some months later where we would also invite participants from some other countries. Our eventual aim would be to share the product of our discussions with the wider membership of United Nations. 

I wish you success in your deliberations. 

Thank you.
