Presentation by MR. BRAJESH MISHRA,
National Security Adviser, Government of India 
on 
India and the stability of the Asian Continent 
at
Institute Franchise Des Relations Internationale, Paris 
January 31, 2001

Starting from somewhere around the early nineties, if you opened any newspaper, magazine or journal in Europe or North America, and saw an article about Asia, you knew it was about a region including China, Japan, Korea and the ASEAN countries (then not including the Indochina countries or Myanmar). The Asian success stories were from these countries. Discussions about the Asian ethos or Asian values revolved around the culture of these countries. The mystique of the Asian inscrutability and the power of the oriental philosophy were analysed deeply to discover in them the source of the extraordinary economic and technological advances made by these countries. 

India, whose civilization and culture are as ancient, which has roughly 40% of the population of what atlases call Asia, and which first tried - as far back as in 1947 - to define the Asian identity of the free countries of the continent, did not figure in this definition of Asia. 

Three years ago, when the same region went through a period of economic turmoil, it was described as the "Asian" crisis, totally obscuring the fact that many Asian countries - whether by sound economic policies or by luck - had largely escaped the more virulent manifestations of the financial virus. 

The point I am making here is not purely one of nomenclature. The whole Asian continent cannot be so simply analysed with uniform and internally self-consistent economic, cultural or political yardsticks. The diversity of the continent defies such monochromatic characterization.

Asia is more than half the world. It is the largest and most populous continent, with nearly 60% of the global population. Eight of the ten most populous countries are in Asia. Several of the great ancient civilizations originated in the continent. Almost all the world's religions - Hinduism, Islam, Buddhism, Christianity, Judaism, Sikhism, Taoism, etc. - originated and continue to thrive in Asia.

This cultural heterogeneity coexists with a widely differentiated pattern of economic development - a legacy of different experiences of colonialism and of Cold War economic arrangements. This means also that, unlike in North America or South America or in Europe or Africa, where single overarching security frameworks exist, Asia cannot be defined within a single security paradigm. The security concerns of West Asia (what you would call the Middle East) are addressed very differently from those in Central Asia, South Asia, Southeast Asia, or East Asia. The inter se relationships between Russia, China, Japan, India, ASEAN, Central Asian Republics and the Gulf countries have been in a state of some flux in the Cold War period and its immediate aftermath and this has obviously affected the security environment in the continent. 

It is important to remember that Asia has four declared nuclear weapon states and a fifth tacitly acknowledged one, some of the largest standing armies of the world, non-Asian navies operating freely in Asian waters, and a large number of missile producing and exporting countries. Asia's military spending has actually increased sharply after the Cold War. 

The political scene in Asia has been transformed significantly in this period. The most dramatic event in this transformation was the collapse of the Soviet Union. Russia has, however, shown over the recent years that, despite its economic setback, it will retain its global role. The internal political consolidation, the emergence of a firm leadership committed to core national interests, and a turnaround in its economy are all positive indicators that Russia is on the recovery path. 

In the Asian context, the rapid growth of Sino-Russian political, economic and military relations has important consequences. Russia also has a major role to play in Central Asia, where it has vital interests.

India-Russia relations have strengthened their multidimensional character. Our declaration of a strategic partnership during President Putin's visit to India in October last year was a positive contribution to the matrix of regional stability in Asia. 

China has always been a major military and economic power in Asia. We have been following with much interest the debate in academic circles in the West - and the ambivalent posture of some of its policy makers - on whether or not China's economic and military strength is a factor of stability in Asia. I do not want to enter into this debate. But it is a self-evident fact that as the world's most populous country and as a permanent member of the UN Security Council, China's participation is an important requirement in any viable Asian security framework. Its profile in military alliances and its commitment to existing nuclear and missile technology transfer regimes would be important indicators of the directions that its role would take in this venture. 

Japan remains an economic superpower, in spite of the recent setbacks of the "Asian crisis". It is in an introspective phase, but has the potential to change course and become a dominant military power in the continent. Published statistics show Japan's military expenditure - about US $50 billion - is among the largest in the world. Japan's evolving engagement with Russia and China, in the background of its undiminished military alliance with USA is another crucial factor in the Asian security equation.

The countries of ASEAN have registered rapid economic growth in the past two decades. The end of the Cold War, however, altered some of the basic security assumptions of the region. ASEAN has responded with political maturity, and is today an important element in Asia's larger regional and global security engagement. The enlargement of ASEAN to include the Indochina countries and Myanmar and the broad-based approach to security through expansion of the ASEAN Regional Forum have moved ASEAN steadily towards a larger Southeast Asian and East Asian community.

In West Asia, the yearning for peace is clearly visible. But the issues involved are highly emotive and complex. Nevertheless, a peaceful resolution of the conflicts in the region is a sine qua non for Asian security. Tied to this is, of course, the question of oil security. 

Iran is in the midst of a socio-political and economic transformation. Reconciling tradition with modernity is the major challenge; indications are that there is a groundswell of pro-reform sentiments especially among the younger generation in that country. Iran's improvement of relations with Saudi Arabia, Russia and west European countries is a stabilizing development. Iran also has an important role in Central Asian security. President Khatami's proposal for a "dialogue of civilizations" provides a framework for engagement with Iran. 

After years of isolation, Iraq is on the verge of resuming normal international interactions, as the sanctions wear out. We should not ignore the fact that Iraq is one of the most influential of Arab countries; its isolation is unnatural and the UN sanctions regime requires urgent review. 

Central Asia has produced the most recent crop of newly independent countries. These countries face daunting challenges of socioeconomic development and nation building. At the same time, the region is also blessed with vast reserves of natural resources, which can transform the lives of its inhabitants. However, the competition for hydrocarbons and the politics of pipelines may generate destabilizing tensions. Religious extremism and ethnic separatism are also real threats. 

Thus there remain a number of question marks on the future security and stability of the Asian continent. Non-military threats like religious extremism, drug trafficking, money laundering and terrorism exacerbate the situation. Poverty and shortages of food and energy threaten the stability of societies and new nation states. Unchecked population growth and the debilitating effects of communicable diseases like AIDS and TB are factors of concern. Territorial disputes bequeathed by colonialism and aggravated by the Cold War add to this already rich cocktail. 

India is located in the geographical midst and the political centre of this volatile continent. We have the undoubted strengths of a functioning vibrant democracy, a well-established economic reform process underway for over a decade, and a network of cordial relations with most countries and groupings in the continent. Our approach seeks to counter immediate military threats to stability even while tackling the non-military threats to long-term security. 

In our immediate neighborhood we have been forced to develop a minimum nuclear deterrent We have explained the security imperatives that led to our decision. Here in Europe, NATO continues to exist long after the collapse of its adversary. If even in this benign security environment and amidst unprecedented economic prosperity a powerful military alliance is still required with hi-tech weaponry and nuclear forces, the security imperatives that India faces should be easy to understand. We are happy that there is a much better understanding today in the international community of India's viewpoint in this matter. 

We strongly reject club-based technology denial regimes as instruments to promote security. Though ostensibly meant to deny dual-use technologies to nuclear aspirants, these regimes often promote hidden commercial motives and adversely affect genuine requirements of developing countries for socio-economic development. The regimes have failed miserably in controlling proliferation and transfer of nuclear weapons and missiles in Asia. They have not adequately dealt with missile proliferation linkages across regions. By penalising genuine importers of development technology and condoning clandestine transfers of offensive technology, these regimes actually worsen the security picture.

Besides the response of military self-defence and nuclear deterrence to our security environment, we have also given high priority to resolving outstanding problems with our immediate neighbours and to increase our level of engagement with other major countries which have a role in promoting stability in Asia. We have sincerely tried to resolve our problems with Pakistan. The initiatives that the Prime Minister of India has taken in the recent past have led to a significant reduction in firing along the Line of Control and the International Border with Pakistan. Our latest decision to extend by yet another month the suspension of actions against militant groups demonstrates our sincere effort to go the extra mile in search of a peaceful solution. But terrorism and violence continue unabated and there is no indication that Pakistan is inclined to give up its hostility towards India.

We have a close dialogue with Russia, ASEAN, China, Iran and other countries. We have a regular security dialogue with several nations including China with which we are also holding discussions on clarification of our borders. One round of security dialogue was held last year. Rakesh Sood, Joint Secretary (Disarmament) was present. Another one will be held on February 8, in a few days' time. We intend to deepen our security dialogue with other countries of the region. 

We believe that the strengthening of regional cooperation both for security and for economic development is a crucial input in the promotion of regional stability. We are members of SAARC, BIMSTEC and the ASEAN Regional Forum. A relatively new grouping - the Shangai-5 - has also been moving in the right direction towards fostering cooperation and reducing tension in Central Asia, which is a region of strategic importance for us. 

The financial and economic crisis in South East Asia in 1997 vividly demonstrated the relationship between globalisation, development economics and security. The crisis itself showed the pitfalls of a headlong rush into globalisation and liberalization without taking local socio-economic realities into account. The recovery experience of various countries has further shown that countries would have to choose their own pace of globalisation and that prescriptions from world financial institutions are not necessarily the best way to secure political and economic stability. Our economic engagement with Asian countries and groupings is firmly based on this recognition. We seek a dialogue bilaterally and multilaterally with other countries of the region to harmonise our position on key issues of development, trade and investment and environment. 

We have laid great stress on confidence building measures in the security field. The Indian Navy has friendly contacts with several navies of the region. With the multiple overlap of maritime economic zones of different countries of the region, cooperation against sea piracy and misuse of marine resources is important. The Indian Navy is hosting in Mumbai next month an International Fleet Review, "Bridges of Friendship", which seeks to foster friendship among the navies of different countries. We also support greater openness in military affairs through greater contacts among security establishments. Incidentally, it is not very well known that Directors General of Military Operations of India and Pakistan have regular telephone conversations on the hotline every Tuesday. They must have taken place yesterday at 12 o'clock our time. They can also have unscheduled conversations at the request of either party. 

India has been active in sensitizing other countries in the Asian continent to the menace of international terrorism, drug trafficking and organized crime. Cooperation among ourselves, to expose the linkage between the three and to counter the threat, is sorely needed. According to a recent report, Afghanistan now produces nearly 80% of the world's opium, leading to heroin production worth about US $ 100 billion. Much of the rest of the world's opium is produced in Myanmar. The implications are obvious. India has proposed a UN Convention on Terrorism. It can only be a first step towards closer coordination among countries in the Asian continent against terrorism fuelled by drug trafficking and organized crime. 

These are some ideas aimed at promoting Asian stability. They need to be fleshed out and constantly updated through bilateral and multilateral discussions, which would generate political confidence building measures to overcome mutual suspicion and distrust. Balanced economic growth with regional economic integration, and a multipolar political order rooted in pluralism are the prerequisites for a stable security equilibrium in Asia. India is committed to working with other Asian countries for this.
