Text of Presentation: 
'India's Perspective on International and Regional Security Issues' by
Hon'ble Mr. Jaswant Singh
Minister of External Affairs of India
at 
The Egyptian Council For Foreign Affairs,
Cairo on February 3, 2001 at 1700 hrs
INTRODUCTION 

The theme on which we are gathered here today to share thoughts has four integrals; the expectation, understandably, being that it is the Indian view point which will be put across. This is reasonable enough with two caveats, however. Firstly, though there distinctly is an Indian approach to the questions that the theme poses, it cannot be "that the answers that are offered are so exclusively Indian that none other share those views, because the broader aspects of the theme do have an universal relevance. The second caveat is that though I owe the honour of this invitation to the fact of the office that I hold, I do not share thoughts with you in that capacity alone. I do so as a student of current history, too

What are these four aspects?

i) Perspective:- Of what is a perspective born? 

ii) Security:- What exactly is it that we seek as security? What are the 
vital integrals of national security? 

iii) Issues: - Our outlining of a perspective and broadly defining quest for security - (holistically, and in detail) itself identifies the issues that confront us, and finally; 

iv) Regional and International:- This explains itself though we need to be clear about who defines regions, and why and how?

Let me attempt to now share thoughts on the questions that I have myself raised. 

2. Perspective and issues:-

I intend to cover these two together, principally because from perspectives flow issues.

It is both expected of and is of course the responsibility of foreign ministries to constantly study global trends, to attempt not just to identifying these trends, as against events alone, but to also attend to the duty of influencing them into more benign directions.

Where is our world, as we face this new millennia, headed? In what direction, and impelled by what ideas or issues? We can only surmise, for though an Indian, I am afraid, I am not endowed with that faculty which I am informed is our characteristics an ability to look into the opaque uncertainty of the future and be able to decipher its imponderables, We have but one foundation on which we can structure an assessment of possibilities - History. Necessarily, our future must have some connection - (which is not a synonym for continuity) - with the past. From History, therefore, what connection do we endeavour to discern for the future? Primarily, of those broad trends of ideas, which in turn influence events. Thus an understanding of the trends of history becomes our guide. But only a guide. Because a realistic and objective assessment of the nature of the world as it is then becomes a necessary balancing requirement, for the world, alas, is as it is: which is a very far cry from our dream of the world as it ought to be.

A distinction also needs to be made at this juncture between trends and events. We routinely speak of the sheer unpredictability of events. This is an accurate observation, verified by our experience of history. Yet, I believe, this is more applicable - to single, individual events. Trends are born of ideas and larger tendencies, which remain relatively unchanged. They are a consequence of that broader sweep of the evolution of human thought, hence history.

A Perspective for and the Issues of Our Times

We stand on the doorway of the 21st Century. As we reflect on the Century that has just passed, the 20th, the trends that were then either set in motion, or demonstrated clearly stand out, Let us look back to 1900: What is most striking as we do so is to find that but for the USA, or France, almost the entire rest of the globe was under monarchies. Democracy and representationalism' was but in its infancy. And yet, as the Century progressed, this trend towards democracy got so irresistibly established that it became the predominant movement of the 20th Century, Monarchies collapsed, as did the great colonial Empires, which had had their origins in the discovery of the sea routes to India, and incidentally only to America. I reflect often upon this great chance discovery: Christopher Columbus setting sail for fabled India and reaching America. What if he had not? 

The 20th Century saw not only the end of dynasties and monarchies and empires, most transiting to democracy, but also the ascendancy, reign and then the decline of the Doctrine of Marx. This example - of the rise and fall of such a powerful idea - of Marxism - (Communism) within one life span, has seldom been experienced earlier. Is there a lesson here: about fallibility, and about the arrogance of doctrinaire certainty? 

The 20th, was also without doubt, possibly the most violent of all the centuries that mankind has experienced since the dawn of time. And, as we, therefore. attempt to distill trends from this broad sweep of history, are we to infer that, at the dawn of this new Millennium, and our entry in to the 21st Century, we have now entered an era that marks the and of conflict? I cannot answer in the affirmative. 

Conflict, has not come to an end, it continues, for it never really ceased. What has altered is the nature of war and peace, which has changed both in the political and in the technological sense. And as part of technological please reflect upon a reality: Instant TV is one of the modern day participants in conflicts thus directly influencing its conduct, also often, the handling and a resolution of it. It is also no longer possible to think in terms of a single great conflict; and not simply because there is only one superpower, It is no longer possible to conceive of a situation in which any country is entirely overrun and all its populace made subject - the Gulf War actually exemplifies this. In fact, conventional wars, or what are sometimes called 'limited wars or conflicts', or even 'regional conflicts', persist, as I earlier said, simply because they never really ceased. Yet another aspect of change is this obliteration of the distinguishing line between 'internal' and 'International' conflicts. Axiomatically, therefore, in such situations, the difference between war and peace, therefore, between a state of war and a state of peace has also altered radically, This is, possibly, an unintended and Unexpected consequence of the end of the Cold War. Politically, amongst other aspects the Twentieth Century is marked by the collapse of Empires, the end of colonialism, emergence of new sovereignties, the two great conflicts, the employment of air and its technology for war, the dawn of the atomic age, and of profound importance - the collapse of the USSR, The consequences of this last are yet to be fully assessed The relative stability of the world, during the Cold War, was based on certain foundations of time-tested international conduct: sovereignty of nation states, the borders of it being sacrosanct, because the consequence of acting to the contrary resulted in the unsettling of an established balance. With the great melt down. in the freeze of the Cold War and the disintegration of the Soviet Union also arrived an end to this self-limitation. Thus Central Africa, Yugoslavia, Kosovo, Iraq today happen and we accept them, as if all this is in the natural order of things, But this does raise serious questions - questions as pertinent as the relevance of nation states.

The other great transformation, directly impacting on security and international relations has been brought about by advancements in technology. This has, unequally though, given nations the power of "selective destruction". This is, of course, an advancement but it has also inherent in it the possibility of arbitrary and unjust employment. Thus, technological advancement in the field of armaments has recreated. (for through History, there always has existed such disparity), a new category of haves and have-nots. This, too, has seeds of potential discord because, as the have-nots strive to obtain that which the haves have, denial of access will lead to resentment. Let me also refer here itself to what I call the phenomenon of a 'democratization of access to means of destruction'. Increasingly, we witness this now, as yet another consequence of the end of the Cold War. That Cold War period was the golden age for the global armaments industry, they worked overtime, at full capacity, reaped huge profits and proliferated arms of such variety as mankind had not ever earlier seen. Even as the Berlin Wall was being pulled down, this vast arsenal and its great capacity became available, almost without interruption. Here readily entered the private armies, the narcotics dealers, the terrorists, the purveyors of death. It is this availability of arms that, at least in part, gave a new impetus to what is now almost a new ideology - the ideology of terrorism. 

An outstanding example of this is, of course, to be found in what is currently happening in Afghanistan-Pakistan. Earlier, a great contest on the soil of Afghanistan, between the then USSR and, by proxy, the USA, resulted in the birth of the Talibans and in consequence a great deal else that has followed: Arms, narcotics, terrorist training camps and the creation of a new and additional destabilizing agency. Equally inevitably what this has resulted in is a kind of a validation of the employment of terrorism and terrorists, as extensions of state policy. Thus encouragement, abetment, financing, arming, training of terrorists by agencies of state has got conferred a kind of legitimacy. From this an advocacy of 'Jehad' as an instrument of foreign policy, has followed as a matter of course. The ertire world is now, therefore, an arena for the application of this destructive doctrine. 

As we address ourselves to what is by now a cliché phrase, the 'post-Cold War world', we ought at least to identify some distinguishing features of it. I have already referred briefly to the as yet un-assessed consequences of the disintegration of the USSR. We are just a decade down the tine from that great event, and no doubt this historic development will influence our future That is one part of it. The other is the emergence of the United States of America as the pre-eminent global power:, militarily, technologically, economically, indeed even, in terms of popular culture. And, there is then that other irresistible trend - the spread of English language, globally, in its many forms and derivatives, but most importantly as compu-talk. Therefore, it is clear that for so long as United States of America is able to maintain its economic, technological and industrial position. it will remain the pre-eminent global power. 

Such a situation has not been experienced earlier, not during the Roman Empire, not even during the vast sweep of the British Empire, And, here-in perhaps lies both a paradox and the challenge: the world today is far too complex to remain permanently so uni-polarised. Such a global primacy by itself throws up questions which will unfold in the decades ahead. The challenge, therefore, lies in understanding the perils of pre-eminence; in recognizing that the essence of power lies in accepting the limits of it.

We must, at this juncture, reflect on the future of the State. Here three contradictory tendencies are experienced. In large parts of the world, particularly in Asia, and Africa, there exists, in contra-distinction to trends elsewhere, an assertion of the role and relevance of the State, the inviolability of the sovereignty of such State, and marked caution, if not suspicion of trends that advocate a dilution of the State. In other parts, we are witness to a re-definition of these very sovereignties, a dilution of the hard lines of it, a movement towards coming together, to federating into larger units. There is then the third, as for example in the Balkans, or in parts of Africa, or disquietingly in Central Asia, where the functional efficiency, political authority, indeed even legitimacy, of the political organism of State is not simply being questioned, the State itself has begun to whither. This is not unrelated to what I termed earlier as a 'democratisation of means of destruction', in other words, a loss of monopoly of the power of coercive force, earlier the sole prerogative of the State. As there no longer obtains a power balance in the globe, and as this is accompanied by an absence of any distinguishing central authority, and we continue to persist in keeping in abeyance a real reform of UN and expansion of UNSC. I apprehend that this simultaneous trend towards anomie and the withering away of State could well persist in the decades ahead. Self-evidently, this poses many security issues of altogether different and new dimensions. Such States then become, in themselves, the source of radiating instability, discord and violence. 

A brief word here about globalisation. An assertion of it is an advocacy of the theoretical need for a wider access by all the global national players to the total economic cake cif the world. The proposition is, however, limited to greater access, not to greater sharing. Besides, this does not mean that such advocacy actually results in any quick and equal access either, Globalisation is, obviously, only a process, and it makes no claims to operating evenly in all fields of human endeavour. Certainly even economic globalisation is a far cry from political globalisation. The world is not a single unit, and the globe, of course, is not a village. 

SECURITY 

Nation States, when they seek security in today's world are, therefore, not any longer doing so purely in a militaristic sense, or as part of a quest for possessing overpowering mechanized forces.

In considering the totality of national security, economic security is the pivot: its vitality, growth and dynamism becomes the principal security imperative Economic strength, however, is impossible without industrial output, which in turn is unsustainable without energy, and in the absence of technological parity. All these are crucial security issues. So is a protection of territory and the inviolability of national boundaries against aggression. This is self-evident. But how is this to be addressed. when a great question that we face today is demographic; both in the growth of population, in the global imbalance in its growth, as also in the irrepressible impulse of human beings to seek a better standard of life for themselves; for which, to move where they can - irrespective of national boundaries. 

National Security must, of course, take into account the continued relevance that weapons of mass destruction have, in the lexicon of real-politick, as a currency of power. It is both ironic, and paradoxical that such weapons are not, and must never be war fighting instruments. They subserve a very different purpose, that of deterrence, which is the only sense in which their relevance ties. Yet, here too, lies another great paradox. You can only deter the rational and the responsible. We have yet to find an answer for the factor of irrational response. That is why for Weapons of Mass Destruction there is only one solution-universal, non-discriminatory and total elimination of all weapons of mass destruction from the face of the earth. India remains committed to this goal and we will continue to pursue it untiringly. Like others, India, too, cannot do so unilaterally, in a world of differentiated norms, of uneven and selective criteria of assessment of national security. 

Increasingly, it is my conviction, five other issues will gain security salience in the years ahead. They are: global consequences of environmental profligacies of the past persuading the 'strong and the able' to act in self interest, inviting protest from those that are left out. A quest for higher energy security, at lower cost and on a more assured basis will result in movements towards monopolization. Yet, again, an area of tension. Thirdly, water, assured, potable water and its shortage, resulting in water-stressed zones could cause the stress lines to radiate towards self-preservation, hence conflict. So, too, with food security. The world is neither evenly supplied nor evenly fed. Food adequacy is not universally applied, in consequence plenty and scarcity exist simultaneously. To expect thereafter that they will co-exist in perpetuity is to deny the reality of rising human expectations, of first raising unrealistic hopes about an equitable global economic regime and then not realizing them, whereafter - most tellingly, the consequences of a spread of TV advertising, which informs the large global community of have-nots, vividly, and daily, of what they do not 
actually have. Finally, will this quest for a militarization of outer space introduce elements into national security calculii that are at present absent? 

REGION

I am required, finally, to share thoughts about the 'region' that India inhabits. In the context of India's security our region gets defined, simply, by the very geography of it. India's long land boundaries, unsettled and yet to be defined, challenge the state of the craft of the country. Yet, some other geographical facts borns from the  and define our concerns more accurately. India is but just about 90 odd nautical miles from the northern-most Province of Indonesia-Aceh. Developments, therefore, in the entire South-East Asian region are a matter of direct consequence to us. In another direction, from the northern most point of India, which is now part of Pakistan-occupied Kashmir, Tajikistan a mere 35 kms away; thus the Central Asian lands define that part of our region. The great vortex of medieval malevolism and terrorism that has come into existence in Pakistan and Afghanistan identify yet another aspect of the security of our region. Just 50 years back, in 1947, we shared a land boundary with Iran. This and our relations with the Gulf countries define the western aspect of it. In the South stretches the Indian Ocean, which is not simply a spread of salt water. It imparts a larger Political definition to India's regional security imperatives. Besides, it is an old and established axiom that the strategic boundaries of a nation's security concerns can simply not coincide with its geographical limits alone. Of course, they are not coterminus with simplistic regional classifications either, but More that as our digitalized globe shrinks so, too national security concerns expand.

CONCLUSION

I have taxed your patience and goodwill. In conclusion, permit me to address the aspects of new agenda items of international conduct that have emerged. For the first time in human history, issues like environment and human rights are on the screens of national security formulators.

Environment, ecology, human rights, etc. have certainly for the past two decades, at least, destabilized the nation that foreign policy is mostly political. Yet, these issues along with others, coalescing into the realm of foreign economic policy have acquired a cutting edge, which was not so evident till the Berlin Wall finally collapsed. The doctrine of humanitarian intervention in civil wars, has effectively superseded the older doctrine of untrammeled national sovereignty. To this web of human rights and social standards, environment, etc. other new factors are on the anvil. Most prominently, the imminent creation of an international criminal court.

These then constitute the issues of our tomorrow's even as we endeavour to attend to our collective inheritance of today's perceptions and challenges.
