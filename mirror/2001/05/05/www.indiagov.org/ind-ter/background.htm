- B A C K G R O U N D -

International terrorism has emerged as a global problem affecting countries in all regions. This is a particularly grave problem in situations, where terrorism is sponsored by States across national frontiers against other countries in quest for territorial expansion. The resolution adopted by the Sixth Committee on "Measures to Eliminate International Terrorism" underlines growing international consensus to prevent and combat terrorism. India has contributed to strengthening this consensus. India is party to major international conventions against terrorism. India has also proposed a Comprehensive Convention against International Terrorism. The 53rd UNGA gave legislative mandate to negotiate this convention on a priority basis, after discussions on the draft conventions against nuclear terrorism and financing of terrorist activities have been completed. As a country deeply affected by the menace of terrorism, India stands ready to extend cooperation to UN's efforts to curb this problem.

Global nature of the problem

Threat of terrorism is no longer limited to a particular country or region. It transcends international boundaries. Advance in technology and communications has extended the reach of groups and States sponsoring terrorism. Sarin gas attack in Tokyo some years ago has raised the danger of weapons of mass destruction falling into the hands of terrorists. Terrorists use electronic media to organise their subversive activities. They have access to flow of funds from abroad, including money raised through so called charitable organisations. They enjoy foreign sanctuaries. They are answerable to none. They violate international standards of human rights, and humanitarian law, with impunity.

International Terrorism, as an instrument of State policy

Organised terrorism as an instrument of policy by certain states is very different in scale, nature and consequences than actions of stray individuals and groups. Sustained campaign of terrorist violence is not possible without sanctuaries, training, financing, encouragement and assistance by States. Government patronage gives terrorist groups and individuals enormous resources and safe haven. As the object of terrorism sponsored by States is to destabilise other countries and governments, it poses a threat to international peace and security.

The Declaration on "Measures to Eliminate International Terrorism" enjoins a positive obligation on Member States to ensure that not only the government organs do not sponsor terrorism, but their territory is not used by terrorist groups and individuals. The argument that a given State was not responsible for actions of terrorist groups based on its territory, is not tenable. The Declaration also clearly lays down that acts of terrorism are criminal acts, whatever the underlying considerations. Support to terrorism in any form - political, diplomatic, moral or material must be condemned. Nexus between terrorism, fundamentalism and drug trafficking pose a threat to civil society, democratic pluralism and human rights.

The need for strengthening legal framework

The problem of international terrorism has to be addressed by strengthening international legal regime as well as domestic legislation and implementation. The UNGA resolution "Measures to Eliminate International Terrorism" has urged the Member States to ensure that the jurisdiction of their courts enables them to bring to trial the perpetrators of terrorist acts and to cooperate with and provide assistance to other states and relevant international and regional organisations. The success of this effort requires political will on part of the Member States.

Comprehensive convention against international terrorism

Existing international conventions against terrorism do not adequately address the issue of State responsibility to prevent and refrain from acts of terrorism. This concept is reflected in the Security Council resolutions imposing sanctions on States accused of sponsoring terrorism. A legal convention adopted by the UN General Assembly would provide a more broad-based basis for combating international terrorism and avoid controversies arising from unilateral action. India has therefore, proposed adoption of a comprehensive convention against international terrorism. A draft has already been circulated. During 53rd UNGA session, the Sixth Committee also gave legislative mandate to negotiate the proposed Convention on priority basis after conclusion of discussions on draft conventions currently under consideration.

India's contribution to preventing and combating terrorism

India has played a major part in strengthening international consensus against terrorism in UN, NAM and SAARC fora. India is party to major international conventions against terrorism, and has also incorporated them in domestic legislation:

i) Convention on Offences and certain other Acts committed on Board Aircraft, signed at Tokyo on 14th September, 1963.

This Convention has been given effect to in India by the Tokyo Convention Act, 1975 (20 of 1975).

ii) Convention for the Suppression of Unlawful Seizure of Aircraft, signed at the Hague on 16th September, 1973.

This Convention is implemented through the Anti-Hijacking Act, 1982 (Act No. 65) of 1982).

iii) Convention for the Suppression of Unlawful Acts against the Safety of Civil Aviation, concluded at Montreal on 23rd September, 1971 and the 1988 Protocol for the Suppression of Unlawful Acts of Violence at Airports serving International Civil Aviation Suppression of Unlawful Acts against Safety of Civil Aviation Act, 1982 (66 of 1982).

This Convention is implemented through the Suppression of Unlawful Acts Against Safety of Civil Aviation Act, 1982 (66 of 1982).

iv) Convention on the Prevention and Punishment. of Crimes against Internationally Protected Persons, including Diplomatic Agents, adopted in New York on 14th December, 1973.

This Convention is implemented through the provisions of the Indian Penal Code, 1960.

v) International Convention against the taking of hostages, adopted in New York on 7th December, 1979.

This is given effect in India under section 364 A of the Indian Penal Code, 1960.

vi) UN Convention against Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances, 1988.

This is implemented in India by the Narcotic Drug and Psychotropic Substances (amendment) Act, 1989 which amended the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985.

vii) International Conventions against the taking of hostages, adopted in New York on 7th December, 1979.

This is already covered, and implemented under Article 364 A of Indian Penal Code.

The Government has decided to sign and ratify the International Convention for the Suppression of Terrorist Bombings.

Regional Conventions

India is party to the following regional conventions:

i) SAARC Convention on the Suppression of Terrorism, 1987

This is implemented in India by SAARC Convention (Suppression of Terrorism) Act, 1993 (36 of 1993).

ii) SAARC Convention on Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances, 1990.

Since the provisions are basically based on the UN 1988 Convention, the provisions of the SAARC Convention on the Suppression of Terrorism are implementable under the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 as amended in 1989.

N   A   M

India has also played a role in evolving NAM consensus against terrorism. NAM has unequivocally affirmed that criminal acts intended or calculated to provoke a state of terror in the general public, a group of persons or particular persons, for whatever purposes, are unjustifiable. XIIth NAM Summit at Durban in 1998 has also re-affirmed that all Member States have an obligation to refrain from organising, assisting or participating in terrorist acts in the territories of other States. The Summit unequivocally condemned any political, diplomatic, moral or material support to terrorism. The NAM Summit also called for early adoption and implementation of a comprehensive convention against international terrorism

Impact of International Terrorism in India

Since 1988, India has suffered total of 45,182 terrorist incidents in J&K alone. Though in many incidents security forces were targeted but majority involved attacks on civilians.

Pakistan supported terrorists are also responsible for 2,466 kidnappings and 791 cases of extortion since 1989 .

Pakistan supported terrorism also resulted in extensive destruction of property in J&K. There were targeted attacks on educational institutions (780) in a bid to destroy the modem education system, which the fundamentalist ideology of the terrorists did not approve.

Since 1988, security forces have recovered very large number of weapons from terrorists in J&K. This included 889 rocket launchers, 347 rockets, 1525 rocket boosters, 1124 machine guns, 20,193 AK series rifles, 34,141 grenades, 21,332 kilograms of explosives and 1970 bombs. The range and sophistication of weapons show the intensity of terrorist campaign, which cannot be sustained without the support of Pakistan (or neighbouring country) in violation of all bilateral and international commitments.

Pakistan has also supported terrorism in other parts of India. On March 12, 1993, serial blasts in Mumbai resulted in killings of around 260 persons and injury to 700 others. The loss of property was estimated at Rs. 270 million. According to some of the culprits arrested, 22 persons involved in the blasts had received training in handling arms/explosives in Pakistan in February 1993. The weapons and explosives used in the blast were brought to India by boats which picked up their consignments from a Pakistan Marine Agency vessel near the Karachi coast.

Abduction of 4 foreigners by Harkat-ul-Ansar (HUA), September-October, 1994

The abduction of 4 foreign nationals (3 British and 1 American) from Delhi by the Harkat-ul-Ansar was carried out by a core group of 5 Pakistani nationals. The kidnappers demanded release of a number of terrorists in the custody of Indian Government.

Abductions in Pahalgam

During the period 4-10 July, 1995 member of the terrorist group 'AL FARAN' kidnapped 4 British citizens, 3 US citizens, One German and one Norwegian to use as hostages in their demand for the release of 3 important HUA (Harkatul Ansar) of Pakistani. nationality and some other militants.

Three ladies among the hostages were later released, one American managed to escape, the Norwegian was killed by decapitation on 13.8.99 and the fate of the remaining four is still not known.

Hijacking

There were six incidents of hijacking of Indian Airlines aircraft since the eruption of terrorist activities in Punjab. Attempts were made in all these cases to take the aircrafts to Pakistan. The hijackers succeeded in hijacking the aircrafts to Pakistan in two cases. In September 1981, a Delhi-Amritsar-Srinagar flight was hijacked to Lahore by Dal Khalsa commandos who demanded the release of Bhindranwale. In August 1982, two flights - one from Delhi to Srinagar and the other from Jodhpur were diverted to Amritsar. Two planes hijacked in July and August 1984 ended up on Lahore and Dubai respectively.

In the August -1984 case, the hijackers were handed over a pistol and some ammunition at Lahore by the Pakistan authorities. The hijacker was subsequently repatriated by UAE authorities to India, who handed over the pistol recovered from the hijacker. Investigations revealed that the pistol was manufactured in Germany and was part of 75 pistols consigned from Germany to CAO, PO Box 1040, Islamabad.

Assassination of Punjab Chief Minister

A human bomb attack was made on Beant Singh, Chief Minister of Punjab on August 31, 1995. The attack resulted in assassination of Punjab Chief Minister, killing of 17 others and injury to many more. The attack was carried out by a hardcore terrorist group based in Pakistan, where they were given sanctuary and assistance by the ISI.

Terrorist's trained in Pakistan carried out a series of explosions in India during 1997. This included 26 explosions in Delhi killing 11 persons and injuring 241, 5 explosions in Haryana during 1996/97 killing 1 and injuring 39 and 3 explosions in UP during October 1997 killing 2 and injuring 19 persons. The culprits arrested revealed that they received training in Pakistan Occupied Kashmir.

According to well documented reports from international sources, Pakistan continues to provide sanctuary to terrorist groups like former Harkat-ul-Ansar and current Harkat-ul-Mujahidin and Lashkar-i-Taiba, which have threatened not only targets in India, but also in many other countries. These organisations enjoy safe haven in Pakistan, openly raise funds and recruit new cadres. Pakistan has also used Afghan territory and nationals to foment terrorist activities in India as well as other parts of the world. The UNGA Declaration "Measures to Eliminate International Terrorism" states that acts of terrorism are criminal acts and unjustifiable whatever the considerations of a political, philosophical, ideological, racial, ethnic, religious or any other nature that may be invoked to justify them.

Pakistan's actions are not only violative of the basic norms relating to conduct of inter-state relations, but also of the bilateral treaty obligations flowing out of the Simla Agreement and Lahore Declaration. Under the Lahore Declaration, the two countries have reaffirmed their condemnation of terrorism and their determination to combat this menace.
